Urine dipstick (Urinalysis)

Specific gravity

  • Proportional to urine osmolality;
  • appropriate changes to volume status indicate adequate renal concentrating ability
  • hydration status

pH

  • 7.0 may indicate urease-positive infection (E.g. Proteus)

    • can occur in strict vegetarians and infections
    • may be inappropriately high in RTA I (distal RTA)
  • low pH can occur in high protein diets
    • may be inappropriately low in RTA IV (distal RTA)

Blood

  • detection of peroxidase activity of blood and free heme
  • sensitivity
    • can detect 3+ erythrocytes
  • False positives
    • Myoglobinuria (e.g. muscle breakdown from exercise, rhabdomyolysis)
    • iodine solutions cross-reacting with dipstick indicator
    • hemoglobinurea (due to IV hemolysis, e.g. post-transfusion, mechanical heart valve, perivalvular leak)
    • peroxidase expressing bacteria
    • drugs - chloroquine, ascorbic acid
  • Red coloured urine (not blood):
    • drugs - rifampin, phenytoin
    • food - beets

Protein

  • Proteinuria indicative of glomerular dysfunction
  • false positives
    • highly concentrated urine
    • highly alkaline urine
  • false negatives
    • highly dilute urine

Glucose

  • excess glucose indicative of diabetes
  • False positives
    • drug exposure
      • Tenofovir
      • Disoproxil
      • fumarate
      • SGLT2 inhibitors (empagliflozin, canagliflozin)

Ketones

  • Occurs in
    • Starvation
    • DKA
    • Alcoholic ketoacidosis
    • Drug toxicity - salicylate, isopropyl alcohol
  • Detects acetone and acetoacetate but not beta-hydroxybutyrate; will underestimate ketosis in DKA and alcoholic ketoacidosis
  • False positives
    • Drugs - sulfhydryl drugs (e.g. captopril)

Leukocyte Esterase and Nitrites

  • Nitrites - suggests presence of bacteria/UTI
  • Leukocyte esterase - suggests presence of WBC and UTI

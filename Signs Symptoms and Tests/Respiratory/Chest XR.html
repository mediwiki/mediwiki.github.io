Chest X-Ray

Systematic intepretation

Basic details

  1. Ensure correct name and date of study
  2. Read the film markers (L vs R)
  3. Evaluate the film technique (supine? AP vs PA?)
  4. Film adequacy
    1. Penetration
      • Lower thoracic vertebrae should be visible THROUGH the heart - doesn't need great detail, but should be able to see outline
    2. Inspiration
      • should be taken in full inspiration - should see 10th or 11th rib intersecting with right cardiophrenic sulcus
    3. Rotation
      • Bony landmarks should be used - soft tissue (E.g. trachea) can move
      • spinous process should be between the heads of the two clavicles
      • if rotated, appearance of lung field can be altered, mediastinum can appear widened/displaced
    4. Completeness
      • everything in chest needs to be seen - e.g. costophrenic angle
      • A true chest XR should include a lateral as well

ABC approach

  1. Airway
  2. Bones
  3. Cardiomediastinum
  4. Diaphragms
  5. Everything Else
    • Line and tubes
    • foreign bodies
    • air under diaphragm, in mediastinal space
  6. Lung Fields
    • Silhouette signs
      • Loss of right heart border → right middle lobe pneumonia
      • Loss of right hemidiaphragm → right lower lobe pneumonia
      • Loss of left upper heart border → Lingula pnuemonia See Chest XR cases 1

Geographic approach

  • risk of not looking at the rest of the film

Targeted approach

  • used for rule in/out in scan - very high risk of missing film

Diseases on chest XR

Pneumonia

  • Consolidation
  • "Silhouette signs"
    • Loss of heart border
    • Loss of diaphragm
  • "Spine sign" on lateral CXR
  • Air bronchograms
  • Increased interstitial markings
    • "reticular" pattern
    • often bilaterally
    • suggests viral/atypical organism
    • may also be seen with pulmonary oedema

Pulmonary oedema

  • Cardiomegaly
  • increased interstitial markings
  • Effusions
  • Kerley B lines
  • Cephalisation of vessels - only valid on uprihgt filme
  • Thickened interlobular fissures
  • Perihilar crowding

Pneumothorax

  • take inspiratory and expiratory views
    • expiratory view contenses thorax content to accentuate pneumothorax
  • image inversion makes the pleural reflection more obvious
  • lateral decubitus (88% sensitivity), upright CXR (59% sensitivity), supine AP (37% sensitivity)
  • deep sulcus sign
    • on a supine CXR, air pushes the diaphragm down
  • haziness on CXR lung field on context of trauma - consider haemopneumothorax

Rib fractures

  • image inversion

Pneumomediastinum

  • continuous diaphragm sign - can see separation between heart border and diaphragm
    • caused by pneumomediastinum and pnemopericardium (rare)
  • caused typically by forced expiration against closed glottis
    • THC
    • Cocaine
    • Athletes
    • Asthmatics
    • Severe coughing
  • Clinical vomiting - consider Boerhaave's Syndrome (effort related oesophageal rupture) → can result in mediastinitis, universally fatal

Bronchiectasis

  • Tram-track opacities
  • air-fluid levels in cystic bronchiectasis
  • increased bronchivascular markings
  • ring shadows - end on view of bronchi
  • ill-defined pulmonary vascularture - represents peribronchovascular fibrosis

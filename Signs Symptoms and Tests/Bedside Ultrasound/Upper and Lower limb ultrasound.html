Method

  • linear array probe - 5-10MHx - allows achieving of < 60 degree angle and vascular compression
    • larger patients may need curved array lower frequency

Lower limb technique

  • lay patient supine
    • limb being examined in slight external rotation with knee slightly flexed
  • Arteries - B-mode, pulse-wave, colour doppler (for peak systolic velocity and waveforms)
  • Veins - B-mode (compression), pulsed-wave, colour doppler (response to respiration and augmentation)
  • Lower limb vasculature ultrasound best using linear 6-13MHx probe in B-mode and doppler

Normal LL anatomy

  • Veins
    • anechoic lumen
    • echogenic walls
    • leg veins are duplicated in 25% of population; below knee veins are usually paired
      • calf and forearm veins are called venae comitantes - follow a single artery
  • Artery
    • Pulsed-wave doppler
      • normal triphasic waveform
        • systolic forward flow
        • short reverse component
        • diastolic forward fall
      • loss of elasticity in elderly patients results in normal biphasic waveform
      • peak systolic velocities decreases from proximal to distal (i.e. from groin to ankle)

- triphasic waveform of arterial doppler

LL DVT assessment

  1. position probe at inguinal crease midway between ASIS and pubic symphysis
    • CFV lies medial to CFA
    • CFV bifurcates into SFV and DFV 2-4cm below saphenofemoral junction
    • femoral vein moves to lie deep to artery in mid and distal thigh
  2. Continue moving distally - til you hit popliteal vein
    • probe at posterior knee
    • vein lies more posterior than artery - vein is close to probe
  3. Find great/long saphenous vein
    • drains into CVF at saphenofemoral junction
    • duplicated in thigh in 35% and in calf in 55%
  4. Perforating veins connect deep and superficial veins
    • three groups: Medial, lateral, posterior
  5. Use dynamic B-mode - compress the vein with the probe
    • should ALWAYS require less force to compress the vein than it takes to compress the artery
  6. Use colour doppler to assist in imaging
    • shows venous filling and patency
    • from mid-thigh and distal, flow can be augmented with compression

  7. Use pulsed-wave doppler

    • can be used to confirm patency and measure reflux flow
    • can show flow velocity and direction variation with respiration
      • if NO variation occurs during respirationry, could indicate iliac vein thrombus or other thrombus preventing venous return
    • distal augmentation can demonstrate normal increase in venous volume
      • e.g. squeeze with nonscanning hand

  8. Primary sign of DVT

    • echogenic clot in vein lumen - either occlusive OR non-occlusive
  9. Secondary sign of DVT
    • noncompressible veins on B-mode w/ direct probe pressure over vein
    • lack of increased flow on doppler with distal leg augmentation

Documenting DVTs - describe location and extent - distal to proximal - degree of occlusion - occupy lumen vs. partial occlusion of venous flow

Bilateral LL DVT - uncommon - associated with procoagulant states - malignancy - pelvic mass - compression of iliac veins

DVT DDx - cellulitis with oedema in subcutanoeus tissue - - disconnected anechoic channels in subcut tissue on ultrasound - Superficial thrombophlebitis - can cause leg swelling - appears as painful purple/blue lumps under skin - use US to confirm superficial thrombus in superficial system and measure proximal extent relative to Saphenopopliteal junction or saphenofemoral junction

- superficial thrombus in lesser saphenous vein close to saphenofemoral junction

Bakers cyst rupture

  • fluid distends medial gastrocnemius-semimembranosus bursa → pain in oedema in adjacent soft tissues (mimics DVT)
  • always check for bakers cyst when scanning to exclude the DVT

  • ruptured bakers cyst
    • can be complicated by haemorrhage into cyst
    • place probe in transverse as posterior knee to rule out

Upper limb DVTs

  • Scan with upper limb in external rotation and slightly abducted

Upper limb anatomy

- basilic vein - communicates with cephalic vein via median cubital vein - medial side of biceps deep to brachial fascia - joins the brachial venae comitantes to form the axillary vein - cephalic vein - lies in superficial fascia anterolateral to biceps brachii - terminates to axillary vein via the deltopectoral groove - Median cubital vein - in superficial fascia of cubital fossa - connects cephalic and basilic veins

UL DVT

  • same sonographic appearance as lower limb
    • echogenic clot in vein lumen
      • occlusive or nonocclusive
    • noncompressible vein
    • no flow alteration with distal augmentation
    • loss of phasicity

Ultrasound physics

- high frequency sound wave - diagnostic ultrasound from 2MHx - 200MHx - waves are either electromagnetic or mechanical waves - mechanical waves require mediums to travel - transverse vs longitudinal describe movement of vibration vs. direction of travel - transverse = perpendicular (e.g. ocean) - longitudinal = in same direction (e.g. sound) - electromagnetic waves (e.g. light) do not - light can travel through space

  • Wave components
    • Wavelength = distance between two correspodning points of wave
      • longer wavelength = lower frequency
    • amplitude - maximal displacement from equilibrium
    • frequency = number of times a wave cycles per unit time (f = 1/t)
      • period(t) = time taken to complete one cycle
    • velocity = distance/time = wavelength/period = wavelength x frequency
      • velocity depends on medium through which the wave propagates; proportional to medium density and elasticity
      • uniform wave has constant velocity
    • acoustic impedance = measure of mediums resistance to sound waves passing through it
      • high acoustic impedance (bone, soft tissue) are more white
      • low acoustic impedance (air) appear black

Principles of image formation

Peizoelectric effect

  • certain materials reversibly give off electric chage in response to mechanical stress
  • can be used to generate an ultrasound wave
    • electric signal → peizoelectric crystal → vibration of crystal → ultrasound waves
    • ultrasound waves can interact with tissue
      • absorption - conversion of wave into other forms of energy (e.g. heat) - decreases wave propagation into deeper structures
      • reflection - change in direction of wave due to interaction of interface of two different media with different impedance - the wave will return to its original medium
      • refraction - change in direction of wave as it passes through two mediums of different impedance; waves can continue to propagate through the second medium
      • scattering - interaction of waveform with object smaller than its wavelength - creates a uniform amplitude wave in multiple directions with little or no reflected waves towards origin of wave
      • attentuation - reduction in intensity of sound wave as it travels through medium. summative effect from absorption, reflection and scattering
      • diffraction - change in waveform direction as it travels through an opening or barrier
  • ultrasound waves travel to target tissue and some will reflect back - the piezoelectric crystal in the probe will receive this wave and produce an electrical signal
    • time to receive the signal back will produce the image
  • Common imaging mode
    • B-mode - grayscale (B = brightness)
      • each echo produces a shade of grey/brightness
      • produces 2D image for morphological information for anatomy
    • M-mode - motion for cardiac and lung (M = motion)
      • one light of sight to demonstrate movement along that line of sight
    • Doppler - pulsed wave and colour - for blood flow in arteries and vein
      • frequency of sound changes with motion relative to listener
      • used to assess direction of blood
      • 2 types
        1. Spectral doppler - demonstrates velocity and direction vs. time (horizontal axis)
        2. 2D colour flow - adjustable colour box superimposed over B-mode image - each colour pixel is average velocity
          • red = flow towards transducer
          • blue = flow away from transducer
          • depth of colour = velocity

Advantages and limitations of diagnostic ultrasound

  • Advantages
    • No ionising radiation
    • Highly mobile and bedside performable
    • Fast and cost effective
    • Ideal soft tissue modality
    • Operates in real time/dynamic imaging allowed
    • Hemodynamic studies can be done with doppler function
    • Modality of choice for interventional procedures
  • Limitations
    • certain body areas are unsuitable - e.g. bone (reflection) and bowel
    • operator dependent - sensitivity increases with operator experience
    • reproducibility more difficult than CT and MRI - due to scan plane variability
  • Biological effects
    • Thermal effect - tissue may increase temperatures by 0.1C
  • Mechanical effects
    • will not occur at diagnostic ultrasound frequencies. Microbubble formation and macromolecule scission unlikely to occur in PoCUS

Technique for ultrasound examination

  1. Patient Details
  2. Probe/Transducer - choose most appropriate probe for body part
    1. phased array - cardiac, abdominal, obstetric
    2. linear array
      • high frequency - superficial body parts, thyroid, scrotum, breast, superficial artery/veins, MSK
      • low frequency - superficial and small parts/deeper and deeper arteries/veins, MSK in large patients
    3. curved array - obstetric, abdominal, gynaecology
    4. Note - high frequency has better resolution but lower penetration
  3. Preset - probe and image settings to be optimised for body parts scanned
  4. Position - patient to be positioned to demonstrate area of interest, and for examiner to position well
    • anatomical landmarks to be used
  5. Place gel - needed for coupling medium
  6. Produce and capture - obtain best images possible

Probe holding

- crab claw grip, hold probe closer to patients skin. use 5th digit on patient skin - transducer pressure light for superficial structures - ensure the transducer is oriented properly. When scanning in sagittal pain, keep marker towards patients head. When in transverse plane, keep marker towards patient's right - 2 hand rotation technique - keep structure in centre of image - one hand rotates probe (nonscanning hand) - one hand stabilises probe (scanning hand) - Scanning movements - slow, controlled, and move in only one plane at a time

Optimisation of B-mode imaging

  • Don't Forget Of This
    1. Depth - shallowest depth to not cut off image of interest
      • start deep then go shallow; leave small area beneath to observe artefacts
    2. Focus - adjust focus to depth of interest for best resolution
      • focal zone is an arrow/bar on side of image
      • using more than 1 focus at once will reduce frame rates
    3. Overall gain
      • amplification of received signal (gain) will increase brightness of entire image
      • too much gain can obscure subtle changes in echogenicity - can miss out on pathology
    4. Time gain compensation (TGC)
      • corrects for loss of energy of sound beam
      • amplifies weaker deeper echos returning from deeper structures
      • most are linear amplification however can be adjusted by operator to allow even amplification

Terminology

  • Resolution
    • ability to differentiate between small structures in B-mode imaging
      • axial resolution - discern 2 parallel objects to the ultrasound beam - better at high frequency
      • lateral resolution - discern 2 objects perpendicular to ultrasound beam - maximum at focal zone
  • echogenicity
    • anechoic - no internal echoes (e.g. fluid filled cyst)
    • hypoechoic - low echoes compared to surrounding tissue - thyroid nodules, breast fibroadenomas
    • isoechoic - internal echois equal to surrounding/reference tissue
    • hyperechoic - increased echogenicity - e.g. tendon, fascia, vessel walls
  • Artefacts
    • Shadowing - strong attentuation and moderate reflection of sound wave (e.g. soft tissue-calcium interface)
      • bone and renal calculi are strong attenuators
    • enhancement - no or reduced attentuation of sound wave - e.g. fluid
      • system auto provides TGC which makes brighter behind structure
      • can differnetiate solid from cystic lesions - cystic will have enhancement
    • reverberation - sound reflected by 2 highly refelctive surfaces (e.g. anterior gallbladder wall or surface of breast implant)
      • same structure represented twice - at correct level, then deeper in image
      • e.g. lung pleura reflection - repetition of the pleural line seen

Chest Ultrasound

Background

  • Common clinical questions
    • Pneumothorax?
    • Pleural effusion
    • ETT correctly placed?
  • Ultrasound limitations
    • acoustic impedance mismatch - 90% of ultrasound reflected back to probe, so normal lung tissue cannot be visualised (air almost completely reflects ultrasound)
    • echo deep to pleura is reverberation artefact and NOT lung tissue
    • curvilinear probes are not ideal for prone chest imaging

Technique (6Ps)

  • Probe - high frequency linear array (lung only 3-4cm from surface) - 5-10MHz
  • Present - chest/abdomen
  • Position
    • Supine
      • MID-CLAVICULAR
        • locate mid-clavicular line, palpate to find 2nd and 4th rib
        • hold probe perpendicular to ribs, hold transducer roughly sagittal
      • MID-AXILLARY
        • mid-axillary line between 5th and 9th ribs
        • keep probe perpendicular to ribs - probe marker superiorly pointing
    • Erect
      • posterior mid-scapular line, transducer between 7th and 9th ribs

Normal - B-mode

  • Normal imaging window - mid-clavicular line
  • 'Bat sign' - Rib, lung pleura, rib
    • looks like a bat flying towards you with wings up
    • bone of ribs attenuate and reflect ultrasound → resultant acoustic shadow deep to rib

- 'comet tail artefact' - sound reverberates between two reflectors - e.g. air bubble, - crystalline stone, surgical clip, parietal/visceral pleura - soft tissue/air interface - most sound (90%) reflected back to transducer - internal reflection between visceral/parietal pleura resulting in comet tail artefact - echos not truely lung tissue but echos due to reverberation - pleural line rewritten deep to true level - expected appearance in normal lung tissue - absent in - pneumothorax - air in pleural space - chest mass - fluid filled lungs (pulmonary oedema) - Lung sliding - pleural line 'shimmers' during normal respiration because of movement of visceral pleura against parietal pleura

Normal - M-Mode

- Seashore sign - M-mode single line displayed as moving graphic - cursor placed between the two ribs - anterior stationary linear region = stationary chest wall and parietal pleura - deep sand-like pattern - moving visceral pleura and comet-tail artefacts

Pathologies on chest ultrasound - Pneumothorax

  • Pneumothorax - gas between parietal and visceral pleura
    • gas rises to non-dependent portion of chest → patient scanned supine
      • small will only be visible anteriorly
      • large will be visible to midaxillary line
    • anterior chest wall to be scanned first
    • if present → probe moved to midaxillary line to determine extent of pneumothorax
  • B-mode - negative findings
    • sliding lung sign absent → becomes a single motionless white line (parietal pleura only - visceral pleura not sliding against it)
    • comet tail artefact absent → air in pleural space causes total reflection before it can reach visceral pleura and subpleural interstitium
  • B-mode - positive findings
    • 'lung point' - 100% positive sign for pneumothorax → absent lung sliding begins or ends and comes in/out of view in respiration
  • M-mode - positive findings
    • Stratosphere sign replaces the seashore sign - air in pleural space produces series of horizontal lines similar to wave-like pattern of anterior chest wall
    • 'lung point' in M-mode seen where normal seashore sign becomes stratosphere sign

- stratosphere sign

Pathologies on chest ultrasound - Pleural effusion

  • Advantages of ultrasound - more sensitive than Chest XR
    • US > 20mL vs. 200mL for CXR
  • Patient positioned erect (seated), transducer against midscapular line in inferior chest OR mid-axillary supine
  • Fluid is black/anechoic (no tissue interfaces) - collects between visceral and parietal pleura
    • US 100% sensitive for effusions > 100mL

Thoracocentesis

  • Use to find best intercostal space for needle placement
    • aim for superior margin of inferior rib to avoid Neurovascular bundle
  • position patient erect/seated and place array in parasagittal plane
    • use pen to mark deepest pocket of fluid immediately superior to rib
    • use aseptic technique
    • 1% lignocaine to level of parietal pleura
    • safety needle with 3 way tap
    • demonstrate needle tip relative to visceral pleura and angle/retract needle once within pleural space

ETT Placement verification

  • Upper airway ultrasound to ensure ETT placement correct
    • end of ETT should be level with medial ends of clavicles ~4cm superior to carina
    • HF linear array probe to scan trachea in transverse - ensure ETT proximal to vocal cords
  • Benefits - 98% accurate in confirming ETT placement, good agreement with waveform capnography

- hyperechoic line indicates ETT position (anterior surface) within trachea

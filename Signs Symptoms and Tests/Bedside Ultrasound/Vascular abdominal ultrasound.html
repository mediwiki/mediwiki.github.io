Vascular abdomen - abdominal aorta and IVC

Clinical use

  • Abdominal aortic aneurysm
  • Intraperotineal or extraperitoneal rupture of AAA

Method

  • use Curvilinear 2-5MHx probe
  • transducer in midline â†’ downward pressure to displace bowel gas and physically reach aorta
  • B-mode to assess size of aorta in sagittal and transverse section
  • Doppler to ensure complete filling of vessel and rule out dissection

Anatomy

  • Branches which can be identified on US
    • Coeliac
    • Superior Mesenteric Artery - seen in long axis
    • L and R renal arteries - ~3cm below origin of SMA; short axis
    • Bifurcation into the common iliac arteries - oblique view - rotate transducer along line from umbilicius to the left OR right hips

Aorta anatomy

  • 3 layers
    1. Tunica intima - bright echogenic line - innermost portion
    2. tunica media - anechoic middle layer
    3. adventitia - moderately bright line, outermost layer
  • central aorta is anechoeic blood
    • longitudinal view - bright echogenic walls
    • transverse view - lateral walls less echogenic due to angle of insonation ~ 0 degrees (i.e. sound not reflected back to probe at lateral edges) - beware of overmeasuring transverse diameter
  • Expected values
    • proximal diameter <= 2.5cm (between confluence of splenic and portal veins, and L) renal vein)
    • distal diameter <= 2cm (just above aortic bifurcation)
    • pulsed wave doppler of aorta should show triphasic, pulsatile flow
  • Pathologies visible on sonography
    • Ectasia (Widening)
    • Abdominal aortic aneurysm (AAA)
    • Dissection

Aortic pathology - Ectasia

  • distal ectasia common with increasing age
    • abdominal aorta diameter 2.5-3cm is 'ectatic'

Aortic pathology - AAA

  • Definition - dilated segment > 1.5x diameter of adjacent normal segment OR distal aorta > 3cm diameter
  • sonographic assessment should include
    • sagittal and transverse diameter measurements
    • location of aneurysm
    • renal artery involvement
    • shape of aneurysmal dilation
    • complications
  • Method
    • measure AP and transverse diameters - outer layer to outer layer
      • include wall thickness
    • measure longitudinal length of section
    • measure distance of aneurysm from renal arteries (or SMA origin - assume renal arteries are ~2-3cm below SMA origin)
    • Shapes
      • fusiform - balloons equally in all size
      • saccular - balloons on one side only
  • Complications
    • rupture - increases as short axis size increases
      • 4-5.6cm diameter = 10% risk of rupture

      • 6cm diameter = 25% risk

      • elective AAA repair recommended when diameter reaches 5-5.5cm; post-op survival rate >95% for elective AAA repair, and survival rate <10% for AAA rupture outside of hospital
      • rupture can occur intraperitoneal - 20%, will suffer hemoperitoneum
        • easier to diagnose sonographically than retroperitoneal ruptures
      • retroperitoneal rupture in 80% of cases - pain, hypotension, pulsatile abdominal mass
    • thrombosis
      • mural thrombus is common - can lead to luminal narrowing, stenosis, distal thromboembolism
      • downstream thromboembolism can lead to lower limb arterial blockage, ischemia or infarction
    • dissection - CT more accurate than US in defining dissection
    • distal embolism
    • invasion - fistula
      • communicate and bleed into bowel, IVC, kidneys, ureters or renal arteries

Inferior vena cava

  • US IVC assessment can determine hydration status or IVC thrombus
  • Method
    • Subxiphoid location
    • scan 2cm below junction of IVC and RA
    • Assess using B mode and M-mode
    • note lies deep to segment 4 of the liver, to the RIGHT of the aorta
      • 0.01% have situs inversus
      • 1.5% of population have duplication of IVC

IVC anatomy

- Tributaries - Hepatic veins drain into IVC in manner resembling 'bunny ears' or 'reindeer' - L and R renal veins - R gonadal vein - R and L common iliac veins - Transverse US - L) renal vein draining into IV at level of pancreas - AP diameter = 2cm - TV diameter < 3.7cm - diameters should increase with valsalva and inspiration - diameters should decrease with expiration - Lumen is anechoic and walls are echogenic - Flow velocity varies with respiration and cardiac pulsation - reduces with inspiration

IVC Pathology - high CVP

  • CVP/RA pressure describes pressure of blood in thoracic vena cava in RA
  • IVC diameter > 2.5cm without diameter reduction on inspiration indicates high CVP (>20mmHg)
  • IVC diameter < 1.5cm indicates low CVP (<5mmHg)
    • low CVP increasingly likely with decreasing IVC diameter
    • IVC collapses completely with deep sharp inspiration with low CVP

IVC Pathology - IVC thrombus

  • usually extension of DVT from iliac or LL vessel
  • low level echoes within IVC lumen which doesnt change diameter
  • colour doppler can demonstrate echogenic filling defect
    • can determine if thrombus is occlusive or nonocclusive
  • Complications
    • Life-threatening emboli and PE

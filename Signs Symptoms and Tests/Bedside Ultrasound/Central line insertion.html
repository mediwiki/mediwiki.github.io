Central line insertion

Technique

  • Use 5-12MHx linear probe for vasculature
  • Peripheral vascular pre-set to optimise B-mode and doppler
  • Identify normal anatomy in region of interest

Internal jugular vein

- Lateral to the common carotid artery - hold probe in transverse to vein; colour doppler to confirm flow direction - probe pressure used to collapse the vein - Local jugular neck anatomy can be altered by - clavicle fracture - previous endarterectomy - neck irradiation - R) IJV more commonly accessed as it is more comfortable for RH operators and has direct course to SVC

Basilic vein

- medial to brachial vessels - holds the straightest course through the axilla compared with the cephalic vein

Common femoral vein

- Medial to artery at groin crease - NAVY (lateral to medial) - Nerve (femoral) - Artery (common femoral) - Vein (common femoral) - Y fronts

CVC insertion technique

  1. Use dominant hand and light probe pressure to identify vasculature
    1. carefully swap hands so dominant hand can be used for needle insertion
  2. Use marker to line direction of approach at site of probe closest to yourself
  3. Prepare sterile field at site of insertion, and use sterile probe, prepare sterile tray, clean area with chlorhexidine. drape margins. use sterile gel
  4. inject local anaesthetic
    1. 5-10mL of 1% lignocaine with 25g needle under US guidance to margin of target
    2. make sure the probe is set up correctly - marker towards patients right (transverse) or towards head (longitudinal)
    3. keep needle parallel to probe to ensure maximal echo reflection
  5. recall planned needle approach
    1. confirm structures - arteries are not compressible, and have faster pulsatile flow on doppler
    2. make sure you do not apply too much probe pressure - it can compress vein
  6. place central line
    1. trendelenberg can be used to increase diameter of IJV and decrease risk of air emboli
    2. PICC lines preferrably in basilic vein above elbow crease

  7. apply post-procedural care

    1. CXR performed post IJV insertion to confirm catheter position, assess for aspiration, and exclude pneumothorax

  8. Complications

    • Hematoma of pucture site
    • inadvertent puncture of adjacent vessels or nerves
    • infection at puncture site

  9. Transverse approach

    • more difficult to assess needle depth - only visualising tip and not needle shaft
    • use to define target vein for venous access prior to using longitudinal approach
    • used in most venous line insertions
  10. longitudinal approach
    • allows direct needle visualisation throughout length
    • ideally do not move the probe but only the needle into the planned trajectory
    • allows needle insertion plane to be the same as scanned plane
    • used in most US guided FNA and tissue biopsies
    • doesnt allow visualisation of adjacent artery and nerve

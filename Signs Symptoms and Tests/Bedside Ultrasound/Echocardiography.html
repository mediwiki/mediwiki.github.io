Basic Echocardiography

  • Use
    • evalulate cardiac structure and function
    • assess myocardium, cardiac chambers, valves, pericardium, great vessels
    • diagnose
      • pericardial effusion
      • RV and LV size and contractility
  • non-invasive and bedside exam
  • Approaches for heart assessment
    • Transthoracic (TTE)
    • Transoesophageal (TOE)
  • Approach - 6Ps
    1. Patient details
    2. Probe - 1.5MHz phased array; low frequency allows good penetration to deeper structures
    3. Preset - echocardiography; optimises B-mode and doppler parameters - high velocity flow and fast frame rates
    4. Position
      • supine for subcostal
      • right anterior oblique and right lateral decubitus for parasagital scan and apical 4-chamber view
    5. Place Gel
    6. Produce and Capture
  • Three scanning windows
    1. Subcostal 4 chamber
    2. Parasternal
      1. Short axis
      2. Long axis
    3. Apical 4-chambers

Normal anatomy

  • Majority heart in mediastinum
    • 2/3 lying left of midline, 1/3 lying right
  • heart extends obliquely from 2nd to 5th intercostal space
  • diameter 12-14cm
  • Orthogonal planes
    • Long-axis (from aortic outlet to the apex)
    • Short-axis (perpendicular to long-axis)
    • Four-chamber axis (transversing both ventricles and atria through mitral and tricuspid valve)

  • Subcostal scanning window - position
    • hold probe so hand is over probe
    • keep patient supine
    • over-hand position to place probe beneath xiphoid process
    • ask patient to take a deep breath in - liver to act as acoustic window - sound won't pass through stomach gas
      • apply downward pressure and slowly scoop up into the chest
      • marker to patient's left (opposite to usual convention)
        • R) heart will be on left for 4-chamber view
        • apex on left for long-axis view
  • Parasternal long axis
    • line from left nipple to right shoulder (~ 10-oclock view)
    • start at left 4th intercostal space
    • slide probe towards sternum (midline) until heart visible
    • Patient held-expiration is best view
      • if not visualised - move inferiorly to next intercostal space
  • Parasternal short axis
    • rotate probe from PLAX view 90 degrees - probe marker should be at 1-oclock
      • aligned with line from left shoulder to right nipple
    • fanning movement to angle probe superiorly
      • aortic valve
      • mitral valve
      • papillary muscles near apex
  • Apical 4-chamber view
    • positioned supine or left lateral decubitus (or anywhere between)
      • brings heart to chest wall and gets rid of lung between probe and apex
    • place probe at apical pulse
    • fan/rotate probe to achieve best 4 chamber view

Views

Subcostal 4-chamber

  • Anterior and posterior pericardium
  • apex and 4 chambers
  • tricuspid on right, mitral on left
  • R) heart is closest to heart - R) side appears closest to screen at point of image

Parasternal Long Axis

  • Mitral, aortic valve visualised, apex aligned
  • Longitudinal view of LVOT and RVOT
  • anterior and posterior pericardium
  • intraventricular septum must be as horizontal as possible
    • may need to move to next rib space to achieve this view
  • aortic and mitral valve visible
  • tricuspid valve and LV apex should not be visible
    • LV apex if seen → move to higher intercostal space

Parasternal Short Axis

  • Aortic valve
    • 3 cusps
  • Mitral valve
    • tendinous cords attaching to papillary muscle
  • Papillary muscle of left ventricle near apex - LV muscle changes can affect papillary muscle shape and affect MV function

Apical 4-chamber view

  • Right and left ventricles
  • Right and left atria
  • Mitral and tricuspid valves
  • Apical myocardium
  • Interventricular septum

Assessing cardiac contractility

  • PLAX is best view for contractility
    • RVOT is closest to probe - this view is used to assess intraventricular septum and Left posterior wall thickness and contractility
    • use to compare LA and aortic root diameters - should be the same
  • Normal findings:
    1. Heart walls should contract to centre of each chamber
    2. Mitral valve should open widely
    3. Cardiac muscle should thicken on contraction (e.g. septum/walls) - pay attention to intraventricular septum and anterior/posterior myocardium
  • LV function
    • e-point to septum
      • in M-mode - can see septum → waves (e is taller, a(atrial) is lower)
      • end-diastole - >1.2cm indicates low output state
    • fractional shortening
      • end-diastole distance from septum and posterior wall
      • end-systole distance from septum and posterior wall
      • proportionality indicates EF

Common heart pathologies in critical care

Pericardial effusion

  • measured in the parasternal long axis view
  • Echo is imaging modality of choice for pericardial effusion
  • anechoic fluid in pericardial space
  • Assessment
    • measure distance between parietal and visceral pericardium
      • Small = 0.5cm
      • Moderate = 0.5-2cm
      • Large => 2cm → may contain low level echoes due to clotting rather than anechoic fluid

Pericardial tamponade

  • Aetiology - pericardial fluid accumulation resulting in in-flow blood obstruction
    • can occur at 200mL (acute) or up to 2000mL (chronic)
  • Clinically (Beck's triad)
    • Hypotension - impaired LV stretch and reduced SV
    • Soft/absent heart sounds
    • JVD
  • Findings on US
    • Large pericardial effusion
    • Dilated IVC
    • RA and RV collapse
  • Treatment
    • Aseptic technique - ultrasound needle guidance
    • Approach planned where fluid is closest to the skin
    • Measure the distance to the centre of the effusion with sonography
    • Subxiphoid appraoch for pericardiocentesis most common
      • overhand grip, apply downward pressure; probe to immediate left of xiphoid process and aim towards left shoulder
    • parasternal approach
      • transducer between 2nd - 4th intercostal spaces at left lateral border
      • probe marker at 10-oclock, probe aligned from left nipple to rigth shoulder
      • start in expiration
    • Activated saline technique
      • 16-18 gauge needle and syringe
      • inject agitated saline - bubble appearance at arrow point in sonograph - confirms diagnosis of pericardial effusion

Myocarditis

  • Apical 4-chamber view
    • left and right ventricular dysfunction
      • systolic dysfunction in LV - 30-35%
      • mildly RV dilation
      • mild atrial dilation
    • tricuspid annulus moves reasonable well
    • RV size is OK; LV size is dilated; not assymetrical in movement
    • biventricular dysfunction seen - DDx myocarditis, sepsis, takotsubos cardiomyopathy

  • Parasternal short axis view through ventricle / below mitral valve (chordae level)
    • small pericardial effusion - however this should be measured in diastole in PLAX view (NOT short axis)

Cardiomyopathy - Takotsubos

  • apical takotsubos
    • tends to a focal distributions
    • entire apex involved and sometimes into mid-ventricle
  • symmetrical involvement of ventricles
    • apical takotsubo can look like a LAD infarct

DVT Ultrasound

Indications

  • Leg pain, swelling, erythema
  • Dyspnoea, tachypnoea, pleurisy, concern for PE

Binary questions

  1. Are the deep venous structures compressible (i.e. do anterior and posterior walls touch)
    • Common femoral vein
    • Common femoral vein and saphenous bifurcation
    • Femoral vein and profunda femoris vein
    • Femoral vein
    • Popliteal vein
    • Popliteal trifurcation
  2. bolded = '2 point ultrasound' location

Anatomy

  1. Identify the greater saphenous vein - orient yourself along venous system - this takes off medial on the patient
    • common femoral vein has greater saphenous vein PRIOR to its bifurcation into superficial and deep components

Method

  1. Position - supine, frog legged on side of interest; consider reverse trendelenberg
    • bend knee, externally rotate - makes veins more visible
  2. Place probe in inguinal canal

Thigh ultrasound

Item Image
Common Femoral Vein - left side is the vein
Saphenous junction - see common femoral vein and saphenous tributary; this is a superficial vein but clot will drain into deep system; normally a valve present; should see both the common femoral vein and the saphenous vein close on compression. NOTE you may be able to see the valve flicker in the CFV.
Common femoral vein - check for CVD compression; note that valve may be visible in CFV
Deep branch of CVF - just compress at each deep branch to ensure no further clots
Femoral vein-deep femoral vein junction - make sure DFV is also compressible
Superficial femoral vein and deep femoral vein collapse

Leg ultrasound

Item Image
Popliteal vein - make sure the VEIN is compressed (NOT the artery) - the vein is posterior
Popliteal trifurcation - will see popliteal vein separating trunk (and 2 come off)

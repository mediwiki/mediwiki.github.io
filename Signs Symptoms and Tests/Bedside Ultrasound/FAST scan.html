FAST scan

Indications

  • does not assess for solid viscera and should not be used in acute abdomens
  • FAST = 'focused assessment with sonography for trauma'
    • not reliable for solid organ, bowel and retroperitoneal trauma
    • detects free intraperitoneal fluid or cardiac tamponade
  • Indications
    • HD instability after blunt abdominal trauma
    • unexplained tachycardia or hypotension
    • distracting/CNS injuries
    • intoxication

Method

  • Performed in - supine position with curvilinear 2-5MHz probe
  • Use 'abdomen' preset
  • 4 scanning windows
    • RUQ window - hepatorenal/subhepatic recess
      • look for Morrison's pouch
    • LUQ window - splenic-kidney interface
    • subcostal 4-chamber cardiac view - pericardial effusion, tamponade and LV function
    • Suprapubic view of pelvis

Anatomy - Morison's pouch

  • RUQ scan to detect hepatorenal recess
    • potential space between deep liver capsule and pararenal fascia surroundingr ight kidney
    • fluid does not usually reflect/scatter → anechoic
  • if free intraperitoneal fluid visualised; extend scan to include R) paracolic gutter and pelvis
    • if seen in hepatorenal AND splenorenal fluid detectable → intraperitoneal fluid volume >= 500mL
    • 0.5cm AP measurement of rim of fluid in morrison's pouch → 500mL of free fluid
  • NOTE - free fluid may be present in Morison's pouch in
    • Ascites
    • Haemoperitoneum

Anatomy - Splenic-kidney interface

  • Left upper quadrant scan - examines potential space between splenic capsule and pararenal fascia surrounding LEFT kidney
    • ultrasound detects fluid at interface
    • on left, fluid flows preferentially into subphrenic area rather than splenorenal area
      • therefore LUQ may be hard to visualise because of overlying stomach or bowel gas

Anatomy - subcostal 4-chamber cardiac view

  • anterior and posterior pericardial space
  • subxiphoid view
  • may show anechoic fluid in pericardial space
  • assess for tamponade → reduced LV stretch, reduced SV, reduced CO and hypoTN

Anatomy - suprapubic anatomy of female

  • full bladder scan shows pouch of douglas (rectouterine pouch) and uterovesical pouch
    • note without full bladder, you may miss abdominal fluid due to bowel gas
    • free fluid may be mistaken for bladder
Longitudinal scan Transverse scan

- positive sonographic finding within pouches

Anatomy - suprapubic anatomy of male

  • full bladder in men provides acoustic window to enhance pelvic free fluid detection
  • men - fluid in rectovesicle pouch
    • pelvic fluid flows here as most dependent areas of pelvic
  • normal anatomy

E-FAST scan

  • contains chest ultrasound on top of FAST scan to assess for hemothorax and pneumothorax
  • RUQ and LUQ views extend above and below diaphragm
  • superior-anterior chest scanned with linear array
    • pleural fluid present - normal image artefact present
    • lung floats in chest fluid with inspiration
  • supero-anterior chest scanned with linear arary

RUSH scan with HIMAP protocol

  • rapid ultrasound for shock and hypotension - nontraumatic patients to identify source of shock
  • HIMAP protocol
    • Heart - pericardial effusion, tamponade, RV enlargement (from RH strain 2ndary to PE, RV infarct, pulmonary HTN; enlargement is if RV >= 60% LARGER than LV)
    • IVC
    • Morison's pouch
    • Aorta
    • Pneumothorax (Chest)

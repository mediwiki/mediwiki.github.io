Hypernatremia

Clinical manifestations

  • Neurological signs
    • Fatigue, Headache, Nausea/vomiting - Develops at Na 125-130mEq/L
    • Confusion, Seizures, Coma - at Na+ 120-130mEq/L
    • Symptoms/severity related to derangement severity and speed
    • Occurs due to reduced extracellular osmolality (low Na+) resulting in osmotic gradients causing intracellular fluid increase -> cerebral oedema
    • Note this is the same as in Hyponatremia

Aetiology

  1. Unreplaced water loss
    1. Excessive GI/insensible losses - diarrhoea, sweating, etc.
    2. Diabetes insipidus - inadequate ADH function
      1. Central - reduced ADH production
        • Idiopathic, neurosurgery, trauma, infiltrative disorders (e.g. sarcoidosis, histiocytosis X, GPA), malignancy, CNS infection, genetic/congenital disorders
      2. Nephrogenic - reduced ADH sensivitity in kidneys
        • Medications (e.g. Lithium, amphotericin, demeclocycline, ifosfamide, cisplatin), chronic hypercalcaemia, chronic hypokalemia, various renal diseases (renal amyloidosis, MM, Sjogren's, sickle cell disease), Genetic disorders, Pregnancy
    3. Osmotic diuresis
      • Uncontrolled DM - excessive fluid loss due to loss of glucose (osmolyte)
      • Mannitol usage
  2. Primary sodium intake
    • PO intake
    • Excessive saline administration
  3. Transcellular shift of water in cells - occurs in breakdown of glycogen into lactate (typically self-resolving)
    • Vigorous exercise, seizures

Diagnostic approach

  1. Consider history
    • H2O loss + Primary neurologic disease or lack of H2O access -> hypernatremia
  2. Check Uosm
    • UOsm < 300mOsm/kg -> Diabetes insipidus
    • UOsm 300-600mOsm/kg -> DI or Osmotic diuresis
    • UOsm > 600mOsm/kg -> Impaired thirst or access to H2O (i.e. ADH working properly)
  3. For patients with apparent DI:
    1. Assess plasma osmolarity
      • UOsm < POsm -> Complete DI
      • UOsm > POsm -> Partial DI (i.e. SOME ADH function)
    2. Administer Desmopressin
      • Complete DI cutoffs
        1. UOsm increases by > 50% -> Complete central DI
        2. UOsm increases by < 50% -> Complete nephrogenic DI
      • Partial DI to consider history and brain MRI

Management

NOTE: Excessive rapid correction can result in [[Cerebral Oedema]]

Correction rate

  • Chronic hypernatremia (> 48h): Correction rate <= 10mEq/L/24h
  • Acute hypernatremia (<48h): Correct to normal over 24h

H2O administration

  1. Fluid choice
    • Oral water is PREFERRED
    • NS should ONLY be used for hypovolemic shock initially
    • D5W should be used in pure H2O loss (e.g. Diabetes Insipidus)
    • Hypotonic fluid (e.g. 0.45% NaCl, half normal saline, NS/2) used in hypotonic fluid loss (e.g. diarrhoea)
    • do NOT use D5 1/2NS + 20mEq/L KCl as it is hypertonic
  2. Rate of administration
    1. Calculate Total body water:
      • \(\text{Total Body Water (L)} = \text{Patient lean body weight (kg)} \times k(\text{Proportionality constant - 0.6 children, 0.5 adults, 0.45 elderly})\)
      • \(\Delta[Na^+]_\text{Serum in responsse to 1L IVF} = \frac{([Na^+]_{infusate} + [K^+]_{infusate}) - [Na^+]_{serum}}{TBW + 1}\)
        • does not account for ongoing fluid losses during treatment (E.g. ongoing diarrhoea)
        • recheck serum Na+ every 2-8h and recalculate rates
  3. Anticipated therapy duration
    • \(\text{Anticipated duration of therapy (hrs)} \geq \frac{24h}{10mEq/L} \times ([Na^+]_{initial} - [Na^+]_{goal})\)
  4. Treatment of volume depletion
    • Shock or dehydration -> Focus on fluid resuscitation first - cerebral oedema is not a concern here especially if using NS

Nephrolithiasis (Kidney stones)

Background

  • Risk factors
    • FHx, PMHx
    • high protein diet - breakdown of protein lowers urinary pH and increases uric acid excretion - increases risk of forming uric acid stones
    • Male
    • low fluid intake, dehydration
    • recurrent UTIs
    • Diabetes, gout
    • RTA
    • electrolyte abnormalities
    • medications (e.g. acetazolamide, furosemide, allupurinol)
  • Subtypes
    • Oxalate - the most common renal stone - leading cause is hypercalciuria
    • Uric acid
    • Calcium phosphate
    • Struvite - caused by UTIs secondary to urease positive organisms (e.g. Proteus), can form staghorn calculi
  • Stone location
    • Typically originate from kidney or renal pelvis, but can develop anywhere
    • Typically stuck at narrowing points of the genitourinary tract
      1. Ureteropelvic junction
      2. ureteric crossing over the iliac vessels
      3. ureterovesical junction

Clinical features

  • 'Loin to groin pain' - acute colicky flank pain, extending to groin area
    • period of severe pain during which patient will shift and not stay still, with temporary resolution
    • different from peritonitis as patients would otherwise prefer to remain rigid
  • nausea, vomiting, dysuria
  • Asymptomatic unless lodged in urinary tract and causing obstruction

Investigations

  • CT-KUB is preferred
    • more accurate than XR KUB
  • US KUB if pregnant women and women of childbearing age

Management

  • Smaller stones (< 5 mm) will pass freely into bladder and exit during micturition
    • Supportive management UNLESS patient septic, has solitary kidney, or uncontrolled pain
  • Moderate size (5-9mm) managed based on clinical judgement
  • Large (>9mm)
    • Extracorporeal shock wave lithotripsy
    • percutaneous nephrostomy
    • ureteroscopy
    • rarely, nephrolithotomy
  • Ureteric wall relaxant
    • Alpha blockers (E.g. tamsulosin) can assist stone passage
  • Excessively symptomatic stones (urosepsis, pain, renal damage, solitary kidney) requires ureteral stents or percutaneous nephrostomy tube placements

Syncope

Background

  • Complete, transient loss of conscionsness and postural tone
  • Epidemiology
    • cumulative incidence
      • 3-6% over 10 year period
    • Prevalence
      • 40% of all adults have experienced once
      • 80% of these have had first episode before 30 years
  • 1-2% of all A+E visits

Pathology

  • Global cerebral hypoperfusion due to either
    • decrease in cardiac output
    • decrease in systemic vascular resistance

Classification

  • Neurally mediated (reflex) - most common
    • seen primarily in younger adults
    • Neurocardiogenic / vasodepressor reflex → vasldilation, bradycardia → systemic hypotension → transient hypoperfusion
    • Subtypes
      • Vasovagal syncope - provoked by noxious stimuli, fear, stress, heat overexposure
      • Situational syncope - triggered by cough, micturition, defecation, deglutition
      • Carotid sinus hypersensitivity - head rotation, shaving, tight-fitting neck collar
    • Prodrome - nausea, diaphoresis
    • Postdrome - fatigue, generalised weakness
  • Cardiovascular - second most common
    • associated with increased morbidity, mortality (incl, sudden death), direct trauma
    • LR+ features in history
      • Older age (> 60 years)
      • Male
      • History of IHD, structural heart disease, arrhythmias, reduced ventricular function
      • Prodrome (E.g. palpitations, sudden loss of consciousness without prodrome)
      • Exertional syncope
      • Syncope in supine position
      • Lower number of syncopal episodes (one or two)
      • Abnormal cardiac examination
      • Family history of inheritable conditions or premature sudden cardiac death (< 50 years)
      • presence of known congenital heart disease
    • LR- features in history
      • Younger age, nil cardiac disease, supine syncope or positional syncope
      • prodromal features, specific triggers, situational triggers
      • recurrence and prolonged history syncope with similar characteristics
    • Features
      • typically no prodrome
        • can be associated with chest pain, palpitations
    • Causes
      • arrhythmias
      • coronary artery disease
      • structural and obstructive diseases - including aortic and pulmonary valve stenosis, HOCM, aortic dissection and cardiac tamponade
      • pulmonary embolism associated as well
  • Orthostatic - third most common form
    • Triggered by
      • changes in position
    • Prodrome
      • lightheadedness
    • Mechanism
      • hypovolemia, medications, alcohol intoxication, primary autonomic failure (PD, secondary autonomic failure) → neurogenic orthostatic syncope
  • Neurologic - RARE cause of syncope
    • Causes
      • Cerebrovascular events (TIA, stroke)
      • seizures - can be confused with syncopal events
        • LR+ features of seizures - abnormal posturing, involuntary head turning, tongue laceration, Auras, incontinence, prolonged post-episode confusion
      • diect head trauma
    • Mechanism
      • Posterior (vertebrobasilar) circulation issue → symptoms including dizziness, vertigo, gait changes, focal neurological findings
      • RARELY caused by anterior circulation involvement
  • Psychogenic (Pseudosyncope)
    • LR+ features
      • Younger age
      • Underlying anxiety, panic disorder, depression
  • Idiopathic / unexplained (~ 40%)

Clinical features

  • History
    • Onset - sudden, abrupt
      • Prodromes to be elicited
    • Postepisode symptoms - rapid
      • complete return to baseline level of functioning
    • Psychosocial context (?emotionally charged situation)
    • OTC medications and prescription review
  • Examination
    • Cardiovascular examination
    • Postural BPs
    • Basic neurological assessment for focal defects
    • Carotid hypersensitivity - can attempt carotid sinus massage
      • contraindicated in known carotid disease or TIA/stroke in last 3 months

Diagnostic criteria

Investigations

  • ECG
    • identify underlying arrhythmia, MI, QT prolongation
  • Echocardiography
    • suspected valvular heart disease
    • hypertrophic cardiomyopathy
    • reduced LVEF
  • Exercise stress test in exercise related syncope
  • Holter monitor - dpeending on probably causes/arryhthmias
  • Tilt-table testing
    • suspected vasovagal syncope or if initial orthostatic hypotension investigations not diagnostic
  • Cardiac imaging
    • CT or MRI for structural/infiltrative heart disease
  • CT angiography for pretest probability of pulmonary embolism
  • Electrroencephalography if concern of neurological syncope
  • Brain imaging (CT, MRI) is not routinely recommended
  • Carotid duplex ultrasound is NOT INDICATED in syncope evaluation

Management

  • Neurally mediated syncope - reassurance and education on avoiding provoking measures
    • Physical counterpressure techniques to be considered (E.g. squat, leg squeeze)
  • Cardiovascular syncope - specifically targeted
  • Orthostatic syncope
    • Volume expansion (Salt liberalisation), medication reconciliation, compression stockings, education
    • Vasoactive agents - fludrocortisone, midodrine (limited evidence)
  • Psychogenic syncope - psychiatry referral

Prognosis

  • Patients with syncopal episodes increased risk for
    • all cause morality (HR 1.3, 95%CI 1.1-1.5)
    • cardiovascular events (HR 1.3, 95%CI 1.0-1.6)
  • Patients with cardiac syncope
    • Death (HR 2.0, 95%CI 1.5-2.7)
  • Neurally mediated and orthostatic snycope - nil increase in cardiovascular moratlity
  • Affected QoL, functional independence, self-confidence

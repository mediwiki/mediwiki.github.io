Neck pain

Background

  • Prevalence 30% - 4th leading cause of disability in US
  • Most common causes
    • MSK - cervical strain, spondylosis, cervical facet osteoarthritis
    • whiplash injuries following rapid acceleration/deceleration
    • myofascial pain
      • localised tenderness or 'trigger points' on palpation
    • neurologic causes
      • radiculopathy - spinal nerve root compression resulting from degenerative spinal changes or disk herniation
        • neck pain + radiating arm pain, paresthesia, decreased deep tendon reflexes, diminished strength in affected extremity
        • pain occasionally limited to shoulder girdle
        • Can be reproduced with Spurling test (Sn 30%, Sp 94%) and improved by holding hand above head (shoulder abduction test, Sn 17-78%, Sp 75-92%)
      • Compressive cervical myelopathy - associated with leg weakness, gait, coordination disturbances, bladder/bowel dysfunction
        • UMN signs incl. increased muscle tone, hyperreflexia, clonus
        • LMN signs near level of compression or after acute injury
        • Lhermitte sign may be produced by forward flexion of neck
    • Cancer - consider when suggestive systemic symptoms e.g. weight loss
    • infectious (E.g. epidural abscess, diskitis, ostemyelitis) - consider in chills, fever, IVDU, recent bacteremia

Diagnostic criteria

Investigations

  • History
    • Red flag symptoms in neck pain
      • Recent trauma
      • lower extremity weakness
      • coordination or gait disturbance
      • bladder or bowel dysfunction
      • fevers or chills
      • IVDU
      • chronic glucocorticoid use/immunosuppression
      • Hx cancer or unexplained weight loss
      • unrelenting headache
  • Physical examination
    • inspection/palpation of cervical spine and muscles
    • ROM testing
    • comprehensive neurologic assessment - sensory, motor, reflex testing
    • Spurling test for cervical radiculopathy
  • Imaging
    • nil red flags - imaging JNOT necessary
    • XR for ?fracture, infection, cancer or 6-8 weeks of conervative therapy
    • MRI or CT myelography - ?cancer, ?infection, ?myelopathy or ?neurology despite normal radiographs

Management

  • Most self resolve within 8-12 weeks by using conservative measures
  • Multimodal approach
    • ROM exercises, PT
    • Ice/heat applications
    • Analgesia
      • Oral/topica NSAIDs and paracetamol
      • Muscle relaxants for neck pain associated with muscle spasm
      • Chronic radicular symptoms - venlafaxine and neuromodulators (gabapentin, TCAs)
  • Avoid
    • regular cervical collar use
    • opioids - abuse potential
    • oral glucocorticoids - nil evidence to support
  • Surgery considered for confirmed cervical nerve root compression and progressive motor weakness/persistent radicular pain for > 6-12 weeks

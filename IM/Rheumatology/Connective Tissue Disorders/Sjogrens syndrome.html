Sjogrens syndrome

Background

  • Autoimmune disorder characterised by salivary and lacrimal gland dysfunction
    • decreased tear and saliva production

Pathology

  1. Primary
    • prevalence: 2-10/10,000
  2. Secondary
    • secondary to autoimmune disorders incl Systemic Lupus Erythematosis

Pathophysiology - proliferation and infiltration of lymphocytes in exocrine glands - autoantibody production and role of B-cells in pathophys are unclear

Clinical features

  • Keratoconjunctivitis sicca
    • ocular dryness → corneal injury
  • xerostomia
    • oral dryness → dysphagia
      • dental caries
      • oral candidiasis
    • tongue can look erythematous, loss of papillae, fissure formation
  • nasal dryness, epistaxis
  • vaginal dryness → dyspareunia, candidiasis
  • Parotid gland enlargement
  • Extraglandular disease in primary SS
    • peripheral neuropathy
    • Vasculitis
    • ILD (Lymphocytic interstitial pneumonitis, NSIP)
    • synovitis
  • Associated conditions
    • Connective tissue diseases - Systemic Lupus Erythematosis, Rheumatoid arthritis, Scleroderma
    • hypothyroidism
    • cryoglobulinaemia
    • autoimmune hepatitis

Diagnostic criteria - Primary Sjogren's syndrome

Investigations

Management

  • Exocrine gland dysfunction
    • Xerostomia - oral hygiene, salivary simulants (pilocarpine, muscarinic agonists), see dentist every 4 months
    • KCS - tear replacement, topical cyclosporine
  • Treatment of extraglandular disease difficult
    • B-cell directed therapy
  • Hydroxychloroquine - in fatigue and arthralgias; overall a safe medication

Prognosis

  • Risk factors for aggressive disease
    • positive RF
    • low C4
    • cryoglobulinaemia
  • increased risk of lymphoma with Sjogren's syndrome (lifetime risk ~ 5%)

Behcets disease

Background

  • more common in 'silk route' region of world

Pathology

  • inflammation within veins - perivasculitis
  • disorder related to HLA (HLA B51)
    • 60% with behcets have HLA B51
    • 5.5-6x increased risk of developing Behcets with HLAB51 positivity
  • MHC-1-opathy
    • seen in IBD, psoriasis, ankylosing spondylitis
    • genetically predisposed + enviornmental trigger → develops disease
  • inflammation develops in same area - recurs and relapses
    • relapses can be triggered by stressors/infections

Clinical features

  • Triad - systemic symptoms
    • Mucocutaneous ulceration: Oral and/or genital ulcers, very painful
      • hard to treat with topical agents
    • Rash, folliculitis
    • Uveitis/iritis
  • Meningoencephalitis - can affect brainstem, spinal cord
    • rarely affects peripheral nerves
  • perivasculitis develops and spreads into nervous system
    • develops in vasa vasorum of veins - vein wall inflammation → thrombosis (blood sticks to wall)
    • anticoagulation won't help - need to treat inflammation in vessel wall

Diagnostic criteria

  • Recurrent oral ulceration plus two of the following:
    • Recurrent genital ulceration
    • Eye lesions (anterior/posterior uveitis or cells in vitreous or retinal vasculitis)
    • skin lesions (Erythema nodosum, pseudofolliculitis, papulopustular lesions or acneiform nodules)
    • positive pathergy test
  • Sn 91%, Sp 96%

Investigations

Management

  • systemic treatment
    • corticosteroids, colchicine
    • occasionally AZA, TNF inhibitors
  • Vascular disorder - treated by treating underlying disease

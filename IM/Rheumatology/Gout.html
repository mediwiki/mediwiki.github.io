Gouty Arthritis

Background

  • Metabolic heterogenous disease
  • Abnormal urate deposits in tissues
    • deposition occurs with supersaturated uric acid levels
  • Mechanism of hyperuricaemia
    • overproduction of uric acid
    • underexcretion of uric acid
  • Populations at risk
    • Pacific islanders (e.g. Filipinos, Samoans)
    • Familial risk - heritable component
    • Men (90%) over 30
    • Postmenopausal women
  • Precpipitants
    • EtOH excess (particularly beer)
    • Hospitalised patient
    • Changes in medications affecting urate metabolism
    • Fasting prior to medical procedures
    • Chronic lead intoxication (saturnine gout)

Pathophysiology

  • Uric acid supersaturation → Urate crystal deposition
  • Monocyte and synviocyte urate crystal ingestion → inflammation
  • late gout: tophaceous invasion of periarticular and articular tissue → secondary degeneration (OA) and structural derangement

Clinical features

  • recurrent acute arthritis - usually monoarticular
    • later - chronic deforming arthritis
  • Gout tophus - nodular monosodium urate monohydrate crystal w/ associated foreign body reaction
    • found in cartilage, subcutaneous and periarticular tissue, tendons, bones, kidneys, etc.
    • MTP of great toe most susceptible - 'podagra'
  • Uric acid kidney stones (5-10%) → chronic urate nephropathy (monosodium urate crystal deposition in renal medulla and pyramids)
  • Fever may occur during attack

Investigations

  • Single normal uric acid level does NOT exclude gout, especially if taking urate lowering drugs
  • Joint Aspirate is diagnostic
    • needle-like, negatively birefringent crystals
  • Punched out 'rat-bite' erosions overhanging rim of cortical bone
  • Ultrasound
  • Dual-energy CT

Differential diagnoses

  • Septic arthritis, Pseudogout
  • Chronic RA

Treatment

  1. Acute Attack
    • NSAIDs - full dose, continue until symptoms have resolved
    • Colchicine (of onset < 36h) - started at 1g/500mg then 500mg BD until pain resolves
    • Corticosteroids - Prednisone PO 40-60mg/day for 5-10 days then discotninued
    • IL-1 inhibitors - Anakinra/canakinumab (NOT FDA approved)
  2. Between attacks
    • Diet - reduce EtOH consumption, organ meats, high fructose beverages
    • Avoid hyperuricemic medications - Thiazide, loop diuretics, low dose aspirin
    • Colchicine prophylaxis - when starting urate lowering therapy
    • Urate lowering therapy

Special cases

  • Transplant patients - uric acid excretion can be inhibited by antifailure medications (cyclosporine) and diuretics (loop, thiazide)
    • If polyarticular, ry to increase prednisone dose
    • If monoarticular, consider corticosteroid injection
    • Long term uric acid lowering therapy needed to minimise overall risk of CKD from gout crystals
    • Avoid allopurinol and febuxostat in patients using azathioprine (can inhibit metabolism)

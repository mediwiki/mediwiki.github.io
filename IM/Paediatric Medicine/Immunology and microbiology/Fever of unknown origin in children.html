Fever of unknown origin in children

Pathogenesis of fever

  1. Foreign material phagocytosis
  2. Peripheral synthesis of IL-1, IL-6, TNF, IFa, pyogenic cytokines
  3. Cytokines enter anterior hypothalamus
  4. Induction of Prostaglandin synthesis (PGE2) → raising of hypothalamic set-point
  5. Body recognises current temperature too low → elevation of body temperature to set-point
    1. increased metabolic rate
    2. increased muscle tone and activity
    3. decreased heat loss through decreased skin perfusion
  6. pyrogenic cytokines → synthesis of acute phase proteins by liver
    1. decreased serum Fe, Zn
    2. leukocytosis
    3. skeletal muscle proteolysis
    4. IL-1 → slow-wave sleep (associated with ?somnolence)
    5. PGE2 → myalgia, arthralgia

Euthermia: Hypothalamic set-point is normal, and body temperature approximates set-point and is also normal. Fever: Hypothalamic set-point is elevated, and body temperature follows set-point and is also elevated. Heat illness: Hypothalamic set-point is normal, and body temperature is elevated despite normal set-point.

Approach to fever

  1. Infective causes - most common
    • Generalised infections
      • Common causes
        • Cat scratch disease
        • Malaria
        • Salmonella
        • Tuberculosis
        • Viral infection (CMV, EBV, adenovirus) - nasal discharge
      • Uncommon causes
        • Brucellosis - limb or bone pain
        • Leptospirosis
        • Toxoplasmosis
        • Tularemia
      • Rare causes
        • Arbovirus
        • Enterovirus
        • Fungal infections (nonpulmonary blastomycosis, disseminated histoplasmosis)
        • Hepatitis viruses
        • HIV
        • Lymphogranuloma venereum
        • Psittacosis
        • Q fever
        • Rocky Mountain spotted fever
        • Visceral larva migrans
    • Localised infections
      • Infective endocarditis - rare in term infants, increased frequency in older children
      • Intra-abdominal abscess (hepatic, perinephric, pelvic, subdiaphragmatic) - liver, phrenic, perinephric, pelvic abscess as cause of FUO
      • Liver infection (eg, bacterial cholangitis, granulomatous hepatitis) - granulomatous hepatitis associated with bartonella; seen on US but confirmed on biopsy
      • Osteomyelitis and septic arthritis (less frequently) - limb or bone pain; typically associated with pelvic bones, small bones, flat bones rather than long bones
        • Diagnosis by imaging (CT, MRI, radioisotopic) rather than XR
      • Upper respiratory infection: Chronic or recurrent otitis media, chronic or recurrent pharyngitis, mastoiditis, peritonsillar abscess, sinusitis
      • Urinary tract infection
  2. Autoimmune
    1. Rheumatologic diseases
      • Juvenile idiopathic arthritis - three forms, fevers can be present in each
        • systemic presentation - high, spiking fevers, evanescent rash, lymphadenopathy
        • polyarticular involvement
        • monoarticular (oligoarticular) involvement
      • Systemic lupus erythematosus
      • Vasculitis (eg, polyarteritis nodosa, temporal arteritis)
    2. IBD
    3. Thyrotoxicosis
    4. Vasculitis
      • Kawasaki disease - red eyes with spontaneous resolution
        • Dx: bulbar conjunctivitis, oral changes (strawberry tongue), rash, hand/feet changes, cervical adenopathy
  3. Malignancies
    • Common causes - limb or bone pain
      • Leukemia
      • Lymphoma
    • Uncommon causes
      • Atrial myxoma
      • Hepatoma
      • Neuroblastoma
      • Sarcoma
  4. Miscellaneous causes
    1. Vaccination responses
    2. Drug fever - iatrogenic
      • atropine (topical)
      • some drugs can impair thermoregulation and control mechanisms rather than through allergies (e.g. phenothiazine, anticholinergics, epinephrine, etc.)
      • Antibiotic response (lactam antibiotics in particular)
    3. Anaesthetic agents
    4. Autoinflammatory
      • Periodic fever (eg, Mediterranean fever, PFAPA syndrome) - Autoinflammatory syndromes (abnormalities of innate immune system)
        • Familial mediterranean fever (FMF) - mediterranean descent autosomal recessive disease; episodic fever with serosal inflammation
        • Hyperimmunoglobulin D syndrome (HIDS) - fever, skin eruptions, abdominal complaints, joint involvement
        • cyclic neutropenia - fever during periods of severe neutropenia (15-35 day cycles)
    5. Systematic problems
      • Central nervous system dysfunction - thermoregulation dysfunction with intermittent/recurrent elevated temperatures
        • possible epilepsy-induced fevers
      • Diabetes insipidus (central or nephrogenic) - Ulster Scots have an association of fever, also presents with polyuria
        • diagnosis: electrolytes and osmolality in serum and urine during normal hydration and water restriction
    6. Congenital
      • Familial dysautonomia - association with Ashkenazi Jews
        • riley-day syndrome, HSAN3 - autosomal recessive
    7. Blood
      • Blood in the wrong place (e.g. haemopericardium, haemoperitoneum, haemothorax, IVH) - can cause a profound immune response
      • clotted blood (DVT, thrombophlebitis - inflammation in response to blood clot)
    8. Ectodermal dysplasia
    9. Factitious fever
      • false report by parent, caused by dipping thermometer in hot liquid
    10. Granulomatous colitis
    11. Hemophagocytic lymphohistiocytosis
      • nonmalignant life-threatening disorder due to lymphocytic and hystiocytic proliferation → hemophagocytosis and disregulated hypersecretion of inflammatory cytokines
    12. Immunodeficiency
      • immunoglobulin deficiences (e.g. Bruton agammaglobulinaemia) have Hx of recurrent fevers +/- focal infections
    13. Infantile cortical hyperostosis (Caffey disease)
      • persistent fevers up to 40, subperiosteal bone hyperplasia, overlying tissue swelling, irritability, tenderness, fever, leukocytosis, raised ESR
      • Dx: clinical features + radiological involvement of periosteum
    14. Kikuchi disease (Kikuchi-Fujimoto, Kikuchi histiocytic necrotizing lymphadenitis)
      • Young females < 40y/o, related to T-cell and hystiocytic response to infectious agent
      • Lymph node biopsy demonstrates paracortical focu with necrosis and histiocytic cellular infiltrates
    15. Pancreatitis
    16. Sarcoidosis
    17. Serum sickness

Management of fever

  1. Patient education
  2. Antipyretic agents
    • Indicated for patient discomfort, reducing water loss/dehydration
    • THINGS TO WATCH FOR
      • Liver failure → avoid paracetamol
      • SSRI usage → avoid ibuprofen (antiplatelet effect)
    • Start with paracetamol - especially if anti-inflammatory effect is desired (better safety record with therapeutic dosing)
    • Ibuprofen can be used - slightly longer lasting and more effective, but kidney injury risk

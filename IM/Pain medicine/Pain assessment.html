Clinical approach to pain

Classification of pain

  • Acute vs chronic pain
    • Acute pain
      • Duration: < 6 weeks
    • Chronic pain
      • Duration: > 3 months
      • typically associated with tissue healing
  • Nociceptive vs neuropathic
    • Nociceptive pain
      • MOA: visceral or somatic nociceptor involvement
      • Visceral pain occurs from injury or abdominal firing of visceral pain fibres
        • Sx: localised cramping, achiving
      • Somatic: injury to somatic pain fibres conveing signals from muscles, bones and joints
        • Musculoskeletal pain
        • Inflammatory pain
        • Sx: sharp stabbing pain
    • Neuropathic pain
      • MOA: Injury to peripheral nerve structures or central nervous system
      • Peripheral nerve syndromes
        • e.g. postherpetic neuralgia
        • Diagnosed by identifying distribution of affected peripheral nerves
      • Central neuropathic pain syndromes
        • e.g. cerebrovascular accident or spinal-cord related injuries
        • widely varying presentations, can evade diagnosis
        • associated with hyperalgesia (hypersensivity to painful stimuli) or allodynia (pain from normally non-painful stimuli)
  • Pain from chronic opioid therapy (Opioid induced hyperalgesia)
    • MOA: repeat exposure to systemic opioids
    • Sx: Changing character if pain during opioid therapy, worsening pain with increased opioid disages, pain reduction on reducing opioid dosages

Assessment of pain

  • History
    • Location, character, duration, severity, timing, responsiveness to treatment
    • Pain scales
      • Numeric - common, however note this may not correlate with severity, tolerability or functional impairment
    • Functional status assessment
      • Physical function
      • ability to perform iADLs
      • psycho-social-spiritual function
    • Psychological comorbidities and mental health disorder screen
      • Depression and anxiety associated with chronic pain
    • Substance use - increased risk of opioid misuse, common in chronic pain syndromes
  • Physical examination
    • Inspection of painful region
    • Comprehensive neurological evaluation
  • Further investigations
    • Acute pain - consider additional tests and imaging (though may not be high yield)
  • Red flags
    • Cancer history or cancer treatment-related pain

        • Constitutional symptoms (fever, involuntary weight loss)
        • changes in bowel or bladder function
        • weakness or sensory deficits

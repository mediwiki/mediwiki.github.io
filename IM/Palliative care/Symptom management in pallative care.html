Symptom management in pallative care

Background

  • Effective symptom management crucial to palliative care interventions

Dyspnoea

Background

  • Most prominent and distressing symptom with underlying illness
  • Goal - address underlying cause (e.g. pleural effusion, infection, anaemia, COPD, heart failure) and treat hypoxaemia

Management

  • Hypoxaemia → supplemental O2
  • Refractory dyspnoea → morphine
    • reduces sensation of dyspnoea, do not cause respiratory depression or hasten death when appropriately selected and dosed

Oropharyngeal secretions

Background

  • Audible oropharyngeal secretions ('death rattle') occur at end of life, distressing for families and clinicians
  • occur in up to 50% of dying patients

Management

  • Treatment is generally ineffective
  • antimuscarinics to decrease oropharyngeal secretions not generally recommended
    • scopolamine, glycopyrronium, hyoscine, atropine, octreotide is not superior to no treatment
  • anticholinergics can lead to dry mouth and urinary retention
  • avoid deep suctioning by catheter
  • consider raising bed/positioning of patient

Nausea

Background

  • common and debilitating symptoms
  • rated more distressing than unrelieved pain

Management

  • target towards underlying mechanism and associated neurotransmitter pathway

Constipation

Background

  • common in serious illness
  • causes include
    • opioids
    • dehydration
    • immobility
    • metabolic disturbances
    • nonopioid medication

Management

  • prophylaxis - consider when constipation likely to occur
  • education RE fluid intake and physical activity
  • pharmacotherapy
    • osmotive laxatives
      • polyethylene glycol
    • simulant laxative
      • senna
      • bisacodyl
    • docusate alone or in combination is no more effective than placebo
    • rectal suppository / enema preparations
      • phosphate- or magnesium enemas contraindicated due to risk of dangerous electrolyte shift

Anorexia and weight loss

Background

  • occur as part of the natural progression of life-limiting illness

Management

  • address reversible contributors to anorexia to ease patient and family distress
  • enteral/parenteral artificial nutritional support does not improve survival, is invasive, can cause side effects (increased resp secretions, uncomfortable oedema)

Depression

Background

  • grief common, clinical depression not expected
  • should be treated aggressively

Management

  • Pharmacotherapy
    • antidepressants appropriate for estimated prognosis
    • SSRIs effective and safe with end-organ dysfunction, however therapeutic effects not reached for several weeks
    • methylphenidate favoured if patients have < 6 weeks life expectancy

Anxiety

Background

  • coexists with depression
  • potential triggers
    • existential fears
    • uncertainty inherent to dying process
    • difficult decisions
    • concern for loved ones
    • financial issues

Management

  • nonpharmacological
    • social supports, relaxation therapy, exercise, mindfulness meditation, psychotherapy
  • pharmacologic
    • benzodiazepines - closely monitor for delirium
    • SSRIs for coexisting depression

Delirium

Background

  • may be reversible however cause not often identified

Management

  • non-harmacologic
    • hydration
    • orienting stimuli
    • reducing noise
  • pharmacologic
    • little evidence for antipsychotics
    • first generation antipsychotics (e.g. haloperidol) noninferior to newer antipsychotics

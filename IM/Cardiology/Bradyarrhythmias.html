Bradyarrhythmias

Background

The general principle of bradyarrythmia therapy is:

  • if the cause is identifiable (e.g. drugs - (digoxin, beta blockers, verapamil, diltiazem, amiodarone)), stop the drugs!
  • if is doesn't threaten your life, don't do anything - it's fine (asymptomatic bradyarrhythmias do not need treatment)

  • if it causes an AF or AFib (e.g. in sick sinus syndrome) → anticoagulate to prevent Cardioembolic thrombi

  • If bradycardia originates ABOVE the AV node → give atropine

  • If atropine doesn't work → use pacemaker → Bridge with:
    • isoprenaline OR
    • Adrenaline (preferred if low SBP VIA CVC)

Pathophysiology

  • Two basic mechanisms of bradycardia
    1. Reduced automaticity of sinus node → sl;ow heart rate or pauses
      • cessation of pacemaker activity → heart activated by other tissues with pacemaker activity
      • reduced automaticity caused by
        • increased vagal tone (sleep, carotid sinus massage)
        • increasing age
        • drugs (beta blockers, calcium channel blockers)
    2. Cardiac impulse prevented from activating ventricles normally due to blocked conduction
      • fibrous valvular annulus electrically inert - thus AV node and His bundle normally form the only electrically active connection between atria and ventricles
      • AV node and His bundle thus vulnerable sites for blocked conduction
        • blockade of L) or R) bundle branches does not necessarily cause bradycardia due to contralateral function
      • Occurs with
        • increasing age
        • increased vagal input
        • side effect of drugs
        • congenital disorders (e.g. muscular dystrophy, tuberous sclerosis, maternal SLE)
        • acquired disorders (Sarcoidosis, gout, Lyme disease, SLE, ankylosing spondylitis, coronary artery disease)

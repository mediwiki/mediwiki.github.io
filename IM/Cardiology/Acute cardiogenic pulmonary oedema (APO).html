Acute Cardiogenic Pulmonary Oedema

Background

  • Acute dyspnea associated with elevated left-sided filling pressures, with or without pulmonary edema.
  • Most commonly due to LV systolic or diastolic dysfunction.
  • Flash pulmonary edema refers to a severe form of heart failure with rapid accumulation of fluid in the lungs.
  • Originates from both (1) an increased alveolar fluid and (2) extreme SNS activation

Diagnosis

  • Differential includes pulmonary embolism, asthma, and pneumonia, all of which can cause rapid respiratory distress.
  • Diagnostic tests include ECG, CXR, ABG, B-type natriuretic peptide (BNP), echocardiogram, and possible coronary angiogram if indicated.

Treatment (LMNOP)

  • Oxygenation and ventilatory assistance with nonrebreather face mask, noninvasive positive pressure ventilation, or even intubation as indicated. (only if SpO2 < 94%)
    • Non-invasive CPAP with 100% O2
  • Diuretics to treat volume overload and congestive symptoms—this is the most important intervention. Decreases preload.- typically use Furosemide
  • Nitrates—IV nitroglycerin (venodilator) in patients without hypotension.
    • Decreases preload; GTN spray also can be used
    • Be careful if patient is hypotensive (SBP < 100mmHg or symptomatic)
  • Morphine given (anxiolytic)
  • If the decompensation is due to an AFib:
    • Amiodarone 300mg IV over 1hr
    • Non responsive → consider electrical cardioversion
  • Patients who have pulmonary edema despite use of oxygen, diuretics, and nitrates may benefit from use of inotropic agents (dobutamine).
    • Digoxin takes several weeks to work and is not indicated in an acute setting.

  • If the patient is still unresposive to therapy → intubation is necessary

  • Acute Exacerbation presentation

    • ischemia
    • rhythm disturbance
    • high salt diet

Case:

A 35-year-old man presents to your clinic for a general checkup and cholesterol screening. He denies having medical problems and takes no medications on a regular basis. He works as a computer programmer, exercises regularly at a gym, and does not smoke or use illicit drugs. He drinks two to three beers on the weekend. His father suffered his first heart attack at age 42 and eventually died of complications of heart disease at age 49. The patient’s older brother recently was diagnosed with “high cholesterol.”

The patient’s blood pressure is 125/74 mm Hg and heart rate 72 bpm. He is 69 in tall and weighs 165 lb. His physical examination is unremarkable.

Fasting lipid levels are drawn. The next day, you receive the results: total cholesterol 362 mg/dL, triglycerides 200 mg/dL, high-density lipoprotein (HDL) 36 mg/dL, and low-density lipoprotein (LDL) 266 mg/dL.

  • What is the most likely diagnosis?
  • What is your next step?
  • What are the possible complications if left untreated?

This patient has familial hypercholesterolaemia (most likely) → Treat with HMG-CoA reductase inhibitors (statin)

  • Atherosclerotic vascular disease → can result in CAD
  • Statin usage can result in myopathy (muscle tenderness, elevated CK) or hepatitis (incl. mild transaminitis) → CK, LFTs should be monitored

Familial hypercholesterolaemia is caused by defective or absent LDL-R, and inability to metabolise LDL particles by the liver

Other medications for hyperlipidaemia:

  • nicotinic acid (niacin) → raises HDL
  • Bile acid resins (cholestyramine) → binds bile acid in GIT to prevent reabsorption → lowers LDL
  • Fibric acid derivitives (gemfibrozil, fenofibrate)
  • cholesterol absorption inhibitor (azatimibe)

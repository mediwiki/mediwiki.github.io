Diabetes Insipidus

Background

  1. Two forms
    1. Central DI is the most common form—due to low ADH secretion by posterior pituitary.
    2. Nephrogenic DI—ADH secretion is normal but tubules cannot respond to ADH.
  2. Causes
    1. Central DI
      1. Idiopathic—50% of all cases
      2. Trauma—surgery, head trauma
      3. Other destructive processes involving the hypothalamus, including tumors, sarcoidosis, tuberculosis, syphilis, Hand–Schüller–Christian disease, eosinophilic granuloma, and encephalitis
    2. Nephrogenic DI—the most common cause in adults is chronic lithium use. Other causes include hypercalcemia, pyelonephritis, and demeclocycline use. It may also be congenital—caused by mutations in the ADH receptor gene or the aquaporin-2 gene.

Clinical features

  1. Polyuria is a hallmark finding: 5 to 15 L daily; urine is colorless (because it is sodilute).
  2. Thirst and polydipsia—hydration is maintained if the patient is conscious and hasaccess to water.
  3. Hypernatremia is usually mild unless the patient has an impaired thirst drive

Diagnosis

  1. Urine—low specific gravity and low osmolality indicate DI
  2. Plasma osmolality
    1. Normal: 250 to 290 mOsm/kg
    2. Primary polydipsia: 255 to 280 mOsm/kg
    3. DI: 280 to 310 mOsm/kg
  3. A water deprivation test (dehydration test) is required to make the diagnosis
    1. Procedure
      1. Withhold fluids, and measure urine osmolality every hour.
      2. When urine osmolality is stable (<30 mOsm/kg hourly increase for 3 hours), inject 2 g desmopressin subcutaneously. Measure urine osmolality 1 hour later.
    2. Response
      1. Normal patients: UOsm > 280 with dehydration, but no response to additional ADH
      2. DI patients: UOsm unchanged with dehydration, but responds to ADH
      3. Nephrogenic DI patients: UOsm does not change with dehydration nor ADH
  4. ADH level (not the test of choice; takes a long time to get results)
    1. Low in central DI
    2. Normal or elevated in nephrogenic DI

Treatment

  1. Central DI
    1. Desmopressin (DDAVP) is the primary therapy and can be given by nasal spray, orally, or by injection.
    2. Chlorpropamide increases ADH secretion and enhances the effect of ADH.
    3. Treat the underlying cause.
  2. Nephrogenic DI—treat with sodium restriction and thiazide diuretics.
    1. These deplete the body of sodium, which leads to increased reabsorption of sodium and water in the proximal tubules.
    2. The reabsorption of sodium and water in the proximal tubules means that less water reaches the distal tubules, leading to decreased urine volume and increased water retention.
      • kidneys unresponsive to ADH → urine very dilute, raised plasma osmolarity → thirst stimulation
        • thiazide diuresis → inhibition of NaCl channels → Na released into ultrafiltrate, less absorbed → reduces serum osmolarity → stops thirst stimulation

Type I Diabetes

Background

  • This is characterized by a severe deficiency of insulin. → Patients require insulin to live.
    • complete insulin deficiency
  • The onset is typically in youth (before age 20), but can occur at any age. Not related to obesity.
    • around 50% are diagnosed in adulthood
  • Cause: autoimmune destruction of insulin-secreting pancreatic beta cells
    • Associated with other autoimmune disorders (>90% are HLA-DR3/4)
    • ~30% concordance in identical twins → polygenetic cause
  • Must treat with insulin → risk of ketoacidosis and weight loss
  • Latent autoimmune diabetes of adults (LADA) is a form of T1DM → slower progression to insulin dependence later in life
    • now called "Slowly evolving immune mediated diabetes"
  • 10% of all patients with Diabetes mellitus

Pathogenesis

  • An autoimmune disease—The immune system mediates the destruction of βcells.
  • It develops in genetically susceptible individuals who are exposed to an environmental factor that triggers the autoimmune response; β-cell destruction ensues.
  • Overt Type 1 DM does not appear until about 90% of β-cells are destroyed.

Investigations

  • 20% of patients are estimated to be negative to antibody testing
    • ­Appear to have slower rates of beta cell destruction
    • ­Lower insulin requirements
    • ­Higher C-peptide levels

Management

  • NEVER withhold basal insulin - can risk developing Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA)
  • Only withhold short-acting insulin if they are not eating a meal
    • hypoglycaemic pre-meal → treat hypo, then give prescribed short acting insulin with meal
    • any time they eat any part of a meal → give short acting insulin
  • Check ketones in BGL > 15 or feeling unwell (check if BGL > 10 if obstetric)

Prognosis

Progression of type I diabetes - Symptoms develop quickly over days to weeks. - Sometimes symptoms appear after an illness. - Patients often present with acute diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA)

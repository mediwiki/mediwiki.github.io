Syndrome of Inappropriate Secretion of Antidiuretic Hormone (SIADH)

Background

  1. Pathophysiology
    1. Excess ADH is secreted from the posterior pituitary or an ectopic source. Elevated levels lead to water retention and excretion of concentrated urine. This has two major effects: hyponatremia and volume expansion.
    2. Despite volume expansion, edema is not seen in syndrome of inappropriate secretion of antidiuretic hormone (SIADH). This is because natriuresis (excretion of excessive sodium in urine) occurs despite hyponatremia.
    3. Reasons for natriuresis
      1. Volume expansion causes an increase in atrial natriuretic peptide (ANP) which increases urine sodium excretion.
      2. Volume expansion leads to a decrease in proximal tubular sodium absorption.
      3. The renin–angiotensin–aldosterone system is inhibited, and thus sodium is excreted in the urine.

Mechanism

  1. Neoplasms (e.g., in lung, pancreas, prostate, bladder), lymphomas, leukemia
  2. CNS disorders (e.g., stroke, head trauma, infection)
  3. Pulmonary disorders (e.g., pneumonia, tuberculosis)
  4. Ventilators with positive pressure
  5. Medication (e.g., vincristine, selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors, chlorpropamide, oxytocin, morphine, desmopressin)
  6. Postoperative state (e.g., as a result of anesthesia, pain, or stress)

Clinical features

  1. Acute hyponatremia—Osmotic water shifts lead to increased intracellular fluid volume. Signs and symptoms are secondary to brain swelling and are primarily neurologic.
    1. Lethargy, somnolence, weakness
    2. Can lead to seizures, coma, or death if untreated.
  2. Chronic hyponatremia
    1. May be asymptomatic
    2. Anorexia, nausea, and vomiting
    3. CNS symptoms are less common because chronic loss of sodium and potassium from brain cells decreases brain edema (due to secondary water shifts from ICF to ECF).
  3. Major characteristics
    • Hyponatremia
    • Volume expansion without oedema
    • Natriuresis
    • Hypourecmia and low BUN
    • Normal or reduced SCr because of dilution
    • Normal thyroid and adrenal function

Diagnosis

  1. SIADH is a diagnosis of exclusion after other causes of hyponatremia have been ruled out. The following help in supporting the diagnosis:
    1. Hyponatremia with inappropriately concentrated urine; plasma osmolality <280 mOsm per kg.
    2. Low serum uric acid level
    3. Low BUN and creatinine
    4. Normal thyroid and adrenal function, as well as renal, cardiac, and liver function
    5. Measurement of plasma and urine ADH level
    6. Absence of significant hypervolemia

Treatment

  1. Correct the underlying cause, if known.
  2. For asymptomatic patients
    1. Water restriction is usually sufficient.
    2. Use normal saline in combination with a loop diuretic if faster results are desired.
    3. Lithium carbonate or demeclocycline are other options (with side effects)—both inhibit the effect of ADH in the kidney.
  3. For symptomatic patients
    1. Restrict water intake.
    2. Give isotonic saline. Hypertonic saline may occasionally be indicated in severe cases.
  4. Do not raise the serum sodium concentration too quickly. Rapid flux of water into the extracellular fluid can result in central pontine myelinolysis (or osmotic demyelination syndrome). A general guideline is that the rate of sodium replacement should not exceed 0.5 mEq/L/hr.

Immunotherapy related Endocrinopathies

RACP dotpoint

  • Broad pharmacological principles and complications of chemotherapy, endocrine therapy, immunotherapy, molecular targed therapy and radiotherapy

Immune checkpoint inhibitors

  • Removes break on immune system
    • allows immune recognition and destruction of cancer cells
    • also result in self-targets (off target immune responses)
  • monoclonal antibodies targeting specific cell surface molecules which act as checkpoints on the immune system
    • Anti-CTLA4 - ipilimumab
    • Anti-PD1 - nivolumab, pembrolizumab, cemiplimab
    • Anti-PDL1 - atezolizumab, avelumab, durvalumab

Immune related adverse events

  • endocrine events most common
    • 10-20%
    • median onset 8 weeks post initiation
    • most commonly pituitary, thyroid, less commonly adrenals, pancreas, parathyroid
  • immunologic response tends to result in irreversible endocrine dysfunction and destruction of tissues
    • irreverisble hypotension
    • however the greater the IRAE, the greater the oncological response

- overall survival in melanoma treatment vs. experience of endocrine events

General approach to IRAEs

  • Typically nonspecific presentations or asymptomatic
    • potentially fatal - particularly hypocorticolism
    • routine monitoring needed through biochemical testing
  • European society 2022 guidelines
    • minimum - prior to each endocrine cycle
      • 8-9am serum cortisol, TSH, fT4, glucose, EUCs
    • high risk scenarios
      • ACTH, LH, FSH, E2/testosterone, prolactin (e.g. if on ipilimumab)
      • HbA1c (e.g. if suspected pre-existing T2DM and need to assess for development of autoimmune diabetes
    • Diagnostic testing PRN and screen for other endocrinopathies if concerned
    • existing endocrinopathy is NOT a contraindication for ICI initiation/continuation
      • no additinoal risk of adverse events
      • cancer benefit significantly outweighs endocrinopathy
      • Endocrine emergencies (e.g. DKA, adrenal crisis, severe thyrotoxicosis, severe grave's opthalmopathy) MUST be treated prior to next cycle
      • high dose steroids is rarely indicated - no evidence that it affects permanent hormone deficiencies
      • endocrinologist should be involved unless isolated primary hypothyroidism
        • may be permanent and co-exist
        • risk of long term complications

Pituitary IRAE

  • Hypophysitis related to immunotherapy
    • RF: Male, Older age, Ipilimumab if >3mg/kg
    • typically ACTH deficiency (vs nonimmune mediated hypophysitis where you see hyperprolactinaemia or DI)
    • positive pitutiary antibodies found when tested in literature
      • however not commonly available
    • diagnosed off
      • full pitutitary panel
      • pituitary MRI - mainly to exclude pituitary metastasis

Two main phenotypes

IRAE: ACTH phenotype hypophysitis

Clinical presentation

  • Adrenal crisis

Diagnosis and management

  • Take blood for cortisol and ACTH → send on ice
  • do not wait for results
    • Give hydrocortisone 100mg IV/IM → 50mg q6hrly + IVF resuscitation
    • maintenance therapy
      • hydrocoritsone 15-25mg split doses → monitor clinically (no role for cortisol monitoring)
      • educate RE sick day rules, steroid emergency card, MedicAlert bracelet, taught to monitor Solu-Cortef
      • no mineralocorticoid requirement in secondary adrenal insufficiency - aldosterone is produced by RAAS rather than ACTH
        • Addison's → needs hydrocortisone AND fludrocortisone
        • Central → needs hydrocortisone only
  • NO role for supraphysiological steroids unless visual loss due to suprachiasmal compression or intractible headaches
    • even in these cases, would be questionable

Prognosis

  • ACTH recovery rare → will likely need lifelong treatment

IRAE: TSH deficiency hypophysitis

Diagnosis

  • Low-normal fT3 and inappropriately normal/low TSH
    • may be heralded by TSH drop - >80% drop in TSH resulted in ipilimumab induced hypophysitis in Australian study

Management

  • Thyroxine 1mcg/kg (25-50 mcg if elderly/cardiac disease)
  • repeat TFTs 6 weekly → aim fT4 upper half of normal range

Prognosis

  • TSH recovery infrequent
  • consider tapering and trialling off thyroxine if low requirements

IRAE: Central hypogonadism (LH/FSH low) hypophysitis

Management

  • Depends on oncological prognosis
    • If poor oncological prognosis:
      • do NOT replace LH/FSH - suppression may be physiological with minimal benefit - especially if older
      • deficiency may be reversible
    • If good oncological prognosis without contraindications to sex hormone therapy (e.g. breast, prostate, ovarian)
      • Testosterone for men
      • Oestrogen for reproducitve age women
        • progestin for in-tact uterus for endometrial protection
    • consider preventative management
      • Calcium/vitamin D as needed

IRAE: GH deficiency hypophysitis

Management

  • probably underestimated, but do not look for this in a clinical or academic setting

IRAE: ADH deficiency (Diabetes insipidus) hypophysitis

General features

  • Diabetes insipidus rare

Diagnosis

  • Poluria >50mL/kg/day
  • serum osmolality > 295mOsm/kg + urine Osm:serum Osm < 2
  • Consider other causes of polyuria - hyperglycaemia, diuretics, renal dysfunction, hypokalaemia, hypercalcaemia
  • if cannot diagnose on baseline testing, consider
    • stimulated copeptin (e.g. hypertonic saline)
    • desmopressin stimulation test - 1mcg IV/SC = 10mcg intranasal = 200mcg PO

IRAE: Thyroid related IRAEs

General features

  • Most common ICI-induced endocrinopathy, especially associated with anti-PD1 inhibitors
    • maybe due to PD1-R on thyrocytes

Clinical presentation

  • transient thyrotoxicosis within 6wks of ICI initiation → hypothyroidism after another 6 weeks
  • typically destructive thyroiditis - rarely Graves' disease +/- ophthalmopathy
    • thyrotoxic phase generally asymptomatic and resolves spontaneously
    • hypothyroidism usually irreversible
  • no reliable predictive factors for risk/pattern

Diagnosis and management

  • TSH + fT4
    • consider confounders - drugs, nonthyroidal illness, iodine loads
  • Primary hypothyroidism → Anti-TPO
    • thyroxine if low fT4 + TSH > 10mIU/L - if above this, then possible confounding - hold off treatment til diagnosis confirmed
    • Same dose as for central hypothyroidism:
      • 1mcg/kg starting dose → TFTs 6 wks later, consider trial off thyroxine if low requirements
      • target normal tSH
  • thyrotoxicosis → TSH receptor antibody
    • negative → thyroiditis
      • if cannot manage medically, may need beta blockers for symptomatic relief
    • positive → Graves' disease
      • thionamides (carbimazole, PTU) - used for treatment of immunotherapy related Graves' disease
      • rarely consider TRAb-negative Graves' disease if prolonged thyrotoxicosis, hypervascular goitre (thrill/bruit on examination) or ophthalmopathy (look for disproportionate fT3 vs. fT4, thyroid scintigraphy/US hypervascularity)
  • Generally no role for high dose steroids except possibly in severe thyrotoxicosis
    • used for reduced converstion of T4 to T3 and ophthalmopathy (for antiinflammatory effect)

IRAE: Pancreas related IRAEs

General faetures

  • Autoimmune diabetes is rare - 1-2% incidence
  • Risk factors - Male, older age
    • Anti PD1 >>> Anti-PDL1 > Anti-CTLA4
  • Differentials
    • T2Dm, glucocorticoid-induced hyperglycaemia, pancreatogenic diabetes

Management

  • Hyperglycaemia → Test for T1DM antibodies (anti-GAD, Anti-IR2)
    • not frequently negative → does not exclude autoimmune diabetes
    • expect an inappropriately low C-peptide level for glucose level (inadequate insulin production, IDDM)
    • ketones and blood pH - tend to present with DKA
      • may need fluid resuscitation, etc
  • no benefit from high dose steroids - will almost certainly worsen hyperglycaemia
  • Teach how to use GlucaGen HypoKit due to similar rates of hypoglycaemia to T1DM

Prognosis

  • virtually always permanent
  • risk of DKA if insulin withdrawn

IRAE: Adrenal related IRAEs

General Features

  • Primary adrenal insufficiency very rare
    • 45 definite cases in WHO VigiBase
  • typically follows Anti-PD1/PDL1 use and has coexisting autoimmune disease

Diagnosis

  • low AM cortisol and ACTH >2fold normal
  • may need short synacthen test
  • 21-OHase antibody is sometimes positive + All patients - CT adrenals to exclude metastases
    • differs from addison's disease outside cancer setting where 21-OHase antibody positivity defines addison's disease and CT only for negative natibody to identify infiltrative disease

Management

  • Treat as other cases of primary adrenal insufficiency
    • Hydrocortisone 15-25mg split BD/TDS
    • Fludrocortisone 50-150mcg mane
    • liberal salt intake
    • self management strategies as for central hypocortisolism
  • no role for high dose corticosteroids apart from full physiological replacement in adrenal crisis
    • up to 200mg hydrocortisone a day - no need for higher

Prognosis

  • recovery not reported to date - may need indefinitely

IRAE: Parathyroid related IRAEs

General features

  • primary hypoparathyroidism exceedingly rare - 6 cases reported so far

Diagnosis

  • symptomatic hypocalcaemia - fatigue, weakness, paraesthesia, GI upset
  • serum calcium 1.25-1.63mmol/L
  • undetectable or low-normal PTH
  • activating CaSR autoantibodies in 3 cases - not available clinically
  • permanent calcium requirement in each of the 6 cases reported
  • Cosnider differentials
    • Hypocalcaemia → PO4, Mg, VitD, PTH (note CaSR Ab testing not available)

Management

  • IV/PO calcium - depends on degree of hypocalcaemia
  • Calcitriol
  • Correct hypomagnesaemia

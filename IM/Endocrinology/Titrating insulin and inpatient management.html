Titrating insulin

Background

Steps

  1. Look at fasting (pre-breakfast) BGL - increase long acting by 10% every 1-2 days to achieve target
  2. Look at prandial BGL for when elevated - adjust pre-meal insulin doses by 10% at a time
    1. Look for trends (rather than one-offs) - patients can be eating more or less on a random day
  3. Look for any hypoglycaemic events - determine if due to rapid-acting (mealtime) or long-acting insulin
    1. Dose reduce by 20% where identified
  4. Regular supplementals should be incorporated into a regular insulin regimen

Insulin stacking

  • Multiple insulin doses administered in small time frame often outside of meal time
    • insulin builds up and can lead to a hypo
  • General principle
    • ­Avoid giving short-acting insulins outside of meal-times unless patient is unwell/symptomatic, ketotic, BGL is >30 and hasn’t responded to insulin previously, select patient groups

Supplemental scale insulin

  • avoid sliding scale insulin as it doesn't improve inpatient mortality (and causes increased morbidity)
  • supplemental scales are given PRN with meals on top of regular insulin to correct and excess hyperglycaemia prior to eating

Inpatient insulin management

  • Things to avoid
    • ­Hypoglycaemia
    • ­Withholding insulin in patients who are insulin-dependent (especially T1DM/T3DM)
    • ­Missing doses of long-acting insulin prior to procedures
    • ­Out of meals extra

Insulin in dialysis patients

  • Haemodialysis
    • ­Different clearance rates of insulin
    • ­Can be affected by the timing of dialysis
  • Peritoneal Dialysis
    • ­Can have a large exposure to glucose in the dialysate
    • ­Using protaphane the night before dialysis sessions can cover spikes related to the fluid uses­
  • Patients with dialysis are also very prone to hypoglycaemia
    • ­Often benefit from a reduced insulin dosage regimen the nights prior to, and on the day of dialysis
    • ­And a different dosage regimen outside of dialysis

Insulin infusions

  • Always continue long-acting insulins
  • Consider commencing a low dose of a long-acting insulin if patients aren’t normally on insulin, as it will reduce the requirements
  • IV insulin – lasts 15 minutes, subcut insulin – lasts for hours
    • Patients are at risk of hyperglycaemia and DKA/HHS if no subcut insulin on board
  • Always run with dextrose unless directed otherwise (ie. severe DKA)
    • As this prevents hypos
    • Keeps patients safe
  • Potassium
    • Needs replacing if low normal/low prior to the infusion
    • Requires ongoing maintenance whilst patients are on an infusion

  • Protocols – 1 to 3 (4 is customised)

    • A to D in pregnant women
    • Use the DKA form for DKA, use the non-DKA form for all other insulin infusions

  • When ceasing the infusion, ensure adequate overlap with subcutaneous insulin:

    • For T1DM, cease IV insulin 2 hours after giving insulin (must have long-acting insulin on board)
    • For T1DM on an insulin pump, ensure 2-4h overlap
    • For T2DM, cease IV insulin 1-2 hours after giving rapid-acting insulin.

Perioperative insulin management

  • Almost all patients with T1DM/T3DM should have an insulin-dextrose infusion:
    • Exceptions: very minor procedures on an AM list
  • Insulin-requiring patients undergoing minor surgery may be managed with a modified subcutaneous insulin regime. Have a low threshold to start an insulin-dextrose infusion.
    • All insulin-requiring patients undergoing major surgery should have an insulin-dextrose infusion.
  • Review metformin the day prior to surgery.
    • Doesn’t require withholding for contrast.
  • DPPIV inhibitors – no good evidence for withholding pre-op.
  • Ideally stop SGLT2 inhibitors 2-3 days before surgery (5 half-lives).
    • ­They predispose to euglycaemic DKA.
  • Consider withholding GLP-1 agonists 1-2 weeks prior.
    • Cause gastroparesis

Peri-operative insulin plans -  Basal - Continue until the night prior to surgery – don’t reduce the dose unless the patient had hypos recently - ­On the day of surgery – if there is a morning dose administer without a dose reduction - Bolus - Withhold during the day if not eating - Recommence once eating and drinking normally - Mixed/co-formulation insulin - Continue usual dose the night before - Reduce morning dose by 50-65%, depending on surgery - Resume the evening of the surgery - Insulin pumps - Continue the basal insulin pre- and throughout the surgery - Considerations - ­Duration of surgery - ­Eating and drinking status - ­Patients are often drowsy post-op and unable to work their pumps well - An insulin-dextrose infusion is safe and ensures adequate insulin administration - Can set a temporary basal rate (80% of the basal) on the day of the procedure if running the pump -  Tight glucose control - 7.5 +/- 2.5mmol/L - Exceptions - Pregnancy: 5.0 +/- 1.0mmol/L - ­Emergency surgery with poor glycaemic control: 10.0 +/- 2.5mmol/L - Known hypoglycaemic unawareness: 10.0 +/- 2.5mmol/L

Post-operative insulin plan - Can cease infusions when certain criteria are met - ­Eating and drinking - ­No nausea/vomiting - ­No sign of significant ketosis - ­1-2 hours after administration of subcut insulin - Resume normal doses of insulin - Can restart metformin in the evening unless AKI is present - SGLT2 inhibitors should remain withheld for at least 48 hours

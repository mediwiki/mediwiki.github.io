General Characteristics

  1. A chronic idiopathic progressive disease of intrahepatic and/or extrahepatic bile ducts characterized by thickening of bile duct walls and narrowing of their lumens; leads to cirrhosis, portal hypertension, and liver failure
  2. There is a strong association with Ulcerative colitis (less so with Crohn disease). UC is present in 50% to 70% of patients with PSC; often the UC may dominate the clinical picture. (Note: The course of PSC is unaffected by a colectomy done for UC.) Ulcerative Colitis
    • HLA-B8 and HLA-DR3 positive
    • Associated with other autoimmune conditions (e.g. hypergammaglobulinaemia IgM)
  3. M > F (2:1), approx age ~40
  4. Associations
      • ulcerative colitis: 4% of patients with UC have PSC, 80% of patients with PSC have UC
  5. Crohn's (much less common association than UC)
  6. HIV

Clinical Features

  1. Signs and symptoms begin insidiously, initially asymptomatic
  2. Chronic cholestasis findings, including jaundice and pruritus; all patients eventually present with chronic obstructive jaundice.
  3. Other symptoms: fatigue, malaise, weight loss.
  4. Severe symptoms result in biliary obstruction and acute cholangitis are possible
  5. Eventually biliary strictures result in development of cirrhosis (hepatomegaly, portal HTN, liver failure)
    • cholestasis
    • jaundice, pruritus
    • raised bilirubin + ALP
  7. right upper quadrant pain
  8. fatigue

Diagnosis

  1. ERCP and PTC are diagnostic studies of choice—see multiple areas of bead-like stricturing and bead-like dilatations of intra- and extrahepatic ducts.

    • MRCP preferred > ERCP

    Beading of bile ducts in primary sclerosing cholangitis: Endoscopic retrograde cholangiopancreatography —Multifocal strictures with dilation and beading of the bile ducts (green overlay; beaded appearance) are visible. These findings are characteristic of primary sclerosis cholangitis.

  2. Laboratory tests show cholestatic LFTs.

    • elevated ALP, GGT bilirubin
    • Possible transaminitis if liver injury ensues
  3. pANCA positive in up to 80% of cases
  4. Liver biopsy (not done for workup) shows "onion skin" scarring and bile duct fibrosis
    • endoscopic retrograde cholangiopancreatography (ERCP) or magnetic resonance cholangiopancreatography (MRCP) are the standard diagnostic investigations, showing multiple biliary strictures giving a 'beaded' appearance
  6. p-ANCA may be positive
  7. there is a limited role for liver biopsy, which may show fibrous, obliterative cholangitis often described as 'onion skin'

Treatment

  1. There is no curative treatment other than liver transplantation.
    • Symptomatic treatment: Ursofalk and immunosuppression (E.g. tacrolimus) may reduce liver injury but doesn't stop disease progression
    • Pruritis control - cholestyramine, Rifampicin and naltrexone
    • Fat-soluble vitamin supplementaion for UC (Vitamins A D E K)
  2. When a dominant stricture causes cholestasis, ERCP with stent placement for biliary drainage and bile duct dilatation may relieve symptoms.
  3. Arrange for colonoscopy to assess for UC
  4. Use cholestyramine for symptomatic relief (to decrease pruritus).

Complications

  1. Steatorrhea and fat-soluble vitamin deficiency - no bile salts excreted into intestine means fats and fat-soluble vitamins cannot be absorbed
  2. Liver cirrhosis due to bile duct outlet blockage from the liver
  3. Cholangiocarcinoma, HCC, colorectal, pancreatic, gallbladder cancer → due to continuous inflammation of the gallbladder and bile ducts
    • cholangiocarcinoma (in 10%)
  5. increased risk of colorectal cancer

General Characteristics

  1. Obstruction of the cystic duct (not infection) induces acute inflammation of the gallbladder wall.
  2. Chronic cholecystitis may develop with recurrent bouts of acute cholecystitis.
  3. Ten percent of patients with gallstones develop acute cholecystitis.

Pathophysiology

  • Obstructuve cholelithiasis (~90%) → passage of gallstone into cystic duct → cystic duct obstruction
    • (STERILE) → inflammatory response triggered → gallbladder distention and inflammation
    • Secondary bacterial infection may be present (E. coli, Klebsiella, Enterobacter, Enterococcus most common)

Clinical Features

  1. Symptoms
    • Pain is always present and is located in RUQ or epigastrium; it may radiate to the right shoulder or scapula.
    • Nausea and vomiting, anorexia
    • Dyspepsia, fat intolerance, biliary colic
  2. Signs
    • RUQ tenderness (>6h, more than biliary colic), rebound tenderness in RUQ, guarding, increases over 1-2 days
    • Murphy sign is pathognomonic—inspiratory arrest during deep palpation of the RUQ; not present in many cases
    • Fever, Malaise, anorexia
    • Hypoactive bowel sounds
    • Low-grade fever, leukocytosis
    • Usually won't get jaundice as only the cystic duct is blocked, not the CBD

Diagnosis

  1. RUQ ultrasound is the test of choice.

    • High sensitivity and specificity

    Acute cholecystitis with sludge: Transabdominal ultrasound (right upper quadrant; longitudinal plane) — The anterior wall of the gallbladder (red overlay) has three distinct layers of varying echogenicity. This includes an inner and outer hyperechogenicity (red overlay) that indicates mural inflammation and central hypoechogenicity that indicates mural edema. These features are characteristic of acute cholecystitis. The three layers are not visible in the posterior wall of the gallbladder (green overlay), as it is the tangential section that is seen here. Biliary sludge is visible within the gallbladder as the moderately hyperechoic semi-fluid structure (yellow overlay).

    Acute calculous cholecystitis: Ultrasound of the gallbladder (longitudinal, right paramedian section) —The hyperechoic structure (yellow overlay) within the gallbladder (G) is a large gallstone. Gallstones reflect ultrasonic waves, which causes them to appear hyperechoic. As the ultrasound waves are blocked from further transmission, an anechoic area, known as the posterior acoustic shadow (hatched green overlay), just beyond the gallstone is visible. The gallbladder wall transmits the sound waves, creating a hyperechoic area beyond it (green overlay). Biliary sludge appears mildly hyperechoic (hatched yellow overlay) without a posterior acoustic shadow. The gallbladder wall appears thickened and has three distinct layers of varying echogenicity (hyperechoic/hypoechoic/hyperechoic corresponding to dark red/light red/dark red overlays), indicating gall bladder edema (acute cholecystitis).

    • Findings include thickened gallbladder wall, pericholecystic fluid, edema, distended gallbladder, and presence of stone(s).
    • CT scan is as accurate as ultrasound but is more sensitive in identifying complications of acute cholecystitis (e.g., perforation, abscess, pancreatitis).
    • Radionuclide scan (hepatoiminodiacetic acid
    • Used when ultrasound is inconclusive. Its sensitivity and specificity parallel that of ultrasound. If HIDA scan is normal, acute cholecystitis can be ruled out.
    • A positive HIDA scan means the gallbladder is not visualized.
    • If gallbladder is not visualized 4 hours after injection, diagnosis of acute cholecystitis is confirmed.

  2. Laboratory studies (signs of active inflammation)

    • FBC → leukocytosis; CRP → elevated
    • Blood cultures and bile cultures should be obtained where possible
    • Rule-out tests
      • LFTs → expect mild transaminitis,
        • signs of cholestasis (raised bilirubin, GGT, ALP) are uncommon and can indicate a cholestatic disorder (e.g. cholangitis)
      • Lipase, amylase may be slightly elevated (>3x ULN indicates pancreatitis)

Treatment

  1. Patient should be admitted. Conservative measures include hydration with IV fluids, bowel rest (NPO), IV antibiotics, analgesics, correction of electrolyte abnormalities.
  2. Surgery—Laparascopic cholecystectomy is indicated in most patients with symptomatic gallstones.
    1. Early cholecystectomy is preferred (first 24 to 48 hours). Recurrence rate with nonsurgical treatment is as high as 70%. In most patients, early cholecystectomy is preferred.
    2. Within 6 weeks post-discharge for symptomatic gallstones
    3. Within 3 weeks post-discharge for acute pancreatitis due to gallstones
  3. In critically ill patients with high surgical risk, percutaneous cholecystostomy tube placement for drainage of the gallbladder is an alternative to immediate surgery.

Complications

  • Gallbladder wall perforation is a rare complication of acute cholecystitis and presents on ultrasound with a 'sonographic hole' or increased fat echogenicity in the wall of the gallbladder. Pneumobilia and gallstone ileus: X-ray abdomen (AP view; erect position) —The extrahepatic biliary tree appears radiolucent and dilated (hatched green overlay), indicating air within the biliary tree (pneumobilia). This feature is strongly suggestive of a biliary-enteric fistula. The proximal colon appears dilated (green overlay) with multiple air-fluid levels visible, likely indicating a distal small bowel obstruction. This is the characteristic appearance of gallstone ileus, which is likely caused by a large gallstone entering the colon through the biliary-enteric fistula and becoming impacted at the distal ileum.

Intermediate

  • Solid Pseudopapillary Neoplasm (Franz Tumour)

The investigations to be performed are driven, at first, by the mode of presentation – Most cases of resectable pancreatic carcinoma present with painless jaundice – DDx of a solid lesion in the head of the pancreas causing jaundice: – Adenocarcinoma Pancreas, gallstone, auto- immune pancreatitis, cholangiocarcinoma, ampullary cancer, post pancreatitis stricture

History: – Pain, Nausea, Weight loss, Family history of cancer, new or worsening diabetes, MEN1 syndrome – Symptoms are related to location of the tumour • Jaundice for cancer in the head • Back pain for cancer in the body or tail – Physical Examination: – Jaundice, Abdominal Mass, Palpable Gallbladder,Gastric outlet obstruction, Jaundice, Cervical Lymph nodes, Sr Mary Joseph Nodule, Ascites

Investigations – Labs: – FBC, EUC, LFTs, Coags, Amylase/Lipase, – Tumour Markers: Ca 19.9, CEA, Chromogranin A – IgG4 (autoimmune pancreatitis) – Imaging: – US rule out GS, check for duct dilation – Contrast CT scan chest and abdomen: look for mass, look for ‘double duct sign’ lymph nodes and metastases – MRCP: Defines point of obstruction the best

– ERCP: Only after discussion with surgeon…. • Can do brushings to confirm diagnosis • If palliative then metallic stent, if resectable then plastic (cheaper) is ok – EUS and Biopsy: Most helpful in the assessment of enlarged nodes, and for tissue diagnosis (if required) – PET: not usually, can be useful as a ‘deal-breaker’ – Diagnostic laparoscopy: for some operative candidates prior to committing to Whipple’s

Investigation of a solid lesion without obstruction – Usually picked up on CT for ‘other’ indication, or back pain (bad sign) – Imaging: – Fine cut 3 phase CT: Neuroendocrine tend to be bright on arterial phase, adenocarcinoma poorly defined. May show solid rim with necrotic center. – MRI with contrast – EUS +/- Biopsy – Labs: – LFTs, Amylase/Lipase, Ca19.9, CEA, Chromogranin A, IgG4 – PET: – not usually – (Nb false positives common in pancreas but can be useful for metastases)

Resectibility

– Most resectable cancers occur in the head and uncinate process – They present with jaundice while they are still small – Cancers presenting with gastric outlet obstruction, pain and weight loss are usually too advanced for resection – Patients with metastatic disease or in poor health are considered unresectable

Local resectibility – A resectable cancer is defined by the surgeons ability to remove all cancerous tissue. There are various definitions. – Resectable: Cancers away from vascular structures • cancers that touch the portal or superior mesenteric vein are resectable (with a vein resection) – Unresectable: tumour surrounding an artery – It is sometimes reasonable to remove an adjacent involved organ such as the colon – There are a group of patients called ‘borderline resectable

Borderline Resectable Pancreatic Adenocarcinomas – Tumor abutment of the SMA not to exceed <180 Degrees of the circumference of the vessel wall. – Segmental tumor involvement of the hepatic artery without extension into the celiac axis. – Venous involvement of the SMV/portal vein demonstrating tumor abutment with or without impingement and narrowing of the lumen. – Short segment venous occlusion resulting from either tumor thrombus or encasement but with suitable vessel proximal and distal to the area of vessel involvement. A reasonable definition would be a ‘resectable’ tumour with a high chance of positive margins. – Manage with Neo-adjuvant chemotherapy and operate on the ‘winners’

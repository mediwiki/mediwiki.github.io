Ascites

Background

Causes of ascites most commonly:

  • portal hypertension secondary to
    • Liver cirrhosis (80%)
    • Alcoholic hepatitis
    • Acute (fulminant) liver failure
    • Heart failure
    • Constrictive pericarditis
  • Hypoalbuminemia
    • Nephrotic syndrome
    • Protein-losing enteropathy
    • Severe malnutrition
  • Malignancy-related ascites
    • Ovarian cancer
    • Mesothelioma

Pathology

Clinical features

  • Abdominal discomfort, weight gain
    • progressive painless distension
  • Dyspnoea
  • Spontaneous bacterial peritonitis

Diagnostic approach

  1. Paracentesis
    • Initial tests:
      • Appearance (clear, bloody, cloudy, milky)
      • Serum-to-ascites albumin gradient (SAAH)
      • Cell count and differential
      • Total protein
      • Cytology
    • Other tests
      • Culture
      • Glucose concentration
      • LDH
      • Gram stain

Diagnostic criteria

Grading

International Ascites Club [47]:    - Grade 1 – Mild ascites detectable only by ultrasound examination  - Grade 2 – Moderate ascites manifested by moderate symmetrical distension of the abdomen  - Grade 3 – Large or gross ascites with marked abdominal distension

Investigations

Management

  • Peritoneal carcinomatosis
    • Therapeuric paracetesis
  • Malignancy-related ascites from advanced ovarian cancer
    • Surgical debulking and chemotherapy
  • Portal hypertension
    • Diuretics
    • Dietary sodium restriction

Prognosis

Malignancy-related ascites

Common mechanisms

  1. Peritoneal carcinomatosis → blockage of lymphatic draining channels → fluid accumulation and increased vascular permeability
    • Ovarian cancer
    • Bladder cancer
    • Peritoneal mesothelioma
  2. Massive liver metastases cauising portal hypertension
  3. Peritoneal carcinomatosis plus massive liver metastases
    • Colonic, gastric, breast, pancreatic, and lung cancers
  4. Hepatocellular carcinoma plus cirrhosis
  5. Chylous ascites due to malignancy
    • usually lymphoma
  6. Budd-Chiari syndrome due to malignancy occluding the hepatic veins

Prognosis

  • median survival after malignancy-related ascites is 1-4 months
    • non-ovarian cancer - < 3 months

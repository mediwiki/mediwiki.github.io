Epidemiology

  • Leading cause of cancer death around the world
    • almost equal to sum of next 4 causes of cancer added together
  • smoking is the biggest risk factor - 85% LCa occurs in smokers
    • increases with pack year hx
    • 15-30x increase risk of lung cancer compared to nonsmokers
    • quitting NEVER normalises risk of lung cancer
  • more than half are metastatic at diagnosis (55%)
    • 22% will have locally advanced disease (lymph node, mediastinum, supraclavicular spread)
    • 15% have immediately resectable
  • Staging is the MOST IMPORTANT predictor of lung cancer
    • localised - 55% 5-year survival
    • regional - 28%
    • distant - 4%
  • Distribution
    • NSCLC - 88%
      • filters in cigarettes → smaller particles reaching the lung → lands more peripherally (adenocarcinomas) rather than centrally
      • adenocarcinoma is most common subtype of lung cancer
    • SCLC - 12%

Diagnosis

  • IIIA (tumor in mediastinum, locally advanced but curable) → multimodality - involves systemic chemotherapy and local therapy (surgical/radiation)
  • IIIB - contralateral mediastinum, or supraclavicular nodes → not amenable to surgical intervention, but potentially curative
  • IV - metastasized, in blood stream → not considered curative - systemic therapies (chemo, immuno, targeted)

SCLC - aggressive tumor; metastsizes to mediastinum early - no real consideration of surgical resection - consider possibility of chemotherapy or radiation (if can fit in radiation port)

Initial workup - should be getting - abdominal imaging - bone imaging (PET/CT or bone scan) - brain imaging (MRI or CT) WITH contrast - Chest CT with IV contrast - metastatic sites - liver, adrenals → CT chest with IV contrast - bone → bone scan or PET-CT (preferred) - brain → MRI with GAD (CT head +/- contrast reasonable alternative) - mediastinal staging - can change our stage and approach to therapy - mediastinoscopy - cut made above sternum then use scope - endobronchial ultrasound - bronchoscopy w/ needles for biopsy - surgical LN dissection - Diagnosis - tissue is the issue - diagnosis - genomic analysis - mutations - immunotherapy assessment - PDL1 - clinical trial eligibility - Caveats - core over FNA (more invasive) - non-bone over bone biopsy → Bone biopsy needs to be decalcified; acid can denature some of the DNA, can affect genomic analysis.

Clinical features

Paraneoplastic snydoms - Hyponatremia (SIADH) → SCLC - ADH/aVP production - Cushings syndrome → SCLC - ACTH production → muscle weakness, hyperglycaemia, hypokalemia - infection usually causes death - Lambert Eaton → SCLC - anti-VGCC → fatigue, large muscle weakness - Hypercalcaemia → SCC - altered mental status, ataxia - MG → Thymoma - anti-AChR → fatiguability weakness of voluntary muscles - paraneoplastic cerebellar degeneration - anti-Hu, anti-Yo → ataxia, diplopia, dysphagia

Management

  • Surgical resection
    • anatomic resection is gold standard - take the lesion with its lymphatic drainage (i.e. lobectomy)
    • wedge resection/segmentectomy - not as good as lobectomy
  • for non-surgical candidates
    • radiation
      • can be used in patients with poor operative risk with localised lung cancer
      • stage III cancer w/ mediastinal spread as part of multimodality strategy
      • palliative therapy for brain mets or SVC syndrome (focal areas)
      • NOT for whole lung irradiation for patient with multiple lung lesions throughout a lung - cannot safely irradiate a whole lung
    • ablation - cryoablation, RF ablation
  • Chemotherapy alone - 20-30% response rate w/ median survival 8-12mo
  • EGFR inhibitor (for erlotinib) - 60-80% response rate with 2-3 year median survival
    • EGFR, ALK, BRAF, MET, HER2
    • ROS1, RET, NTRK
  • immunotherapy - PD-1 inhibitor
    • PD-1 and PDL-1 allows cancer cells to be recongised as self (to elude detection) but blockade allows it to be attacked
    • pembrolizumab, nivolumabm etc,

Screening

  • CT lung screening
    • 20% decrease in lung cancer mortality
    • 6.7% decrease in all cause mortality
  • Who to screen
    • Ages 55-80
    • Smokers, heavy and recent; i.e. >=30 pack year hx, current or quit within last 15 years
  • How and when
    • annual low-dose CT until

      • 15 years since smoking cessation or

      • age 80 or
      • unwilling or too ill to undergo curative resection for lung cancer
  • cost-effectiveness
    • $80K/QALY gained
  • Fleischner guidelines for detection
    • for incidental detection
    • noted high risk pt = smoker

  • if for lung cancer screening study, consider NCCN guidelines
    • these patients are all high risk

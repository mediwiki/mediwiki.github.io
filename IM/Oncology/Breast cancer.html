Breast cancer

Background

  • In the united states, 1 in 8 women develop breast cancer
    • most common cancer in women; 2nd most common cause of cancer mortality
  • Risk factors which increase risk of breast cancer are those which increase estrogen exposure
    • Increased age (increased exposure time) - median onset is 61 years old
    • BRCA1/2 mutation
      • BRCA1: 50-70% breast, 40-50% ovarian
      • BRCA2: 45-70% breast, 15-20% ovarian
        • increased male breast cancer risk
      • increased pancreatic and prostate cancer risk in BRCA1/2
      • BRCA1/2 only explains 5-10% of breast cancers - MOST breast cancers are not genetically related
    • Chest irradiation (e.g. previous non-hodgkins lymphoma in childhood)
    • Atypical lesions present (e.g. atypical hyperplasia, lobular carcinoma)
    • hereditary syndromes (e.g. FHx Breast cancer, ovarian, tubal)
    • Reproductive factors (early menarche, late menopause, first full-term pregnancy after 30y/o, nulliparity) → i.e. longer menstrual period with less pregnancy time
    • Lifestyle (obesity, BMI > 30, lack of regular exercise, increased EtOH)
      • most relevant to post-menopausal breast cancer risk - 20-30% increased risk with BMI > 30 - due to main source of estrogen in postmenopauseal being by aromatase in adipose tissue
        • estrodiol levels increase, SHBG (bound estrogen) levels gone down with increasing BMI
      • NO increased risk in premenopausal breast cancer risk
      • breast cancer risk lowers with regular activity
        • reduced weight gain, body fat → decreases estrodiol, estrone, free estradiol, increased SHBG
        • reduced insulin levels → protective (potentially)
        • increased activity (met-hours) results in reduced postmenopauseal breast cancer; however no strong association with moderate/strenuous activity (just few minutes of exercise/wk is enough)
      • EtOH intake associated with increased breast cancer
        • EtOH intake increases estrone and estradiol levels → breast cancer risk
    • Hormone replacement therapy (combined ESTROGEN and progesterone)
    • increased breast density
  • Different countries have different breast cancer screening guidelines
    • Australia's breast cancer screening guidelines as a baseline recommend everyone from age 50-74 to be screened regardless of risk
      • mammograms DO reduce mortality for women aged 40-49 in US
        • against:
          • large RCTs (100000+) show 15-25% decrease in mortality but not statistically significant
          • breast cancer less risk for aged < 50
          • older studies done before modern treatments - not applicable to present (but would therefore underestimate benefit?)
          • many more false positives in women 40-49
        • for:
          • large meta-analyses show significant 7-23% reduction in mortality → however needs a LOT of people to be screened for it to be effective
          • breast cancer is leading cause of death aged 40-49
      • MRI has not had head-on comparisons against mammography to reduce breast cancer
      • multiple randomised trials show 15-20% mortality with screening mammography use
      • recommended screening interval is 1-2 years
    • these guidelines are augmented on a patent-by-patient scale depending on presence of risk factors in the family
      • presence of risk factors means you may be indicated to be screened YEARLY from 40 years onwards
    • Risk factors for increased BCa screening as per the Australian guidelines include
      • Moderately increased risk
        • 1x 1st degree BCa < 50Y
        • 2x ist degree same side BCa
        • 2x 2nd degree same side relatives, with 1x < 50
      • Potentially higher risk
        • increased risk of ovarian cancer due to other factors
        • 2x 1st degree or 2x 2nd degree same side relatives with breast or ovarian cancer WITH
          • additional relatives with BCa/OCa
          • BCa < 40 years
          • bilateral BCa
          • either relative having both BCa and OCa
          • Ashkenazi Jewish background
          • Male BCa
        • 1st degree or 2nd degree same side BCa < 45 with a 1st/2nd degree same side sarcoma
        • family member with a high risk genetic mutation (E.g. BRCA1/2)

False positive mammograms

  • ~ 5% mammograms are false positives → 20-50% cumulative risk over 10 years
    • more common in women < 50y
    • main issue is population and healthcare economics POV
  • overdiagnosis results in overtreatment
    • 5-50% of treated breast cancer detected on screen may be overdiagnosis
    • seen in DCIS and small invasive cancers
    • increasingly sensitive radiologic techniques may result in identification of problems which do not have clinical relevance!
  • DCIS
    • 20% of memmographically detected lesions
    • however natural history of DCIS is unclear
      • 14-40% estimated to recur if untreated - but largest study (Eusebi 1994) is with 80 people; there is no evidence to see what happens to untreated DCIS
      • cannot reliably identify those that recur → everyone gets treated with mastectomy/lumpectomy +/- RCT +/- tamoxifen
      • is DCIS even really cancer? e.g. cervical carcinoma in situ now called CIN III and treatment has changed - cells look similar to invasive disease but basement membrane not involved
  • LCIS (lobular)
    • increased risk of bilateral breast cancer without predictable location → does not need surgical excision
    • manage with observation or tamoxifen (NOT bilateral mastectomy)
    • increases likelihood of developing future breast cancer with incidence of ~ 1% per year
  • Breast density
    • hard to see breast cancer in a dense breast - as breast cancers appear as white/dense
    • associated with increased risk of breast cancer
      • NOT actually related to being unable to see the breast cancer
      • dense tissue tends to have a higher risk of breast cancer even when controlled for this
    • ultrasound best for targeted areas but NOT whole breast
    • MRI has high rate of false positives for these women
      • more sensitive especially in younger women, but does NOT replace mammograms
      • many more false positives than mammography
      • not practical as a screening tool - takes 45 minutes to do, requires more human resources to comment on the images
      • recommended for
        • BRCA1/2, Li-Fraumeni (p53), Cowdens (pTEN)
        • lifetime risk of >20-25%
        • radiation to chest (e.g. for Hodgkin's) between ages 10-30
    • consider breast tomosynthesis (3D mammogram) - XR machine moves to take multiple images of the breast
      • 3D tomosynthesis vs. 2D → no RCTs to compare - but reduced recall rate and increased cancer detection rate

Breast cancer primary factors in prognostication

Prognostic factors

Good prognostic factor Desc
HER2 negativity prognostically beneficial if NOT metastatic
ER/PR positivity (Hormone receptor positive) Targeted treatments to hormone positive cancers. This includes Selective Estrogen Receptor Modulators (SERMs) such as tamoxifen, and aromatase inhibitor (e.g. letrozole)
Smaller tumour size .
Lower grade tumour
Negative lymph nodes

Classifications of breast cancer

  1. ER positive and HER2 negative (Luminal A) - hormonal therapy +/- chemo
  2. ER positive and HER2 positive (Luminal B) - hormonal therapy +/- trastuzumab + chemo
  3. ER negative and HER2 positive (HER2 enriched) - chemo + trastuzumab
  4. Triple negative (Basaloid) - no targeted therapy (just chemo)

Cancer genetics

  • Management of breast cancer changes based on cancer genetics
  • this also offers prognostic information (alongside staging)
  • Things we consider are
    • HER2 positivity → HER2 (human epidermal growth receptor 2) positivity is BAD if it is a tumour with a curative intent. If it is advanced/noncurative, it has better prognostication as there are HER2 monoclonal antibodies available to us
    • Hormone receptor positivity → ER/PR positive is GOOD as we have targeted hormonal therapies available
  • BRCA1/2 mutation risk greater if
    • age < 50 at diagnosis
    • bilateral breast cancer
    • male breast cancer
    • triple negative breast cancer < 60
    • test person with cancer first
  • multigene/expanded panel testing
    • CDH1, ATM, p53, PALB2, CHK2, pTEN, NBN, NF1, STK11, etc. → lifetime risk nowhere near BRCA1/2
    • clinical recommendations unclear
  • OncotypeDX - 21 genes to estimate recurrence risk - good at identifying low, intermediate and high risk DISTANT recurrence (i.e. not within breast)
    • specifically for ER positive tumours - node negative and 1-3 positive lymph nodes
      • node negative - low score indicate < 1-5% recurrence → no need for chemotherapy
      • TAILORx study - indicates for node negative ER+, low score, combined chemo therapy does not improve survival over hormonal therapy alone
    • Proliferation - Ki67, STK15, Survivin, CCNB1 (cyclin B1), MYBL2
    • invasion - MMP11, CTSL2
    • HER2 - HER2, GRB7
    • GSTM1
    • CD68
    • BAG1
    • Estrogen - ER, PGR, BCL2, CUBE2
    • Reference - ACTB, BAPDH, RPLPO, GUS, TFRC

HER2/neu and trastuzumab

  • HER2/neu (c-erbB2) protooncogene - 185kd transmembrane factor receptor
    • 20-25% breast cancer overexpress HER2
      • IHC +2 or FISH ratio > 2.0
  • trastuzumab - monoclonal ab against HER2
    • can cause congestive HF → never administered with anthracycline
  • HER2+ have WORSE prognosis if not treated with trastuzumab
    • 50% reduction in recurrence (Perez 2005)
  • trastuzumab binds to extracellular HER2
  • pertuzumab prevents coreceptor binding of HER2 and HER1/2/3/4
  • Lapatinib binds TKI (intracellular HER2)

### Adjuvant hormonal therapy - 1st targeted therapy - reduced breast cancer mortality by 33%, contralateral breast cancver by 50% - 5 year tx for stage I, 10y tx for node positive cancers - tamixofen - used in pre and postmenopausal - raloxifen can be used as primary prevention but not in adjuvant setting - AIs - preferred for postmenopausal women - ovarian suppression for high risk women

Staging

Diagnosis

  • Diagnosis of breast cancer in Australia is done with the 'triple test'
    1. Clinical History
    2. Imaging - mammography or ultrasound
    3. FNA biopsy

Management of breast cancers

Stage 0 - Ductal Carcinoma in Situ

  • Background
    • Very early stage cancer
  • Clinical features
    • Rarely presents with palpable mass or Paget's disease of the breast
  • Investigations
    • No need for further imaging
  • Therapeutic aim - curative
    • Eradicate area and reduce local recurrence rates
  • Management options
    1. Surgical local resection
      • Lumpectomy (Wide excision) first line
      • Mastectomy considered if primary cannot be removed by wide excision
    2. Adjuvant radiotherapy - improves disease free survival but not BCa specific mortality
    3. Adjuvant hormonal therapy (if ER positive)
      • Pre-menopausal: Tamoxifen
      • Post-menopausal: Tamoxifen/raloxifene or aromatase inhibitor (e.g. anastrazole)
  • Follow-up
    • 6-12 monthly for 5 years
    • 100% 5-year survival

Stage I - II - Invasive breast cancer

  • Investigations
    • Not for PET/CT/Bone scan
    • Not for tumour markers (CA15-3, CA27-29)
  • Therapeutic aim - curative
  • Management options: Surgical and adjuvant chemo/radiotherapy
    1. Surgery - primary
      1. Conservative approach - Wide excision and irradiation
        • Indicated if <= 5cm, no skin involvement and clear margins achieved
      2. Mastectomy +/- Neoadjuvant chemo/endocrine therapy
        • if lumpectomy cannot be satisfactorily achieved OR
        • Inflammatory cancers OR
        • Skin involvement (e.g. Peau d'Orange) OR
        • BRCA1/2 mutations OR
        • Strong family history OR
        • if irradiation is contraindicated (needed for conservative approach)
    2. Surgery - sentinel LN biopsy and axillary dissection
      • If clinically positive lymph nodes → axillary dissection is RECOMMENDED
      • If sentinel lymph node cannot be identified → axillary dissection is RECOMMENDED
      • If sentinel lymph nodes identified AND
        • <= 2 involved → NO NEED for dissection

        • =3 involved → axillary dissection is RECOMMENDED

      • Note see ../../..//IM/Oncology/Breast%20cancer.md../../..//IM/Oncology/Breast%20cancer.mdAxillary dissection complications
    3. Adjuvant Irradiation
      • typically performed on whole breast
      • Recommended post-mastectomy if >= 5cm, positive margins, skin involvement, inflammatory breast cancer, or >=4 lymph nodes
      • reduces risk of local recurrence, metastases and improves overall survival
      • in a node negative setting, the addition of radiation to lumpectomy makes no difference on survival - just reduces risk of local recurrence
    4. Adjuvant hormonal therapy
      • Indicated if >= 70 years old, <2cm, and no lymph node involvement
      • If ER positive - can accept just wide excision and antiestrogen therapy
      • can be used with whole breast irradiation
        • reduces local recurrence
        • no effect on distant mets, breast cancer-specific survival or overall survival
      • Adjuvant hormonal therapy for ER/PR positive
        • Ovarian function suppression (OFS) if premenopauseal
      • Adjuvant therapy for HER2 positivity

Stage III - Locally invasive, nil metastases

  • Investigations
    • PET/Bone scan and whole body CT indicated (Staging)
    • Consider multigene assay (21 gene recurrence assay) to determine if adjuvant chemotherapy will be of benefit
  • Therapeutic aim - potentially curable
  • Management options
    • Neoadjuvant antiestrogen or chemotherapy
    • Surgery
    • Adjuvant radiotherapy
  • Prognosis and followup
    • Annual mammograms
    • Ongoing tamoxifen use - ensure yearly gynaecological exams, evaluate for abnormal bleeding
      • avoid CTP2D6 inhibition (e.g. buproprion, fluoxetine)
      • review bone density every 2 years and consider antiresorptive therapy

Stage IV - Metastatic

  • Investigations
    • Biopsy of the primary site
    • Biopsy of the upstaging secondary/metastatic site
  • Therapeutic aim - symptom control and QoL
  • Management
    • Hormonal therapy first line for ER +ve (unless there is a visceral crisis)
      • AI +/-0 palbociclib for postmenopausal
      • tamoxifen + ovarian suppression for premenopausal
    • aromatase inhibitor - anastrozole, letrozole, axemestane
    • fulvestrant - pure ER antagonist
    • targeted therapy
      • palbociclib, ribociclib, abemaciclib - oral CDK 4/6 inhibitor → increased progression free survival, but not oral survival (but could mean delaying starting chemo)
      • everolimus - oral mTOR inhibitor
      • immunotherapy - unclear how to maximise response
    • chemotherapy has modest improvements in overall survival - main improvement is symptom control and palliation
      • single agent chemo is less toxic than combined

Complications

Axillary dissection complications

  • Complications include
    • Lymphoedema
    • Sensory loss
    • Shoulder abduction defects
  • only done when palpable lymph nodes at diagnosis

Evidence on breast cancer treatment - tamoxifen, raloxifen, aromatase inhibitors - 40-60% reduction in breast cancer risk - only prevents ER+ breast cancer - no survival benefit seen - no benefit in primary prevention cohort - raloxifene and AIs only used after menopause - chemoprevention is not used due to absolute risk of breast cancer low

Bone metastases

  • 70-75% of women with metastatic breast cancer will develop bone mets
  • complications
    • pain
    • hypercalcaemia
    • pathological fractures
    • cord/nerve compressions
  • routine bisphosphonates for bone mets have significantly improved QOL for metastatic patients

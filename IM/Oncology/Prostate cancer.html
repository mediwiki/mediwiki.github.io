Prostate cancer

Background

  • most common male cancer (1 in 7), 2nd most common cause of death in US
  • reducing incidence recently - due to reduced PCA testing
  • risk factors
    • age
    • fhx
    • race - africam aemerican
    • genetic disoposition - BRCA, lynch
  • Spectrum of disease
    • low risk, organ confined - will never kill
    • castration resistant prostate cancer - non treatable
    • aim is to balance treatment with risk of death

Screening

  • rationale - to early identify
  • tests: PSA and DRE
    • PLCO trial (150000 USA)
      • no difference, but 50% of patients had contamination ??
    • european study
      • 21% of mortality
    • goteburg
      • 44% reduction in mortality
  • why not screening
    • risks of biopsy - infection, pain, anxiety
    • risk of overdiagnosis and treatment toxicity - not all needs to be treated
      • sexual dysfunction, urinary incontinence
  • screening guidelines in AUS

Grading and staging

  • Gleason score (GS)
    • most prominent pattern + next most prominent pattern
  • PSA
    • <10 - low risk
    • 10-20 - good risk

    • 20 - high risk

  • TNM staging - staging of bones and nodes (pelvic imaging)

Treatment of localised disease

  • Early
    • Radical prostatectomy
      • reserved for earlier gleason score - 15 year PSCM
        • 6 - <1%
        • 7 - 8%
        • 8-10 - 49% → why put them through surgery when half will die in 15 years
    • Active surveillance - for low risk prostate cancer
      • no initial treatment, but treat when clinically significant (monitor PSA, DRE, repeat biopsy)
      • treat to cure at sign of aggressive disease
      • cons - risks anxiety and progression to noncurative state
    • Watchful waiting - considered if life expectancy < 10 years; symptomatic management only
    • Radiation therapy +/- ADT
  • intermediate risk
    • if not surgical, for radiation therapy
    • if + 6 months hormonal therapy, increased cure rate, survival or delayed representation
      • may not be great for low risk patients
      • biggest benefit in intermediate and high risk patients
      • 36 months of hormonal therapy better than 6 months in high risk disease

ProtecT trial - which is better? - surgery, radiotherapy +/- ADT, active monitoring has similar mortality in low risk group at 10 years - 25% of men in AM group went to receive radical therapy within 3 years; 50% by 10 years

  • Hormonal therapy
    • lupron - hit the pituitary
    • antiandrogens (e.g. abiraterone) stop adrenal production of androgens

Treatment of rising PSA with no mets

  • scans do not show cancer, but PSA continues to rise after local therapy
    • expect post-protactectomy > 0.2
    • post-radiation nadir + 2 → concern of recurrence
  • approach
    • expect PCA rise to occur 2 years after radical prostatectomy
    • 3 years after PSA rise before mets develop
    • 7 years after mets before patient passes from metastatic prostate cancer
    • therefore could consider just doing nothing
  • Early initiation of hormonal therapy
    • can consider intermittent hormoanl therapy to allow testosterone holidays
    • TOAD trial showed trend towards increased overall survival with earlier use

Treatment of metastatic prostate cancer

  • high volume bone, liver or lung mets have worse prognosis with node only of <4 bone mets
  • deeper PSA nadir in response to hormone blockade improves survival
  • hormonal therapy
    • overall survival better for continuous ADT (vs. intermittent)
    • adding docetaxel (6 cycles) improved survival in high volume mets
  • disease will become castration reistant over time

Management of castration resistant disease

  • second line androgen blockade
    • abiraterone - cyp17 in adrenal gland, testis and tumor
    • enzalutamide
  • bone targeted
    • radium 223 (Ca mimetic, uptake by tumor)
  • chemotherapy - docetaxel, carbazitaxel
  • immunotherapy - sipuleucel-T
  • medial survival 18-35 months from use of second line agent → not curative

Primary CNS Vasculitis (Primary Angiitis of the Central Nervous System (PACNS))

Background

Vasculitis

- Wide differential in secondary vasculitis - e.g. immunological - inflammatory - e.g. Hepatitis C - inflammation in kidney, nerves, brain - VZV, HSV → secondary CNS vasculitis and stroke - TB, IE - angioinvasive - e.g. fungi - e.g. drugs - Cocaine - stimulation of inflammatory pathologies - sympathomimetics - constriction - systemic disease - perivasculitis on biopsy appearance - inflammatory pathologies and presentations similar to CNS vasculitis - look for CTD, scleroderma, SLE, etc. - malignancy - esp lymphoproliferative disorders can cause vessel wall inflammation - primary systemic vasculitis - ANCA associated w/ brain involvement, Behcets, etc.

  • Primary angitis of the CNS (PACNS)
    • inflammation of blood vessels of brain and spinal cord without systemic vasculitis
    • RARE - 2.4 per 1,000,000 person-years
    • M:F = 2:!
    • mAge = 50

Pathology

  • Inflammation of blood vessel walls in brain and spinal cord
    • fibrinoid necrosis of the walls
      • narrowing of lumen due to thickening (especially in small vessels )→ stenosis and occulsion
      • hypercoaguability locally at wall → total occlusion from thrombosis
    • focal lesions within vessel wall → aneurysms → rupture → bleed

Clinical features

  • Insidious onset and slowly progressive
  • Diagnosis takes ~ 6 months in 75% of cases, can be delayed up to 3 years due to insidious onset
    • can be missed without high suspicion
  • Headache 70% - generalised/localised, slowly worsening, spontaneously remitting for period
    • can have headaches for 3 months, stop for 1 month, etc.
    • Thunderclap headache is NOT a characteristic of CNS vasculitis - can consider Reversible Cerebral Vascoconstriction Syndrome (RCVS)
      • imaging features would be similar to PACNS but syndrome different
  • Cognitive impairment and behavioual changes 50%
  • Stroke/TIA 50%
  • Constitutional symptoms (fever/weight loss) uncommon - <10%
    • need to consider other diagnoses
  • suspect when
    • cerebral ischemic episodes in different vascular beds separated by time with inflammatory changes in CSF
    • young changes with lack of traditional cerebrovascular RFs for stroke
    • chronic meningitis without demonstrated infectious or malignant etiology
    • subacute or chronic headache w/ cognitive dysfunction w/ aseptic meningitis history
    • combination of unexplained focal and diffuse neurological dysfunction

Clinical subsets 1. Lymphocytic PACNS - acute clinical onset - greatly raised CSF pattern - meningeal or parenchymal enhancing lesions on MRI - Abnormalities found in biopsy - Responds favourably to treatment 1. Angiographically defined PACNS (large-medium vessel) - Abnormal vessel +/- CSF and normal biopsy - Medium sized vessels involved - Need to rule out other vasculopathies (RCVS, ICAD) - Signs of inflammatory brain changes should be present - abnormal CSF or VW enhancement

Diagnostic criteria

  • PACNS diagnostic criteria (Calabrese and Mallek 1987)
    • Presence of an acquired and otherwise unexplained neurological deficit
      • with presence of either classic angiographic or histopathologic features of angiitis within the CNS
      • And no evidence of systemic vasculitis or any condition that could elicit the angiographic or pathologic features (biggest caveat - need to rule everything else out)

Investigations

- CNS lymphoma - needs 60mL of CSF! - Other vascular disorders - e.g. Fibromuscular dysplasia

  • CRP elevated ~ 30%, ESR ~ 20%
    • not usually elevated! If elevated need to consider other causes
    • Local immune response in CNS occluded/hypoperfused vessels can propagate into a systemic inflammatory response
      • CRPs can be elevated from D1 → Peaks at ~ 20 at 5-7 days and can be elevated til 3 months
      • ESR is NOT raised at D1, begins at 5-7 days to ~ 30, and can remain elevated until 3 months
  • Vasculitis screen should be NEGATIVE in PACNS
  • CSF studies
    • abnormal in 80-90% (normal in 10-20%)
    • No cells or aseptic meningitis pattern with lymphocytic pleocytosis (<50 cells), normal glucose, elevated protein (~1.5g/L) and occasional oligoclonal bands (unmatched - in CNS inflammation) and elevated IgG
      • if only oligoclonal bands - ?demyelination
    • CSF also changes in acute stroke
      • Possibly elevated protein ~1g/L
      • Normal glucose
      • 90% will NOT have CSF pleocytosis → when present, usually < 15 cells and evident on day 1 and resolves by day 7
  • MRI features
    • PACNS unlikely if MRI normal
    • MRI findings are non-specific
      • cortical and subcortical infarcts - most common, usually multiple and bilateral
      • SWI sequence - some microhaemorrhages, possibly leptomeningeal enhancement for small vessel vasculitis
      • ICH/SAH presentation
      • Tumour-like mass lesions - gadolinum enhancing possible too
  • Angiography features
    • Highly suggestive
      • Stenosis w/ normal and dilated intervening segments and arterial occlusions - rarely seen picture
      • Usually with several arteries bilaterally - if only in one artery, unlikely PACNS
      • both large and small arteries affected
    • Sensitivity of angiography - 40-90%
      • small arteries may not be captured in angiogram
    • Specificity ~ 30% → could be many mimics
  • MRA - Vessel wall imaging
    • Hi-res 3T contrast-enhanced MRI → Black-blood fat suppressed pre- and post-contrast T1 weighted images
      • perivascular enhancement seen
    • Wall enhancement associated with inflammatory cell invasion, neovascularisation (occurs in areas of atherosclerosis) and presence of vasa vasorum (terminal ICA/VA)
      • vasa vasorum enhacement does not indicate inflammation!
    • Identifies inflammation but not the cause
  • Biopsy
    • Sensitivity ~ 75%; 30-50% biopsies reveal another diagnosis (e.g. lymphoma)
    • 90% of targeted biopsies are diagnostic - if no accessible area that is clearly involved, biopsy the non-dominant temporal lobe (leptomeninges and cortex)
      • 3% rate of major complication
      • need to see intramural (NOT perivascular) inflammation
    • mostly show lymphocytic vasculitis
    • Single isolated negative biopsy does not PACNS

Management - no RCTs to guide

  • Induction therapy (Beuker et al - meta-analysis)
    • Cyclophosphamide (CYC) and high dose prednisone
      • Steroids will generally cause symptom remission, but > 50% relapse on taper if used in isolation
        • Relapse OR 2.90 with prednisone alone
      • Less common - AZA, MMF, RIX, MTX
        • RIX - for induction, considering fertility
      • Methylpred IV 1000mg x 3 days then daily prednisone
      • IV cyclophosphamide - PEXIVAS protocol
        • 15mg/kg (max 1200mg) q2weekly x 3, then q3weekly for 10 doses
      • IV Rituximab - 1g q2wkly for 2 doses
  • Maintenance agents - reduces relapse (De Boysson et al "French Cohort")
    • AZA or MMF or MTX
      • AZA 1-2mg/kg daily
      • MTX 20-25mg/wk
      • MMF 1-2g/day
    • Treat for at least 2 years then consider discontinuation of therapy
  • Prophylaxis
    • Osteoporosis
    • Prophylaxis against PJP - TMP/SMX 80mg/400mg OD
  • Monitoring
    • Serial MRI and MRA after 4-6wk and then q3-4mo during first year OR when new deficit arises
    • worsening symptoms but stable imaging:
      • repeat CSF studies and angiogram
      • consider biopsy to confirm diagnosis

Prognosis

  • Poor prognosis with higher mortality → consider CYC in these patients
    • Large vessel involvement
    • Lack of leptomeningeal enhancement → delayed diagnosis
    • older age
    • associated infarcts (rather than simple inflammation)

Cases

- Case 1 - CSF showed protein 1.1, no cells - R) frontodural and brain biopsy - vessels did not have beta amyloid angiopathy; only amyloid plaques seen - NOT CNS vasculitis - Case 2 - L) frontal biopsy - intramural inflammation and focal scattered EBV positive cells (however PCR negative) - no granulomas or multinucleated cells - CNS vasculitis diagnosed - on rituximab - Case 3 - R) frontal biopsy and meninges - Perivascular and transumural inflammation in leptomeningeal vessels w/o granulomas or multinucleated giant cells - beta mayloid negative - consistent with PACNS - Rx: Prednisone and cyclophosphamide

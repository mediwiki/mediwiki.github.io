Intracerebral Haemorhage (ICH)

Background

Pathophysiology

Hypertensive ICH

  • Most common cause affecting deep structures of the brain (e.g. basal ganglia, thalamus, pons, cerebellum)

Lobar ICH

  • Can be caused by hypertension
  • In >55 year old without hypertension, may be due to cerebral amyloid angiopathy
  • amyloid protein (similar to in A.D.) → deposition in cerebral arterioles near cortical surface → weakened arterial wall → more prone to rupture

Morality

  • Most common: Hematoma expands → Increased ICP → Death

Clinical features

  • Presents similarly to ischemic stroke
    • Headache, impaired consciousness

Diagnosis

  1. Radiological imaging

Management

  1. Prevent hematoma expansion
    1. Control blood pressure (SBP 140 - 180 mmHg)
      • AVOID Nitrates (E.g. GTN, nitroprusside) as they can raise ICP
      • SBP < 140mmHg as target can result in increased renal events
    2. Cease antiplatelets and anticoagulants
      • NO role for platelet transfusions
      • NO role for recombinant factor VII unless patients have coagulopathy
      • Reverse anticoagulants if possible
  2. Prevention of status epilepticus
    • Prophylactic antiepileptics (Valpropate, Lamotrigine) is NOT recommended

Prognosis

  • ICH score for 30 day mortality

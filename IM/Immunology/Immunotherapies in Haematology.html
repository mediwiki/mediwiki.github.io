Immunotherapies in Haematology

BPT learning points - Broad pharmacological principles and complications of immunotherapy and molecularly targeted therapy

Immunotherapies in haematological cancers

  • Advantages and use cases
    • immune responsiveness - allogeneic stem cell transplant and donor lymphocyte infusion
      • immune cells from donor can set up immune response
      • collecting donor lymphcytes and infusing in recipient can ramp up immune response and mop up outstanding malignant cells
    • malignancies are located close to sites of immune monitoring - e.g. lymph nodes, bone marrow, blood
      • immune cells are prone to attack and recognition
    • cellular origins of malignancy are related to immunity - the same as the effector cells which we use
      • can develop effector cells which do not allow escape
    • feasibility of isolating and mainpuating malignant cells
      • easy to access biologic material - bone marrow biopsies
        • low risk procedures, can provide large amounts of 'material of interest' - can be prformed before OR after immunotherapy
  • Challenges
    • malignant cells can be stimulated by the same stimulatory signals as the immune system
      • e.g inflammatory cytokines
      • difficult if effect cell provides cytokines which then stimulates malignant cells to grow
        • need to tease out helpful cytokines
    • ability of malignant cells to suppress and evade the immune system
      • monoclonal antibody use - demonstrated malignant cells suppressing and evading immune system

Classes of anti-tumour immunotherapy

  1. Cancer vaccines
    • vaccinating host to tumour associated antigens
    • T-cell response → recognise and kills cancer cells
    • to date, this has not lived up to expected goals
  2. Adoptive immunotherapy
    • active and increase T-cell numbers
    • e.g. development of CAR-T cells
      • good response in conditions they are used

  3. Immunomodulation

    • use drugs to
      • increase immune stimulation
      • overcome immune inhibition - e.g. PD-1 inhibitor and CTLA-4 inhibitors
        • changed response to previously untreatable cancers (E.g. metastatic melanoma)

  4. Hematopoietic SCT - first developed in 1950s, allowed bone marrow to repopulate with incoming cells

    • graft vs. leukemic effect
    • graft vs disease effect - better relapse
  5. Natural killer cells - becoming more topical; effective at killing tumor cells
    • advantage over T-cells as they do not set up Graft-vs-Host disease
    • CAR-NK cell may give rise to off-the-shelf immunotherapy and haem malignancies
  6. monoclonal antibodies - >. 20 years since rituximab against CD20 B-cell malignancies
    • monoclonal-toxin drugs developed since
    • T-cell engagers related
  7. Immunomodulatory drugs - e.g. thalidomide related drugs in haematological drugs (originally developed against multiple myeloma, thought to act on blood vessel growth)
  8. Bi-specific T-cell engagers
    • made of synthetic molcules (Fc receptors against a single antigen, typically CD3 on T-cells and Fc receptors against tumour associated antigen, joined by peptides and bring a T-cell close to target cell for destruction)
  9. T-cell immunotherapy - haem malignancy dramatic improvement
    • synthetic TCR to bind tumour associated antigens → activate T-cell → target tumour cell
    • promising therapy for future
  10. Immune checkpoint inhibitors
    • used in melanoma and haematological conditions
    • increase T-cell killing of tumour cells
  11. Immunotherapeutic vaccines
    • originally promising in melanoma but has fallen out of favour due to poorer efficacy

T-cell immunotherapy and CAR-T cells

Chimeric antigen receptor T-cell (CAR-T cells)

  • Synthetic TCR - recognises and eliminates cells expressing tumor associated antigens (TAAs)
    • TAAs give specificity for tumours, less off-target effects
  • initial studies
    • directed against CD19 - ALL, CLL, B-cell lymphoma
    • 2010 - regression of advanced follicular lymphoma using anti-CD19 CAR-T
    • 2013 - rapid tumour eradication and achievement of clinical regression in adult patients with refractory B-ALL
    • 2015 - relapsed/refractory CLL - ORR 57%, CR 29%, 4+ year CAR-T cell persistance with no relapse during time
    • 2015 - DLBCL NHL treated with CAR-T cell therapy with 8/15 entering complete remission
  • Limitations
    • Cytokine release syndrome
      • Neurotoxicity - Immune Effect Cell-associated neurotoxicity syndrome (ICANS)
        • Delirium, seizure, encephalopathy
        • can lead to long term morbidity/mortality
      • Management
        • IL6 and IL6R antagonists
          • Tocilizumab (anti-IL6R)
          • Siltuximab (anti-IL6)
        • Corticosteroids if refractory to IL-6 therapies
    • On-target off-tumor toxicity
      • CD19 on both malignant and non-malignant B-cells
      • B-cell aplasia and hypogammaglobulinaemia
      • Management
        • IVIg replacement
    • Downregulation of target antigens or tumour antigen heterogeneity
      • CD-19 positive B-cell tumours good at downregulating target antigen
      • Tumour relapse - common in CD19+ B-cell malignancies
      • Management
        • Use more than 1 CAR T-cells that target different antigens
        • Bispecific CAR-T cells or multiple single target CAR-T cells

Natural killer cell immunotherapy

MOA

  • Attack tumour cells
    • Missing self-recognition
      • activation normally inhibited by the interaction with MHC-I molecules
      • many cancer cells downregulate MHC-I to avoid CD8+ T-cells → opens up possibility of NK cell taking up malignant cell
    • Induced self-recognition - overexpression of ligands for NK activating receptors
  • CAR NK cells
    • potential for off-the-shelf or universal therapeutic cells
    • no Graft vs Host Disease (GvHD)
    • not yet used in practice
  • Clinical trials
    • CD19, CD22, B-cell maturation antigen (BCMA), CD33, CD7
      • BCMA on Multiple Myeloma cells
      • CD33 on AML cells
      • CD7 on T-cells
    • Phase I and II trials
      • Anti-CD19 CAR and Relase refractory CD19 cancers
      • 8/11 participants had objective response, 7 achieved complete remission
  • Limitations
    • Loss of target antigens
    • Tumour heterogeneity - some cells have target antigen, some don't
    • Suppressive tumour microenvironment - complicates treatment,

Monoclonal antibodies

MOA

  • Antibody-dependent cellular cytotoxicity (ADCC)
    • NK cell mediated
    • Monoclonal antibodies directed against TAAs

Examples

  • Rituximab - CD20
    • B-cell lymphomas, CLL, Mantle cell lymphoma
    • some autoimmune conditions
  • Ofatumumab and Obinutuzumab
    • humanised monoclonal antibodies - interact with different CD20 epitope
    • greater ADCC and antitumor effect compared to rituximab
  • Daratumumab
    • mAb against CD38 antigen
    • used in Multiple Myeloma
    • Single agent - Relapse refractory after >= 3 lines of therapy in MM
    • In combinations - RR after failure of >=1 therapy and/or ineligible for autograft
      • used w/ immunomodulatory and proteasome inhibitor combinations
  • Elotuzumab
    • anti-SLAMF7 or CS1
    • single agent use showed no objective response and stable disease in 26%
    • Combined with lenalidomide/dexamethasone showed ORR 79% and median PFS 14.9 months in randomised phase 3 study in heavily pre-treated population
    • PBS listed

Drug or toxin conjugation

Examples

  • Moxetumomab pasudotox
    • CD22 antigen
    • Pseudomonas exotoxin A
    • Not PBS listed
    • Treatment of hairy cell leukemia in trials
  • Polatuzumab vedotin
    • CD79 antigen
    • conjugated with Monomethyl Auristatin (MMAE)
    • used in R/R DLBCL NHL in medically unfit patients - PBS listed
  • Brentuximab Vedotin
    • MMAE against CD30 (HL, Reed-Sternberg cells and some AML cells)
    • PBS listed for CD30 positive systemic anaplastic large cell and R/R classical HL

Bispecific T-cell engagers

  • 2 single chain bariable fragments bind to two different agents
    • just the binding segment between CD3 and TAA (tumor asociated antigen)

Examples

  • Blinatumomab
    • CD3 (T-cell) to CD19 (B-cell)
    • investigated for other B-cell malignancies
  • BiTE (BI 835909)
    • BCMA on Multiple Myeloma
    • Fc fragments with BCMA specific fragments
  • In development
    • CD3-CD33 BiTEs for AML
    • Bispecific NK cell engagers (BiKE)

Immune checkpoint inhibitor

MOA

  • Activation of effective T-cells
    • needs two-level activation or inhibition
    • ensures proper effector T-cell functioning - immune checkpoints
  • Activation phase
    1. Binding to peptide antigen
    2. Binding B7-1 family ligand
  • Inhibition
    • Different hematopoitic meliangicies upregulating expression of PD-1 and CTLA4
    • Targeting immune checkpoint inhibitors has revolutionised cancer treatment - initially melanoma, but now for a large variety

Normal T-cell activation

  • APC presents tumor ligand/antigen to T-cell
    • T-cell receptor recongises antigen (first signal)
    • CD28 - B7.1/2 interaction → cytokine release
  • CTLA4 can be upregulated by tumour cells to prevent second signalling
    • CTLA-4 will bind to T-cell
    • this cucks the B7.1/2 from binding to CD28, instead binding to the CTLA4
    • this results in no second signal → T-cell anergy (i.e. no immune response)
  • Anti-CTLA4 and Anti-PD1 can counter-cuck
    • these antibodies will stop the cucking effect of CTLA4 or PD1
    • ipililumab (anti-CTLA4) - results in CD28/B7.1/2 binding again (second signal occurs)
    • pembrolizumab (anti-PD1) - same as above

Examples

  • Increased PD-1 signalling in Multiple Myeloma
    • Phase 1B trials of pembrolizumab (PD1i) in r/r MM
      • safe but no objective response rate yet
  • increased PDL-1 expression in classical Hodgkin lymphoma from genetic amplification of Cr9p24.1
    • Phase II clinical trials in R/R cHL
    • 82% progression free survival
    • PBS listed for R/R cHL who have undergone or not suitable for autograft
  • Nivolumab - anti-PD-1
    • R/R cHL patients - nivolumab + brentuximab (CD30) before ASCT
      • 82% Objective response rate, Overall RR 61%
    • Primary mediastinal large B-cell lymphoma
      • amplified PD-1 axis
    • Phase 1B study of prembrolizumab in R/R PBML
      • Objective RR 48%

Immunomodulatory drugs

Examples

  • Thalidomide analogues - higher immunomodulation w/ less toxicity compared to thalidomide
    • lenalidomide and pomalidomide
      • increased production of IL-10 (anti-inflammatory cytokine) and production of pro-inflammatory cytokines TNF-a, IL1b, IL-6
      • induce IL-2 and IFN-y production
      • enhance T-cell and NK-cell function
      • inhibitor Treg cell activity
    • Pomalidomide - PBS: R/R MM after failure of >=2 treatment regimens including lenalidomide and bortezomib
    • Pomalidomide, bortezomib and dexamethasone - PBS: R/R MM in patients previously treated with lenalidomide
    • Daratumumab, lanilidomide, dexamethason - PBS:
      • R/R MM after failure of at least one prior therapy
      • MM ineligible for ASCT
  • Thalidomide - originally used in Multiple Myeloma
    • inhibition of angiogenesis and TNF-a synthesis

Immunotherapeutic vaccines

Uses

  • AML in complete or partial remission
    • known intracellular tumour associated antigens existing in AML (e.g. WT1, PRTN3, MAGE)
    • vaccines not succesful in high tumor burden
    • WT1 and hTERT dendritic cell vaccines - induced cellular immune responses and associated with durable remissions
  • not clinically used frequently

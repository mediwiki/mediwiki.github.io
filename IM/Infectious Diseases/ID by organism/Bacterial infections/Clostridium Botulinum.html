Clostridium Botulinum

Background

  • Clinical syndrome - botulism
    • botulism can rarely be caused by other clostridium species (C.baratii, B.noyvii, C.butyricum)
  • Epidemiology - widespread in environment
  • Incidence - once every 2 years in Australia
  • Transmission
    • Food consumption - infant, adult
      • Infants - untreated honey
      • adults - low income countries - e.g. pickled foods and long storage
    • Inoculation - wounds, IVDU, iatrogenic
    • Bioterrorism

Pathology

  • Binds presynaptic motor neuron NMJ irreversibly - inhibits ACh release
  • 7 immunological toxin types: A-G
    • A, B, E, F - human
      • A- most severe
      • B- longest incubation period
    • C, D, E - other mammals, birds, fish
  • microbiology
    • Anaerobic GPR
    • Spore-forming - survives 100C > 5h, survives low pH
    • Bacterial - inhibited by pH < 4.5
    • Toxin - denatures >80C (not pH) - can survive in low pH
    • Organism - SSBA2 (security sensitive biological agent), toxin SSBA1 (0.5mg)

Clinical features

  • Food borne onset 12-72h post ingestion
  • infant botulism
    • most common
    • immature intestine is colonised after ingestion - can survive due to immature biome
      • the issue in children is microbiome differences, not pH
    • organism then produced neurotoxin
  • classical botulism
    • older children and adults - healthy and mature microbiome, so organism cannot survive
    • pre-formed toxin contamination (usually won't grow)
    • preceding nausea and vomiting

Investigations

  • very unlikely to grow in cultures
  • Stool sample - direct toxin, culture, PCR
  • Food - direct toxin, culture, PCR
  • Serum - direct toxin
  • Definitive diagnosis (PHLN lab criteria)
    • detection of C.botulinum neurotoxin in clinical sample (e.g. faeces) OR
    • detection of C.botulinum neurotoxin from food consumed
  • Suggestive diagnosis
    • Detection of C.botulinum in faeces from a patient with clinical features of botulism even if tests for neurotoxin are negative
  • Lab and imaging findings typically normal
  • CSF - no pleocytosis or raised protein
  • MRI - NAD
  • EMG/NCS
    • reduction in muscle action potential and M-wave amplitudes
    • Frequency-varying response to repetitive nerve stimulation.

Microbiology culturing - can only be tested in WSLHD (bioterrorism risk)

  • Cooked meat broth culture - 35C 48h AnO2
  • 80% EtOH (10 minutes) or Heat Shock (80C)
    • c.botulinum spores can withstand EtOH
  • Incubate on blood agar and clostridium selective media - 35C, 4 days AnO2
    • Usually grows after 24h - colonies
  • Identification - toxin detection
    • PCR for toxin-producing DNA
      • US CDC - single target genes toxin A-G (7 different genes)
      • Biofire biothreat panel - target toxin A
    • Identification of toxin - mice bioassay
      • 0.5mL of specimen/antitoxin (A, B, E) and 0.5mL or specimen (w/o antitoxin) injected in laboratory mice and observed over 4 day period for neurotoxin (usually positive in 2h)

Management

Antitoxin

  • Preparations
    • A-G - hepatotoxic
    • Human derived botulism immunoglobin A/B - used in kids
  • Most effective early
    • Adults within 72h
    • Children up to 7d
  • Neutralising circulating toxins only - once toxin binds to nerve, it is irreversible
    • half-life toxin 230-260 minutes in mice - after 72h in adults, all toxins practically gone so nothing left for antitoxin to bind
    • only resolves on neurological recovery
  • No reversal of neurological symptoms
  • Adminstration
    • consultation with public health
    • anaphylaxis risk - 1-2%

Active infection

  • Benzylpenicillin - 95%
  • Metronidazole 100% coverage
  • Augmentin IV - 99% coverage

Prognosis

  • Mortality < 5-8% - highest toxin A
  • Mean period of ventilator support without antitoxin
    • 58 days for type A
    • 26 days for type B
  • Median hospital stay
    • Antitoxin <24h - 10 days
    • Antitoxin >24h - 41 days
    • Not treated - 56 days

Infectious arthritis

Background

  • infection of one or more joints caused by bacteria, viruses, fungi and parasites
  • Acute bacterial arthritis
    • native joint incidence: 2-10/100,000 per year (increased in Rheumatoid arthritis positive patients)
    • via:
      • hematogenous dissemination
      • direct joint inoculation
      • contiguous focus
  • Viral arthritis
    • sporadic infection or community outbreaks (small - e.g. parvovirus B19, explosive - e.g. chikungunya virus)
    • typically acute, polyarticular with other systemic viral symptoms
    • can result in prolonged or chronic arthritis after infection

Pathology

Acute bacterial arthritis

  • Gram positive organisms predominate - Staphylococcus aureus and streptococcus spp.
  • gram negative bacilli in 5-20% - neonates, elderly, IVDU, immunocompromised
  • Gonococcal arthritis rates decreased - see Reiter's syndrome

Viral arthritis

  • Parvovirus B19
  • Rubella (can follow immunisation)
  • hepatitis B, C, alphaviruses (including chikungunya virus)
  • Ross river virus

Clinical features

  • Acute monoarticular or polyarticular arthritis → consider bacterial arthritis

Diagnostic criteria

Investigations

  • Synovial fluid
    • leukocyte count
    • differential
    • examination (for crystals - e.g. gout and pseudogout)
    • gram stain
    • culture
  • Imaging
    • joint XR (plain film)
    • CT or MRI for prolonged or atypical symptoms

Management

  • Bacterial arthritis is a surgical emergency
  • Start empirical antibiotic therapy - IV for 2-4 weeks followed by oral transition
  • joint drainage - repeat aspiration, arthroscopy, arthrotomy

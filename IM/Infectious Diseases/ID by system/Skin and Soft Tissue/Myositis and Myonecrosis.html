Myositis

Background

  • inflammatory, generally necrotising process
  • Aetiology
    • hematogenous seeding with bacterial invasion

  • Epidemiology
    • Pyomyositis - across age spectrum in temperate regions
      • Warm climates - predominantly seen in children
    • Clostridial myonecrosis - complicates penetrating trauma, especially in resource limited settings

Pathology

  • Staphylococcus aureus classic agent
  • influenza and dengue virus infections can lead to acute generalised muscle inflammation - can lead to significant muscle injury and rhabdomyolysis

Clinical features

  • Focal progressive pain is non-specific

Diagnostic criteria

Investigations

  • Blood cultures
  • CT/MRI
    • Gas production in setting of rapidly progressive illness is surgical emergency - requires exploration and debridement
  • Percutaneous drainage based on findings of CT imaging/MRI
  • Toxic shock possible

Management

  • drainage of any local sources
  • empirical broad-spectrum antibacterial therapy against S. aureus (and MRSA) and gram-negative bacilli/anaerobes
  • Toxic shock - use a protein synthesis inhibitor (e.g. clindamycin) and IV Ig
  • Narrow spectrum therapy after identification and sensitivity testing of isolated pathogen
  • Prevention
    • impractical to prevent myositis given association with transient bacteraemia
    • Debridement of devitalised tissue is effective at preventiong myonecrosis

Myonecrosis

Background

Pathology

  • Clostridium perfringens myonecrosis complicates penetrating trauma
    • non-traumatic clostridial myonecrosis can occur after hematogenous dissemination of more aerotolerant species
  • Group A streptococci can cause severe myonecrotic infections - a true medical emergency

Clinical features

Diagnostic criteria

  • as above

Investigations

  • as above

Management

  • Gas gangrene - immediate high dose penicillin and clindamycin, urgent surgical exploration

Dementia as a geriatric syndrome

Background

  • acquired, persistent and progressive impairment of intellectual function
  • compromise of 1+ cognitive domains
  • prevalence doubles every 5 years in older population
    • 30-50% in >= 85 year olds
  • Preventative factors
    • higher education levels
    • control of CVD risk factors
  • Causes
    • Alzheimers disease (2/3rds)
    • Vascular dementia
    • Lewy body dementia
  • Associations
    • Depression - MDD in 20-50%
    • Delirium

DSM5 definition

  • Complex attention
    • easily distracted, difficulty performing calculations
  • Executive function
    • poor abstraction, mental flexibility, planning, judgement
  • Learning and memory
    • recall, recount
  • Language
    • word finding and object naming
  • Percetual motor function
    • navigation, copying image
  • Social cognition
    • personality, social cues
  • Additionally requires functional decline to loss independence in IADLs

Clinical features

  1. Screening
    1. Cognitive impairment - MOCA
      • Score < 26 â†’ Sn >= 0.94, Sp <= 0.60
    2. Decision making capacity
      1. Express choice
      2. Understand relevant information about risks/benefits
      3. Comprehension of problem and its consequences
      4. Ability to reason
      5. Consistency of choice

Investigations

  • FBC, EUC, CMP, TSH, VB12
    • hypothyroid or hypoVB12 can contribute to cognitive impairment

Imaging

  • CT Brain - SDH, tumour, previous stroke, hydrocephalus
  • MRI if acute/subacute presentation

Differentials

  • Mild cognitive impairment - conversion rates from 5-20%/year
  • Delirium - acute onset, fluctuating course, attention deficits (Rather than memory)
    • Can be caused by anticholinergics, hypnotics, neuroleptics, opioids, NSAIDs, antihistamines (H1- and H2- antagonists) and corticosteroids

Treatment

  1. Cognitive impairment
    1. anticholinesterases - donepezil, galatamine, rivastigmine
      • modest cognitive improvement, not convincingly shown to delay functional decline or institutionalisation
      • insufficient evidence to use in MCI
      • side effects - diarrhoea, nausea, anorexia, weight loss, syncope
    2. Memantine - NDMA antagonist
      • no evidence to support its use in anything except Alzherimers
  2. Behavioural problems
    1. nonpharmacological
      • Ix for delirium, pain, urinary obstruction, fecal impaction
      • verbal de-escalation
    2. Pharmacological
      • use only when posing danger to themselves or others
      • Antipsychotics for behavioural disturbances (agitation, aggression)
        • Atypical - risperidone, olanzapine, quetiapine, aripiprazole - better side effect profile
          • increased risk of stroke, weight gain, hyperglycaemia
          • starting risperidone dose 0.25-2mgPO
        • Typical - haloperidol - poorer side effect profile, more dopaminergic activity
          • starting haloperidol 0.5-2mg PO
      • antipsychotics increase mortality compared to placebo
  3. Driving
    • Moderately severe or advanced dementia should stop driving
    • be aware of reporting requirements
  4. Advanced financial planning - EGPOA

Prognosis

  • Life expectancy post AD diagnosis 3-15 years

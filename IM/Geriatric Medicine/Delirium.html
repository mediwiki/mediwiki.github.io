Delirium

Background

  • Epidemiology
    • Prevalence on admission - 5-15%
    • Incidence throughout admission - ~56% of older patients
      • post-operative delirium (NOFF) up to 61%

      • 50% post-CABG

      • 75% in ICU

  • Risk factors

    • Predisposing
        • Age >70

  • Pre-existing dementia

  • Severe medical illness

  • Previous delirium

  • Visual and hearing impairment

  • Depression

  • Abnormal sodium, potassium and glucose

  • Polypharmacy

  • Alcohol/benzo use

    • Precipitating • Use of indwelling catheter

• Adding three or more medications

• Multiple bed moves - disorientation

• Pain
• Surgery
• Anaesthesia
• Hypoxia
• Malnutrition and dehydration • Use of physical restraints

Pathology

  • Hypothesised pathophysiology
    • Inflammatory - IL2, IL6, TNF, CRP
    • Cholinergic deficiency
    • Dopaminergic overactivation
    • Apoptosis
  • Subtypes of delirium
    • Hyperactive - 20-30%
    • Hypoactive - 25%
    • Mixed - 45%

Clinical features

Diagnostic criteria (DSM-V)

  1. Disturbance in attention and awareness.
  2. Disturbance develops acutely and tends to fluctuate in severity.
  3. At least one additional disturbance in a cognitive domain.
  4. Disturbances are not better explained by a pre-existing dementia and do not occur in the context of severely reduced level of arousal or coma.
  5. Evidence of an underlying medical cause or causes.

Investigation

  • Delirium screen
    • Rapid Four 'A's Test (4AT)
      • All patients without intellectual disability >65 yrs (>45 yrs Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander) within 24 hrs of admission.
      • Simple, quick (< 2 minutes) and well validated bedside tool for detecting delirium
      • Built-in cognitive testing
      • No special training needed to implement
      • Gives a clinically informative 4AT score, not an Unable To Assess (UTA), in patients too sleepy or restless for cognitive testing or interview
    • Scoring of 4AT
      • 0 = Delirium or severe cognitive impairment unlikely
      • 1 to 3 = Possible cognitive impairment
        • More detailed cognitive assessment and informant history taking may be required
      • 4 or higher = Possible delirium +/- cognitive impairment

  • History

    • • Collateral history from family/friend/NH very important • Presenting symptoms
      • Falls
      • Infection

    • Bladder and bowel function • Diet and fluid intake
    • Pre-morbid cognitive status • PMH: Previous delirium

    • Medication: recent changes to prescription and OTC • Alcohol history
    • Social history: Functional status - Examination - • Vital signs: hypoxia, hypotension
    • Glucose
    • Mental state exam: arousal, attention, orientation vs GCS • CNS exam: new signs
    • Chest: infection
    • Abdomen: faecal loading, palpable bladder
    • Skin: infection, dehydration - Investigations • Urinalysis/MSU • FBCS, EUC
    • Glucose
    • CXR

    • ECG
    • ? Blood cultures
    • ? Cardiac enzymes
    • ? CTB
    • ? thyroid function, folate and B12

Management

  • Consider precipitating factors

M-metabolic-BSL, high Ca etc I- infections
S-structural-cerebral lesions T-toxins-drugs,ETOH E-environmental-noise, light etc

  • Treat reversible causes

    • • Urinary retention
      • Faecalimpaction
      • Infection

    • Medications - Nonpharmacological management - Environmentmodifications

    • Toileting, eating, drinking

    • Usevisionandhearingaids

    • behaviour chart

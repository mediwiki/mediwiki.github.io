Background

  • lymphoid cell malignancies in predominantly lymphoid tissue (nodes, spleen, marrow)
  • classification based on
    • morphology
    • immunophenotype
    • cytogenetics
    • molecular testing
    • clinical factors
  • Non-Hodgkin lymphoma
    • B-cell
      • precursor vs. mature
    • T and NK cells
      • precursor vs. mature
  • Hodgkin lymphoma
    • Classical
      • nodular sclerosis
      • mixed cellularity
      • lymphocyte rich
      • lymphocyte depleted
    • Nodular lymphocyte predominant Hodgkin lymphoma

- B-cell - precursor B-cell in BM - goes into circulation, encounters antigen - reaches germinal centre - clas sswitch, somatic hypermutation, etc. and matures - majority of B-cell NHL and HL come from germinal centre - leaves germinal centre into mature b-cell or plasma cell - T-cell - BM stem cell to thymus - reaches periphery

Risk factors

  • Exposures
    • occupational - farmers (Mantle cell)
    • environmental
    • prior RT, chemotherapy
  • Immune dysfunction
    • autoimmune disease
    • immunodeficiency/HIV
    • immune suppression/ post-transplant (EBV associated lymphoma)
  • Genetics - 12-30% have family history
  • infectious assocations
    • EBV: Burkitt lymphoma (100% in africans), DLBCL, NK-Tcell lymphoma (aggressive, nasal passage lymphoma), Hodgkin lymphoma, Plasmablastic lymphoma (rare, immunocompromised, typically HPV)
    • HTLV-1: Adult T-cell leukemia/lymphoma (presents with hypercalcaemia++)
    • HHV-8: Primary effusion lymphoma, LB-cell lymphoma associated with Castlemans
    • Marginal zone lymphoma - assoc w/ H.pylori (Gastric MALT - chronic inflammation stimulates B-cell clonal stimulation), B burgdorferi (skin MALT), Hepatitic C (splenic marginal zone lymphoma), C. jejuni

Clinical features

  • Lymphadenopathy (2/3)
  • B-symptoms - Fever > 38, drenching night sweat, 10% weight loss in 6mo
    • more common in HL and aggressive NHL
    • reflects burden of disease and how fast its growing
  • NHL
    • extra-nodal site affected - GI tract, skin, bone
    • rare - testicles (older), thyroid, kidney, bladder, adrenal, heart, lung, breast

Differentials of lymphadenopathy

  • infection - EBV, CMV, bacterial
  • Medications - dilantin, sulfonamides, penicillin, hydralazine; particularly antiepileptics
  • Rheumatologic - Lupus, RA, Still's disease, Churg-Strauss
    • association with rheumatic dx and development of lymphoma too
    • need to watch; progressive or out of proportion adenopathy → should biopsy still
  • Other - sarcoid, kikuchi disease (necrotising granulomatous disease in neck), amyloidosis, chronic sranulomatous disease, Castleman's disease

Investigations

  • Biopsy
    • FNA is NOT adequate to see architecture of tissue and need tissue for immunochemistry and further studies
    • Supraclavicular nodes > Cervical/axillary > inguinal
      • supraclavicular nodes typically NOT infective, and can heal up well
      • inguinal node drains large part of body so don't want to be left with lymphoedema
    • aim for excisional biopsy > CT guided core biopsy
    • sent for pathology, IHC, flow cytometry
      • negative flow cytometry does not mean NO lymphoma → large cells do not flow well
  • Staging scans - CT CAP +/- PET
    • PET for metabolically active tumor
  • BMB
  • FBC + film, EUC, LFT, uric acid, CMP
    • b2 microglobulin for indolent lymphoma
    • LDH for burden of fast growing lymphoma - note can be elevated for many reasons
    • SPEP - CLL/SLL/waldenstrom's/lymphoplasmacytoid - baseline level of Igs (send in B-cell lymphomas)

Serious complications

  • Cord compression
    • if suspecting lymphoma - expedite tissue ASAP
  • pericardial disease/tamponade - in aggressive NHL involving mediastinum, and HL
    • once therapy started, tamponade will typically resolve
  • hypercalcaemia - typically from 1,25(OH)D related; macrophages in tumour microenvironment will hydroxylate vitamin D
  • SVC/airway compromise - same patients as tamponade patients (mediastinal lymphoma)
  • hyperviscosity syndrome - typically in Waldenstrom's (IgM production)
    • rituximab treatment of Wadenstrom's can result in transient hyperviscosity
    • visual changes, headache, mental status change, dyspnoea
  • intestinal obstruction - mass effect
  • ureteral obstruction - mass effect
  • tumor lysis syndrome - Burkitt's may present with spontaneous TLS, but not typical
    • allopurinol and IV fluids treat majority; rasburicase may need to be use
  • ITP/AIHA

NHL

Survival untreated Years Months Weeks
Response to chemotherapy Not curable Curative Curative
Example Follicular lymphoma DLBCL Burkitt lymphoma
  • Indolent lymphomas - slow growing, incidentally picked up (imaging for other reasons)
    • B-cell lymphomas
      • B-cell CLL/SLL -
        • if disease in blood and > 5000 circulating tumor cells = CLL
        • < 5000 in blood, in nodes and marrow → SLL
      • Lymphoplasmacytic / Waldenstroms
      • Hairy cell leukiema
      • Follicular (gr 1-2) - 20 year median survival
      • Marginal zone
        • Nodal - acts like follicular
        • Extranodal (MALT) - favourable, can be treated with focal radiation
        • Splenic - splenomegaly, cytopenia
      • Mantle cell - halfway between indolent and aggressive
        • 7-8 year median survival
      • Plasma cell myeloma - most mature type of B-cell lymphoma
    • T-cell lymphoma
      • T-cell large granular lymphocyte leukemia - indolent disease on spectrum of Felty's syndrome
        • often do not need therapy and can be watched unelss become neutropenic/anaemic
      • mycosis fungoides
  • Aggressive lymphomas
    • DLBCL
    • Follicular lymphomas (grade 3) - grade = size of cells on microscopy
    • Peripheral T-cell lymphoma - 15% of NHLs, difficult to treat with many subtypes
    • Anaplastic Large cell lymphoma - subset of peripheral T-cell lymphoma
      • Alk positive (in kids) is favourable
    • NK/T lymphoma - aggressive
  • Highly aggressive
    • Burkitt lymphoma
    • Adult T-cell lymphoma/leukemia (HTLV-1 associated)
    • Precursor B-lymphoblastic lymphoma (synonymous with B-cell ALL)
    • Precursor T-lymphoblastic lymphoma

DLBCL

  • Most common NHL in adults - 25%
  • mAge 65, M>F
  • Management
    • CHOP (1970s)
    • 1980s - 2nd and 3rd gen regimens with improved CR rates and survivals in pilot studies, but no benefit in RCT compared to CHOP
    • rituximab (anti-CD20) - ADCC by NK-cell activated (tumor cell killing) + complement mediated toxicity + direct killing (poorly understood)
      • R-CHOP improved overall survival
  • Prognosis
    • 65-70% chance of cure with R-CHOP
    • Determined by international prognostic index determines risk and chance of cure
    • Gene expression profiling also available
      • activated B-cell type vs, Germinal center B-cell type, primary mediastinal subtype
        • germinal centre B-cell type better PFS vs. activated B-cell like

Follicular lymphoma

  • 2nd most common NHL (20%)
  • seen as large follicles under microscope
  • mAge 60, F>M (1.17:1)
  • typically presents disseminated disease; 20% may have stage I disease (BMB to prove it is localised)
    • half cured in stage I disease with RTx
    • advanced but low burden → no benefit to treating early if asymptomatic with low burden → observation
      • same rate of histological transformation, no survival disadvantage
      • can sometimes see spontaneous remission
    • advanced but high burden → therapy
      • cytopenias secondary to BM infiltration
      • threatened end-organ dysfunction - relatively uncommon
      • symptomatic disease
      • bulky disease (> 10cm)
      • steady progression during period of observation > 6mo
      • concurrent histologic transformation - treat as DLBCL
      • massive splenomegaly
  • Therapy options
    • rituximab + chemotherapy
      • low burden has good response to rituximab alone
      • Chemo: Bendamustine vs. CHOP
    • maintenance rituximab - keep in remission longer, no benefit on OFS
    • Novel agents - lenalidomide (iMib), idelalisib
    • stem cell transplant MAY be curative in subset of patients with relapsed/refractory disease
      • if comes back → get back in remission → then try SCT

Hodgkin lymphoma

  • Reed-sternberg cells - germinal centre B-cells that cannot make Ig
  • rarer than NHL
  • mAge 39
  • increased incidence in industrialised world
    • seen more common in eldest child
    • nodular sclerosis subtype in USA, high SES
    • mixed cellularity/lymphocyte depleted seen in immunocompromised and lower SES countries
  • Clinical features
    • Fever including classic Pel-Ebstein - cyclical fevers
    • drenching night sweats 25%
    • pruritis 10-15% - no rash until excoriation
    • Lymphadenopathy - neck/mediastinum most common
      • abdominal/groin pain
      • alcohol induced pain - cytokine mediated?
    • rarely jaundiced or severe abdominal pain
  • approach to management
    • consider competing risks - relapses occur within the first 5 years, but secondary complications (malignancy, cardiac) arise late and increase risk over time
    • complications
      • fertility - ABVD low risk for infertility
      • pulmonary toxicity - bleomycin (fibrosis/pneumonitis) and mediastinal RT
      • cardiotoxicity - mediastinal RT (pericardial, valvular, CAD), doxorubicin (cardiomyopathy)
      • secondary malignancies - solid tumors from radiation - breast, lung, etc.
    • Chemotherapy - ABVD - adriamycin, bleomycin, vinclastine, dacarbaxine
    • Radiotherapy - field radiation
    • Stage I and II disease
      • Chemo +/- radio - ~90% cured with chemotherapy alone
    • Stage III and IV disease
      • chemo always, radiation for bulky disease - 75% cured with initial therapy
    • new drugs
      • brentuximab vedotin - Ab drug conjugate
        • CD30 (reed sternberg cell) antibody
        • deliver microtubule inhibitor direct to cells
        • 75% response rate, especially in relapsed or refractory disease
        • 5% difference in relapse rate; not too much better (NEJM 2017 Connors et al)
      • immunotherapy - PD1/PDL1
        • Reed-sternberg has amplications at 9p24.1 - overexpressed PDL1-PDL2

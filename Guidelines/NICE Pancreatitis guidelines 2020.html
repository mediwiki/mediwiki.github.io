Preamble

  • For patients with severe acute pacreatitis, a full recovery may take at least 3 times as long as their hospital stay
  • Hospital admissions for several months is relatively common
  • Local complications of acute pancreatitis may take up to several weeks before we can identify if intervention is needed - indeed, delayed intervention may be safer
  • 15-20% of adults with severe acute pancreatitis die in hospital
  • On discharge, GPs should be notified on
    • how a patient should take their pancreatic enzyme replacement and dose escalations
    • HbA1c testing should be conducted every 6 months
    • Bone mineral density testing should be conducted every 2 years
  • Patients should be educated that
    • Drinking cessation is very important and can exacerbate their pancreatitis
    • Smoking is associated with chronic pancreatitis
  • Investigate the cause of pancreatitis - being
    • Alcohol - do not assume this is the cause immediately
    • Gallstones
    • Metabolic causes (e.g. hypercalcaemia or hyperlipidaemia)
    • Drugs
    • Microlithiasis
    • Hereditary pancreatitis
    • Autoimmune pancreatitis (e.g. IgG4 disease)
    • Ampullary/pancreatic tumours
    • Anatomical anomalies (pancreas divisum)

Guideline - acute pancreatitis

Recommendation GRADE Evidence
Do not offer prophylactic antimicrobials to people with acute pancreatitis - -
Ensure adequate fluid resuscitation is carried out in pancreatitis - -
Do not make patients with acute pancreatitis NBM unless there is a clear reason (e.g. vomiting) - -
Dietician input for severe or moderately severe acute pancreatitis within 72h should be obtained - -
Where suspected infected pancreatic necrosis occurs, endoscopy or percutaneous approach debridement should be offered for patients with acute pancreatitis - -

Guideline - chronic pancreatitis

Recommendation GRADE Evidence
Ensure dietician input is obtained for chronic pancreatitis - -
Consider surgery for pancreatic duct obstruction/painful chronic pancreatitis - -
Consider extracorporeal shockwave lithotripsy for pancreatic duct obstruction by dominant stone if surgery is unsuitable - -
Offer EUC-guide or endoscopy transpapillary drainage of pancreatic head pseudocysts for symptomatic pseudocysts (pain, vomiting, weight loss) or non-symptomatic pseudocysts with any of: Pancreatic duct disruption, pressure on large vessels/diaphragm, risk of rupture, suspected infection - -
consider laparoscopy/open drainage of pseudocysts if endoscopy is unsuitable or failed - -
Assess Type 3c DM every 6 months for suitability of insulin therapy - -
Assess for pancreatic exocrine insufficiency and malnutrition every 12 months and treat vitamin/mineral deficiencies accordingly - -
Offer bone density assessment every 2 years - -
Annually monitor for pancreatic cancer for people with hereditary pancreatitis (40% lifetime risk) - -
Monitor for diabetes (chronic pancreatitis lifetime risk 80%), especially in calcific pancreatitis; 6 monthly monitoring of HbA1c should be performed - -

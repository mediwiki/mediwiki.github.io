Guideline

Assessment and diagnosis - TIA

Recommendation GRADE Evidence
Patients with fluctuating symptoms (TIA-like) should be treated as having a stroke and be assessed in ED Strong ---
The ABCD2 tool should not be used in isolation as it can delay recognition of AF and carotid stenosis. ABCD > 4 7 day Sn 86.7, Sp 35.4 Weak ---
TIA with anterior circulation symptoms should undergo doppler imaging (CT Arch to CoW, US or MRA) within 2 days Strong ---
TIA should undergo brain imaging and MRI where available Weak ---
All suspected TIA should commence secondary prevention ASAP Strong ---
All TIA should undergo ECG (for AF) +/- holter and anticoagulation should be commenced once haemorrhage excluded Strong ---

Assessment and diagnosis - Stroke

Recommendation GRADE Evidence
Where expert stroke team is not immediately available, tools (e.g. ROSIER, CPSS, FAST, LASPP) should be used to assess patients Strong ---
Stroke severity should be assessed by a trained clinician using NIHSS Practice ---
BGL should be taken immediately and excluded as a mimic Practice ---

Investigations - initial imaging

Recommendation GRADE Evidence
Suspected strokes who are candidates for reperfusion should undergo brain imaging IMMEDIATELY (CT perfusion weakly preferred in addition to routine imaging). All suspected patients should have CT or MRI in <60 minutes (MRI > noncon CT) Strong Biesbroek et al 2012, Brazzelli et al 2009
If identifying hyperdense thrombus, thin (<2mm) tomography should be used rather than standard (5mm) Strong Mair et al 2015
Neurological deterioration in confirmed stroke patients need immediate clinical imaging (CT or MRI) Practice ---
24h routine brain imaging should be considered to identify haemorrhagic transformation and delinate infarct region to guide ongoing antithrombotic therapy and DVTp Practice ---
CTA or MRA from Arch to CoW routine recommended for ischemic stroke and TIA patients Practice ---
IV contrast for CT angiogram/perfusion should not be delayed by renal function concerns - advised to post-hydrate with IV NS Practice / guidelines RANZCR guidelines 2016, Ang et al 2015

Investigations - aetiology

Recommendation GRADE Evidence
ECG monitoring should be conducted for all stroke patients, and monitored for 24h Weak Kurka et al 2015
Holter/heart bug monitoring recommended for embolic stroke of uncertain source Strong Afzal et al 2015
Echocardiography should be considered in ischemic stroke patients, and TOE for suspected valvular, L) atrial and aortic arch pathology Weak Holmes et al 2014

Initial management

Recommendation GRADE Evidence
All stroke patients should be admitted and monitored in a stroke unit with an interdisciplinary team Strong Langhorne 2020
All acute stroke services should implement FESS protocols Strong Middleton et al 2011
IV thrombolysis should be offered to potentially disabling ischemic stroke within 4.5 hours of onset (if meets eligibility criteria) Strong Wardlaw et al 2014; Emberson et al 2014
Telecteplase (0.25mg/kg, max 25mg, 5s bolus) or alteplase (0.9mg/kg, max 90mg) should be administered up to 4.5h after last well time Strong Wardlaw et al 2014; Emberson et al 2014; Anderson et al 2016; Parsons et al 2012;; Campbell et al 2018
If perfusion mismatch criteria is met in addition to clinical citeria, IV alteplase (0.9mg/kg up to 90mg) should be administered up to 9h from last well time (or midpoint of sleep for wake-up symtoms) unless ECR is planned Strong Ma et al 2019, Campbell et al 2019
If potentially disabling ischemic stroke of unknown time onset meets MRI FLAIR-diffusion mismatch criteria in addition to standard clinical criteria, IV alteplase (0.9mg/kg max 90mg) may be considered Weak Thomalla et al 2019
ECR should be conducted for Large vessel occlusion in ICA, M1 or proximal/dominant M2, basilar artery, or tandem occulsion of carvical carotid and intracranial arteries within 24h of onset and in consideration of patient comorbidities Strong ...
IV Thrombolysis should be given whilst awaiting ECR if eligible Strong Goyal et al 2016; Turc et al 2022
Swallow screening should be conducted in acute stroke patients (within 4h) - where difficulties are identified, early use of exercises, advice, modifications should be used Strong Poorjavad 2014, Benfield 2020
Where reperfusion therapy is not indicated for ischemic stroke, start antiplatelet therapy ASAP after excluding haemorrhage Strong Sandercock et al 2014
Where thrombolysis has been administered, antiplatelet therapy should not be started within 24h (unless a stent was implanted as part of the acute stroke management) Strong Zinkstok et al 2012
Do not ROUTINELY use anticoagulation in patients without cardioembolism (e.g. AF) following stroke. DAPT (aspirin clopdigrel) should be started within 24h for 3 weeks in minor ischemic stroke or high risk TIA Strong Sandercock et al 2014; Whiteley et al 2013, Hao et al 2018
Blood pressures should not be dropped below SBP < 140mmHg in ANY stroke patient Weak Bath and Krishnan 2014
Haemorrhagic strong patients should aim SBP ~ 140mmHg. Pre-existing antihypertensives may be withheld until neurologically stable Weak Tsivgoulis 2014; Qureshi 2016
Ischemic stroke patients should aim BP < 185/110mmHg before and within 24h from treatment; if BP > 220/120mmHg, reduce BP by no more than 20% over 24h Practice ---

Regarding thrombolysis - NINDS tPA trial suggests treating 'measurable neurologic deficit' - up to clinical interpretation. - rapidly improving symptoms is sometimes considered as an exclusion - Severe stroke patients may not have a better outcome than mild symptoms with tPA - tPA can be considered within 4.5h of symptom onset (from pure clinical assessment) - alteplase (tPA) can be considered from 4.5h-?h of symptom onset (from perfusion imaging or diffusion-FLAIR)

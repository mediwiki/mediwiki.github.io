Case 1

Background

  • 24F Malaysian heritage studying architecture at university
  • fatigue + exertional dyspnoea
    • dyspnoea gradual onset over last 3 weeks
    • nil cough, wheeze, recent cold/flu
    • occasional r) sided chest pain, sharp, nil radiation, pleuritic (inspiratory)
    • denies recent fevers
    • fatigue about 6 months ago - exhausted at end of day, not normal for her
  • nil change in diet or unexpected weight loss
  • rash on face (?malar) last couple of weeks, worse spending time outside (i.e. photosensitive)
  • usually fit and well

Past medical history

  • nil regular medications
  • nil known allergies

Family history

  • NAD

Social history

  • non-smoker
  • non-drinker
  • Australia for last 3 years, doing well in university
  • single, no past boyfriends, not sexually active

Examination

  • Basic obs
    • Afebrile, normotensive, normocardic, SpO2 94% RA
  • CVD exam - HSDNM, JVPNE, nil LL oedema
  • Resp exam - dullness to percussion, intermittent 'squeak' and reduced breath sounds over R) base, nil crackles, wheeze, reduced vocal responance
    • Q: What does the reduced vocal resonance mean?
      • Adds to the differential of a consolidation vs. effusion
    • Q: what does the squeak represent?
      • Pleural rub secondary to pleural inflammation
  • Face - erythematous rash, malar distribution, nasolabial sparing, not tender
  • Hands - mild swelling over proximal interphalangeal joints bilaterally with nil joint deformities

Investigations

Questions

  1. Interpret the bloods
  2. Why is her CRP normal but ESR high
  3. Explain the neutropenia
    1. In a young, otherwise healthy female this would not be consistent with a bacterial infection, and this is supported by the normal CRP
    2. Low lymphocytes may be seen in viral infections, however the duration of symptoms and lack of fever would be unusual for acute infections.
    3. CMV (cytomegalovirus) or EBV may present subacutely and present with arthralgia and fatigue, however the facial rash and pleural effusion are not consistent.
  4. What would you do next
  5. Interpret the CXR
    1. blunting of costphrenic and cardiophrenic angles
    2. meniscus in R) lung
    3. no other abnormalities

Investigations, continued

  • On the basis of her symptoms and pathology results you suspect Hana has Systemic Lupus Erythematosis
  • You refer her to a rheumatologist who does further investigations, confirms SLE and commences her on hydroxychloroquine and a short course of prednisolone.

Questions

  1. How do you interpret the results above?
    • raised ANA causes?
    • AntiDNA Ab has a very high (~95%) specificity for diagnosis of SLE, and patients who are DNA Ab positive are more likely to develop renal involvement
    • Of interest the Ro-52 antibody has been shown to predict a higher risk of interstitial lung disease in patients with autoimmune disease
    • The presence of a reduced complement level indicates the presence of active disease.

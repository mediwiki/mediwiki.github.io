Case 2

Presenting complaint

  • You are working as a GP registrar at a clinic in Taree.  Harriet Jones is your next appointment.  She has seen you a couple of times over the last few weeks with a complaint of fatigue.
  • Harriet is a 32 year old female.  She works in the local shopping centre as the store manager for a retail shoe store.  She has two children – Brock aged 10 and Kylee-Ann aged 6.

  • Harriet has been feeling exhausted for the past 4 months.  She came and saw you 6 weeks ago complaining of this and requesting your assistance in fixing it.

  • 2 children – Brock and Kylee-Ann, married to Tom who works as a car mechanic.

  • Ex smoker – quit 2 years ago.  Smoked 10/day for 10 years.
  • Occasional alcohol on weekends – 2-3 glasses of white wine with friends
  • PMHx childhood asthma, resolved age 15
  • Allergies nil
  • Regular medications: OCP
  • UTD vaccinations and cervical screening
  • Her parents are fit and well
    • Father hypertension
    • Mother osteoarthritis knees
    • 2/4 grandparents are still alive:
    • Maternal grandfather had died CVA age 71
      • Type 2 diabetes mellitus onset age 54
    • Paternal grandmother passed away MVA age 52

•You took a detailed history when you saw Harriet 6 weeks ago:

•She has a reasonable diet with 1 serves of fruit and 2-3 serves of vegetables every day.  She eats red meat 1-2 times per week, chicken or fish on other days.

•She sleeps well at night, ’better than she used to now the kids are older’.  Kylee-Ann wakes maybe once per week.  Harriet averages 7 hours sleep per night.

•Denies night sweats

•She denies daytime sleepiness, states ‘I don’t fall asleep, I’m just exhausted’.  She does not snore.

•Tries to exercise 3 times per week – walking the dog or going to gym but hasn’t been able to recently because of her tiredness

•Her periods are regular and not heavy.  Last menstrual cycle 3 weeks ago and she doesn’t think she pregnant.  She hasn’t missed any of her OCP recently.

•Denies any difficulties with the children – both enjoying school.  She has no concerns regarding them

•Acknowledges that she is frustrated with her fatigue, but denies depression

•No change in bowel habits, weight stable

•No rashes, joint aches or pains.

•No recent travel overseas or trips camping

•She was bitten by mosquitos a few weeks ago, denies any tick bites that she can recall

Examination

•Height 165cm, Weight 68kg BMI 25

•BP 115/72mmHg Pulse 65 regular

•Random BGL 5.0

•Urine pregnancy screen negative

•Afebrile

•Good eye contact when conversing

•Normal train of thought and flow of speech

•Care taken with appearance

•Mood appears normal

•No conjunctival pallor, palmar creases normal colour

•Not clubbed

•

•Oropharynx

•Tonsils not enlarged, no exudate

•Teeth in good condition, gingiva (gums) not swollen, pale or erythematous

•

•HS dual, no murmurs, no added sounds

•Chest normal expansion

•Clear to percussion

•Normal breath sounds with no added sounds

•

•Abdomen soft and non tender, spleen not palpable

•No palpable lymphadenopathy

•No erythematous striae (long standing older marks from pregnancy

•

•Cranial nerves intact (CN 1 not tested)

•Normal tone and power thoughout

•Reflexes intact and symmetric

•No tremor

•

•No rashes, or bruising

•Skin feels supple, not dry

•No joint or muscle tenderness, no effusions

•

You reassured her that everything seemed normal and sent her for some blood tests

Questions

•What would you like to do next?

•·         This is a good place to pause and allow to students to formulate some differential diagnoses. Should they look further? How to balance overinvestigating to avoid missing anything? What are the risks of over-investigating here?

•Are further investigations warranted and if so what might be the principles guiding the choice of investigative pathway?

·         Whay have we ordered each test? Ask the students if this assists them in refining a diagnosis – whilst this does not point in any particular direction for diagnosis it helps rule conditions out.  If they are struggling, or becoming frustrated, rephrasing the differential diagnosis question to ‘what is it not’, may help

Why have we performed spirometry? (smoking history).  Ask the students to describe the CXR and interpret the spirometry.

Followup

•She is now returning for follow up:

•’Nothing has changed’

•‘I’m exhausted’

•Reviewing the history reveals no new information

•Examination findings are unchanged

•She has been ‘Googling’ her symptoms since you last saw her and has a list of questions:

•Am I anaemic?

•What about my thyroid?

•Could it be Ross River fever?

•Should I give up gluten?

•Could I have Lyme disease?

•How do you answer her questions?

Further investigations

  • What test is missing?

Serum IgA level as most coeliac tests are IgA based

•You explain to Harriet that whilst you have not performed every test possible for causes of her fatigue, she has had a broad range of tests that should indicate the presence of any serious  underlying disease.

•

•You reassure her that the negative tests rule out the serious medical causes for fatigue and that in some cases it is not possible to determine an underlying diagnosis. 

•

•Some causes of fatigue do not have a definitive diagnostic test and are a combination of a cluster of symptoms and the exclusion of other causes

•

•You both discuss a plan for further follow up.  Harriet will try an take a little more time for herself and will endeavour to continue to get regular exercise and adequate sleep.  She will return in 2 months time if her symptoms are unchanged.

Ask the students if they think adequate exploration of a non-medical cause of her symptoms has been undertaken. What about her relationship with her husband?. Has this been explored? Could there be any other stressors she may now wish to talk more about now that she is aware that her concerns have been heard and addressed?

Chest XR Interpretation

Weber Liu

Basic concepts

  • We only see differences in density
  • outline of mediastinum visible due to aerated lung next to it
  • white branching pulmonary vessels visible due to air in alveolar spaces next to vessels
  • blood vessels behind the liver in R) LL due to vessels outlined by air
    • loss of vessels = ?lesion

Consolidation

  • Can be blood, fluid, pus or protein
    • hard to tell what it is, needs clinical correlation
  • Look through the heart and diaphragm to look for consolidation

AP CXR

The very basics

  • Confirm ID
    • Patient name, age / DOB, sex
    • Type of film – PA or AP, erect or supine, correct L/R marker, inspiratory/expiratory series
    • Date and time of study
  • RIPE
    • Rotation – medial clavicle ends equidistant from spinous process
    • Inspiration – 5-6 anterior ribs in MCL or 8-10 posterior ribs above diaphragm, poor inspiration?, hyperexpanded?
    • Picture – straight vs oblique, entire lung fields, scapulae outside lung fields, angulation (ie ’tilt’ in vertical plane)
    • Exposure (Penetration) – IV disc spaces, spinous processes to ~T4, L) hemidiaphragm visible through cardiac shadow.

Airway

  • Trachea – central or slightly to right lung as crosses aortic arch
  • Paratracheal/mediastinal masses or adenopathy
  • Carina & RMB/LMB
  • Right middle lobe (R)
    • hugs the right heart border
  • Lingula (L)
    • touches L) heart border
  • L) lower lobe
    • touches the descending aorta - loss of descending thoracic aorta indicates consolidation of L) LL
    • touches the L) hemidiaphragm

Bones

  • Ribs, sternum, spine, clavicles – symmetry, fractures, dislocations, lytic lesions, density
  • Soft tissues – looking for symmetry, swelling, loss of tissue planes, subcutaneous air, masses
  • Breast shadows
  • Calcification – great vessels, carotids

Cardiomediastinum

  • Mediastinal width <8cm on PA film
  • Aortic knob
  • Hilum – T6-7 IV disc level, left hilum is usually higher (2cm) and squarer than the V-shaped right hilum.
  • Check vessels, calcification.
  • Heart position –⅔ to left, ⅓ to right
  • Heart size – measure cardiothoracic ratio on PA film (normal <0.5)
  • Heart borders – R) border is R) atrium, L) border is L) ventricle & atrium
  • Heart shape
  • Aortic stripe

Diaphragm

  • Hemidiaphragm levels – Right Lung higher than Left Lung (~2.5cm / 1 intercostal space)
  • Diaphragm shape/contour
  • Cardiophrenic and costophrenic angles – clear and sharp
  • Gastric bubble / colonic air
  • Subdiaphragmatic air (pneumoperitoneum)

Extras

  • ETT, CVP line, NG tube, PA catheters
  • ECG electrodes, PICC line, chest tube
  • PPM, AIDC, metalwork

Fields

Lung Fields:

  • Vascularity – to ~2cm of pleural surface (~3cm in apices), vessels in bases > apices
  • Pneumothorax – don’t forget apices
  • Lung field outlines – abnormal opacity/lucency, atelectasis, collapse, consolidation, bullae
  • Horizontal fissure on Right Lung
  • Pulmonary infiltrates – interstitial vs alveolar pattern
  • Coin lesions
  • Cavitary lesions

Pleura

  • Pleural reflections
  • Pleural thickening

Additional points of reference

- should be of equal transradiancy - horizontal fissure may be more difficult - should be around hilum to 6th rib in axillary line - displacement could indicate lung collapse - L) hilum should be higher than R) - diff should be < 2.5cm - should be concave and appear similar

--

- check for sharp and clear --

- Note R) diaphragm should be higher than L) diaphragm - difference should be < 3cm - outline should be smooth --

- white edge should be < 2-3cm wide on erect film - central but deviates slightly right at aortic knuckle - shifting could indicate mediastinum or lung issue

-- - Cardiac size should be < half the diameter of the chest - fuzziness of the mediastinum should only ever been seen at the intersection of the heart and diaphragm

Lateral film

  • Normal 'more black' sign
    • vertebral bodies become more radiotransparent as you move down
  • Double diaphragm
    • Can see two diaphragms on lateral
      • if you can only see 1, consider some form of consolidation sitting over a lung

More black sign

  • Normal finding in lateral CXR
  • gradual increase in apparent radiolucency of vertebral bodies from upper to lower chest
  • due to increased proportion of chest comprosed of air containing lungs over distal dorsal spine c.f. upper parts
  • on displacement of air by higher attentuation material (e.g. consolidation, fluid, mass), lower thoracic vertebral bodies become more radiodense (i.e. loss of the more black sign)
  • https://radiopaedia.org/articles/more-black-sign

- oblique fissure - blue area = if lung were to collapse, where you would see opacity

--

  • identify diaphragms
    • R) hemi - stretches across whole thorax and can be clearly seen passing through heart border
    • L) hemi disappears at posterior border of heart
    • pleural effusion can result in blunding of costophrenic angle (anteriorly or posteriorly)
  • lung fields
    • in front and above heart vs. behind heart - expect equal density
    • look for lesions
  • retrosternal space
    • should be blackest part of film
    • anterior mediastinal mass will turn this space white
  • horizontal fissure
    • faint white line passing horizontally from mid-hilum to anterior chest wall - if not horizontal, the fissure is displaced
  • oblique fissure
    • T4/5 through hilum, ending anterior 1/3rd of diaphragm
  • Hilar density
    • dense hilum can indicate mass
  • vertebral bodies
    • should get darker lower
    • vertebral collapse or density changes - can indicate bone disease

Wound Healing

Wounds

  • interruption in the continuity of epithelium
  • classified as:
    • surgical - due to surgery
    • traumatic - laceration or tissue loss
    • neoplastic - tumour ulceration when outgrowing blood supply
    • infective - e.g. eruption of an abscess

PHases of wound healing

  • Three phases of wound healing
    1. Inflammatory - clot, exudate, PMN within 24-74 hours of injury
      • control of bleeding
    2. Proliferative - angiogenesis and granulation and epithelialisation (grow/heal) starts from 4 days and lasts 1-2 weeks
    3. remodelling - wound heals to increase strength (scarring or shaping), occurs 2 weeks post-injury and continues for up to a year

Healing by primary intenton

  • primary closure - closing wound to give best cosmetic results
    • Done with sutures, stables or steristrips
    • to improve wound closure
      • maintain tension free wound
      • avoid inversion of skin edges (better to evert)
    • delayed primary closure
      • e.g. open appendicectomy wound (could be contaminated/infected), where it is packed for 1-2 days to ensure it is free of infection before primary closure
      • aim is to eventually close wound, when we are happy the wound is safe to close

Healing by secondary intention

  • healing by secondary intention - allowing skin to heal itself
    • allows wound to heal by itself (E.g. with dressings)
    • reserved for smaller wounds (e.g. 5cm leg wound could take 1-2 months to heal, would be bad for the patient)
    • secondary intention for big wounds has poor cosmetic result
    • granulation tissue forms in the proliferative phase
    • the epithelium grows from the edges and the wound contracts to decrease surface area
    • the healing is better if wound is free of infection and kept moist with dressings
    • look for a 'sloping edge' - good indication of a wound - poor healing (Sharp demarcations, etc.) could suggest malnutrition
    • raised edges - is it from BCC/SCC neoplastic process?

Fracture healing

Background

Fracture healing occurs in two different ways

  1. Direct (primary) healing
    • Direct healing - bone WITHOUT callous formation
    • good cortical apposition, absolute stability, no movement/gaps between fragments
    • normal osteoclastic-mediated remodelling is directed across fracture interface
      • osteoclastic cutting-cones cut across fracture line
      • osteoblasts lay down lamellar bone across fracture
  2. Indirect (secondary) healing
    • most common form
    • transition from one tissue to another with callus formation
    • Process of healing (union) occurs by:
      • Fracture hematoma ( bleeding, oedema); even in open fractures
        • can result in compartment syndrome
        • organisation of hematoma can heal and remodel fracture
      • Granulation tissue → osteoid (3 – 14 days)
        • will develop into a callus
      • Callus formation (mineral deposition in osteoid)
      • Ossification (3 weeks – 6 months)
        • most fractures hurt less after 3 weeks
    • Consolidation (fragment distance closes).
      • occurs with ossification
      • if the gap is >3-4mm, can result in delayed or non-union
    • Remodeling (union, move towards original shape and strength)
      • excess bone absorbed by osteoclasts
    • in response to strain and under influence of bone stimulating factors
      • tissue undergoes differentiation
      • hematoma → fibrous tissue → soft callus → mineralisation → formation of mature bone
    • strain determines nature of tissue
      • <2% → hard callus and progressive mineralisation
      • < 10% → soft callus
      • < 100% → fibrous tissue

Fracture healing goals

  • Overall goals of treatment:
    • acceptable realignment of bone (reduction) if not perfect
    • Immobilisation to maintain alignment (fixation)
    • Restoration of function (physiotherapy)
      • priority of remodelling occurs in planes as above and below
      • forearm - remodelling occurs in forearm ext and flex, less remodelling in wrist lateral movement and forearm rotation

Bone healing terminology

Union

  • fracture sufficiently healed clinically to withstand physiological loads
  • very little pain, minimal tenderness
  • radiologically seen as a bridging callus

Delayed union

  • fracture slow to heal
  • not healing within expected time frame

Non-union

  • fracture not healed, no potential to heal without fruther intervention
  • fracture that fails to demonstrate clinical or radiological improvement over 3 months
  • do not describe a fracture as non-union until after 6 months post-operatively
  • causes of non-union
    • atrophic - typically biological issue (e.g. lack of stimulus, blood supply)
    • hypertrophic - occurs from too much movement at fracture site
    • infected
  • considerations and approach to non-union
    • biology of the fracture
    • mechanical environment
    • host factors (E.g. diabetes, smoking)

Malunion

  • healing (union) of fracture in a non physiological orientation with excessive angulation and/or rotation
  • remodelling can occur in same plane above/below fracture

Consolidation

  • follows union
  • demonstrates bone has returned to normal strength
  • radiologically normal cortical pattern

Remodelling

  • bone remodels based on froces passing through it (strain)

Complications in fracture healing

Infection

  • Open fractures and soft tissue injuries have higher incidence
  • Can delay union
  • Requires aggressive control with antibiotics +/- surgery
  • can lead to amputation of digit/limb

Neurovascular injury

  • Can occur due to direct injury from bone displacement or indirectly from swelling of soft tissues
  • may be transient or permanent (neuropraxia, axonotmesis, neurotmesis) - 3 stages of nerve injury

Compartment Syndrome

  • elevated intracompartmental pressure within a confined myofascial compartment
  • capillary perfusion is reduced below a level necessary for tissue viability
  • Two basic etiologies create compartment syndrome:
    • Decreased compartment size (dressings, splints, casts)
    • Increased compartment content (bleeding, edema)
  • Clinical Manifestations
    • Six Ps
      1. PAIN (most important symptom) - ESP if pain cannot be reduced with S8 medication
      2. Paralysis
      3. Pressure
      4. Pallor/ Poikilothermia
      5. Paresthesia
      6. Pulselessness - latest stage, and far too late to reverse
  • Management
    • Remove/loosen the bandage and/or cast
    • Surgical decompression (fasciotomy)- high priority to notify surgical team of inappropriate pain not responsive to analgesia (surgical emergency)

Venous Thrombosis

  • Deep venous thrombosis is common complication in fractures. Those of high risk (obesity, smokers, oral contraceptive, pregnancy, inactivity, thrombophilic)
  • Precipitating factors:
    • Virchow's triad
  • Prevention
    • mechanical (intermittent compression device, graded compression stockings)
    • chemical (anticoagulation)
    • Early mobilisation

Fat Embolism Syndrome (FES)

  • Characterized by the presence of fat globules in tissues and organs after a traumatic skeletal injury
    • fat from long-bone travels to lung → pulmonary fat embolus
  • Clinical Manifestations
    • 24-48 hours after injury
    • Chest petechial rash; respiratory distress
    • Interstitial pneumonitis
  • Management
    • Aim to prevent
    • immobilization of a long bone fracture, careful instrumentation
    • Symptomatic
    • Fluid resuscitation
    • Oxygen

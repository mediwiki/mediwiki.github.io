Central Venous Pressure

Background

  • Central venous pressure (CVP) is the pressure in the thoracic vena cava near the right atrium
    • as the right atrium at rest is passively filled by the vena cava, it is essentially equal to the right atrial pressure
  • central venous pressure deterimines atrial filling
    • as the majority of ventricular filling (~70%) is passively filled via the atrium, this will also determine ventricular filling
    • degree of ventricular filling is also known as the preload
    • therefore CVP is a deterinant of the right ventricular preload
  • The change in CVP is defined as the following:
    • \[\Delta CVP = \frac{\Delta V}{C_v}\]
    • Where
      • \(\Delta V\) is the change in volume in the thoracic veins
      • \(C_v\) is the Vascular compliance of the thoracic veins

Factors increasing CVP

  • Right sided backwards heart failure - results in venous congestion
  • Increasing total blood volume from renal failure or fluid retention via RAAS pathways
  • SNS mediated venoconstriction which decreases venous compliance
  • increased intrathoracic volume by changing from standing to supine
  • arteriodilation in normal hearts increases CVP by increasing blood flow into venous compartments
  • forced expiration (e.g. through valsalva) results in external compression of thoracic vena cava which functionally reduces IVC compliance
  • muscle contraction will mechanically compress (decrease compliance) of peripheral veins which increases venous return into thoracic compartment

Factors decreasing CVP

  • arteriodilation in heart failure results in decreased afterload and increased stroke volume, relieving backwards heart failure congestion

Technicalities

  • The larger thoracic veins (e.g. vena cava) does not undergo large changes
  • majority of venous compliance are in venules and extra-thoracic
    • altered venule compliance results in reduced pressure upstream at the larger thoracic veins

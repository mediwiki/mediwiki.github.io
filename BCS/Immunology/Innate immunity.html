Innate immunity

Background

  • Components include
    1. Physical/mechanical and chemical barriers
      • Skin keratinisation
      • Mucous formation on mucosal epithelium and ciliary clearance in respiratory tract
      • production of antimicrobial factors (lysozyme, factic/fatty acids) in secretions
      • digestive enzymes, peristalsis
    2. microbial antagonism by body's own mutualistic and commensal microorganisms
    3. liver - xenobiotic oxidation, detoxification, synthesis of defense factors
    4. cytotoxicity by complement
    5. Phagocytosis and NETosis
    6. Acute phase reaction (CRP, serum amyloid A, mannose binding lectin)
    7. natural antibodies (by CD5+ B-cells)
    8. antimicrobial peptides - alpha defensins, cathelicidins, lactoferrin, dermicidin
    9. natural cytotoxicity - innate lymphoid cells (ILCs) e.g. NK cells, NKT cells, γōТ cells and IFN induced natural cytostasis
  • Cellular components
    • Neutrophils
      • phagocytosis and I/C killing
      • inflammation and tissue damage
    • Macrophages
      • phagocytosis and I/C killing
      • E/C killing of infected or altered self targets
      • Tissue repair
      • APC for adaptive immune response
    • NK and LAK cells
      • Killing of virus-infected and altered self targets
    • Eosinophils
      • Killing of certain parasites

Pathways of intracellular killing

- NO dependent killing - MOA: - bacteria binds to macrophages → TNFa production → iNOS upregulation and NOS production - O2 independent killing - Cationic proteins (cathepsins) - damage to microbial membranes - Lysozyme - Hydrolyses mucopeptides in cell wall - Lactoferrin - Deprives pathogen of iron - Hydrolytic enzymes (proteases) - digests killed organisms

Toll/TLR mechanism

  • History/discovery
    • discovered by Nuesslein-Volhard and Wieschaus in Drosophilia embryos without mesoderm or nervous system
    • parallel b/w dorsoventral polarity in drosophilia and inflammatory response in mammals
    • 1996 Bruno-Lemaitre - toll receptor loss of function mutation meant the flies did not survive
    • Medzhitov - discovered human equivalent to Toll - called Toll-like receptor
  • Background
    • Type 1 integral membrane glycoproteins, highly conseved
    • member of IL-1 receptors
    • 2 parts
      • Leucine repeat regions (LRRs) - picks up pattern
      • TIR domains (box 1/2/3) for signalling
  • MOA
    • PAMP on pathogen binds to TLR (PRP) → releases cytokines
    • TLR1/2, TLR2/6, TLR4 homodimer, TLR5 monomer
    • TLR3, 7, 8, 9 are for intracellular sensing
    • Gram negative bacteria binds TLR4 → activates Mal, MyD88, Tram, Trif → activated NFkB enters nucleus → nucleus production of:
      • IL-1/TNFa - enhanced inflammatory response
      • IL8 - neurophil chemotaxis
      • IL6 - B-cell activity
      • IL12 - T-cell activity

LPS signalling pathway

- LPS binds LBP - CD14 + TLR4 signalls through TIR/MyD88 → IRAK, MAP3K → NEMO → IKKb activation → NFkB release into nucleus

TLR4 polymorphisms lead to other diseases

  • Human TLR4 mutations
    • common TLR4 missense mutation
      • A896G substitution - Asp299Gly substitution (extracellular domain) → called TLR4B
      • LPS given results in FEV1 dropping faster in TLR4B
    • 90% people carry sample TLR4 allele, but TLR4B present in 7% of caucasians

TLR leads to specific immunity

  • innate immunity can push a TH1/2 response
  • each have their own bacterial products and cell wall components to respond to
    • 'pathogen associated molecular pattern' - sensed by various TLRs
      • TLR2 → myobacterial cell wall product, fungal
        • extracellular TLRs communicate via MyD88 and IRAK4 and release IL6, IL12, TNF
      • TLR7, 8, 9 pick up ssRNA and microbial DNA (consider viruses being intracellular)
        • these trigger antiviral response and release T1 IFNs
  • essential in activating the adaptive immune response

- can result in fungal and viral infections

TLR Viral response

  • TLR3, 7, 8, 9 more importnat for viruses
    • IFN pathway is pushed
  • RSV in Murine TLR4 - reduced TLR4 expression results in reduced response to RSV but not flu infection
  • human data shows more severe RSV bronchiolitis in TLR4 mutation

Other pathogen sensors

  • Nod-like receptor
    • important for caspase-1, similar to TLR and works in concert
  • Rig-like receptor (RLR) - important for viruses

NOD like receptors

- 2 signals - PAMP activats TLR via MyD88 → NF-kB pathway → converts pro-IL1B to IL-1B - Crystals (e.g. gout, pyrophosphate) activation of NLR pathway - procaspase -I → Caspase-I activation of IL-1B and IL-18 production → enhanced inflammatory pathway - NLR and TLR coacitvation makes stronger inflammatory signal

NLR mutation associated diseases

- NOD2 associated with Crohn's disease

Immune regulation

  • immune responses tightly regulated interactions of cells/mediators and mechanisms to prevent anti-self reactivity
  • failure of regulatory control can occur in
    • enhancement of immune responses or infection → generation of autoimmune reactions (i.e. loss of self-tolerance)
    • decreased immune response leads to immunodeficiency states
    • shift in immune responses → allergy

  • second signal needed for non self response

Complement system

  • Three pathways
    1. Alternative pathway
      • C3 spontaneous hydrolysis through tickover effect
        • this is stopped from going cleavage through regulators
      • in presence of microbial cell wall proteins, Properdin is released + factor B and factor D result in activation of C3 convertase → cleaves C3 into active form
      • immune activation release antibodies, which release IgM, IgG1 and IgG3 → activates classical pathways
    2. Classical pathway
    3. Lectin pathway
      • somewhat redundant - people deficient in MBL tend to have little consequences
      • MBL deficiency as a diagnosis is itself controversial

  • Anaphylatoxins released from c3 → c3a and c5 → c5a important in promoting inflammation and non-immune cell activation
  • complement products used in opsonisation and phagocytosis of microbes
    • asplenic patients are at risk of incapsulated organisms - e.g. neisseria, HiB, pneumococcus
  • adaptive immune system activation

Complement regulators

- regulators exist which cleave convertases, displace c3 convertase, inhibit C9 addition to MAC complex (stopping complement activation) - example - C1 inhibitor deficiency - leads to hereditary angiooedema - absence of C1 and its role in complement is not the cause of the angioedema - it is due to C1's role in the fibrinolytic pathway

- C1 inhibitor inhibits kallikrein from releasing bradykinin - removing the inhibition results in bradykinin release → increased vascular permeability → angiooedema - Some clinical trials - BCX7353 - oral synthetic kallikrein inhibitor - Lanadelumab - humanised antibody inhibitor of kallikrein - plasma derived C1-inhibitor (IV or s/c) - Danazol - Hereditary vs. allergic angiooedema - hereditary - lack of urticaria, post-procedural (clotting activation), family history

- Classical pathway - triggered by immune complexes - role in clearing apoptotic cells - take away classical pathway - can lead to presentation of self antigens and autoimmunity → e.g. lupus - Alternative pathway - C3 and role in clearance of bacterial infection (esp neisseria) - missing properdin, factor B/D - more prone to get pyogenic infections including neisseria infections - missing regulatory products (e.g. factor H) - increased complement deposition → predisposed to aHUS, C3G (saturation of complement) - CD55 and CD59 - found on endothelial cells; absence leads to PNH - new CD55 deficiency can lead to IBD

The inflammasome

- more than one signal needed for inflammasome activation - done to prevent overactivation - 2 signals to indicate danger: 1. TLR activation - pro-IL1b release 2. DAMP/PAMP presence - More than one inflammasome - all activate caspase 1 → cleave pro-IL1B → activates IL1B → induce inflammation - stimulates BM release of neutrophils - acts on endothelial cells to release IL6 - works on liver to induce acute phase response, CRP release, albumin and ferritin decrease

- Inflammasomopathies - can lead to autoinflammation - more relevant to paeds but sometimes presents in adults (esp. hyperIgG and familial mediterranean fever syndrome) - Schnitzler's syndrome - acquired systemic autoinflammatory disorder - can target IL-1R as primary cytokine of interest is IL-1B - Canakinumab, Anakinra, rilonacept - blocks IL1 from working at receptor site - only anakinra available in Australia - Inflammasome responsible in - metabolic syndrome - macrophage release of IL1B driving insulin resistance and atherosclerotic vascular deposition, beta cell death - Gout - Alzheimer's disease - Adult Still's - Behcet's - Rheumatoid Arthritis

Interferonopathies

  • inappropriate Type 1 IFN production
    • normally have sensors in cell which pick up nucleic acids for degradation/clearance
    • defects in sensors can lead to inappropriate release of type 1 interferons → induces inflammation response

- monogenic lupus - treatment can simply be blocking IFN signalling with JAK1/STAT inhibition

Relopathies

  • new, overactivation of NKfB (which regulates B-cell survival and generates inflammation)
    • defects in some of these proteins/mutations can result in overactive NFkB and inflammation

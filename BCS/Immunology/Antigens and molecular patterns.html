Antigens

  • Antigen - substance triggering immune response to constitute memory - can result in long life memory
  • complete vs. incomplete (haptens)
  • t-dependent vs t-independent
    • majority are t-dependent - requires helper t-cells to constitute memory
    • t-independent can activate B-cells on their own
      • Type 1 t-independent (PAMPs)
      • type 2 t-indenendent - highly repetitive surface epitopes (e.g. polysaccharide of encapsulated bacteria) - can activate only mature B-cells
        • children aged 4-6 with mainly immature b-cells cannot adequately defend against encapsulated bacteria for this reason
  • forms
    • pathogens
    • allergens
    • tumour antjgens (former self antigens)
    • autoantigens (self antigens)
  • Descriptors of antigens
    • Immunogen - triggers immune response (e.g. vaccines)
    • tolerogen - triggers immune TOLERANCE (e.g. specific immunological silence)
  • antigenicity - quality of antigen to serve as a ligand for a receptor (incl. TCR, BCR)
  • specificty - antigen to be unique for only one receptor
    • specificity occurs towards the epitope (may be dominant or latent)
  • Immunogenicity - quality to induce adaptive immune responses of different power

  • Can be recognised by

    • T-cell receptor (TCR)
    • B-cell receptor (BCR)

Molecular Patterns

  • Molecular patterns - low molecular substances evoking innate immunity w/ no memory response
  • Types
    • pathogen associated (PAMP) - a conserved motif on small exogenous molecuels linked to components of microbes
      • flagellin, peptidoglycan, lipopolysaccharide (endotoxin, LPS), viral dsRNA, unmethylated CgP motifs
      • may initiate different reactions of innate immunity and do not induce immune memory
    • allergen associated (AAMP)
      • exogenous molecules promote cross-reactivity of IgE allergy
    • Damage associated (DAMP)
      • endogenous molecules released outside of cell because of injury
      • heat shock protein, ECM proteins, S100, hyaluronan fragments, nonprotein substances (DNA, ATP, uric acid, heparin)
      • Clinically - in cases of severe damage to own tissue, may exacerbate immune responses
    • tumour-associated (TAMP)
      • low molecular conserved components of tumour cells
      • can upregulate innate defence, but can also promote growth and metastasis through weakening immune surveillance, chemoresistance and chronic inflammation
  • All molecular patterns result in innate immunity responses. They are recognised by:
    • Toll-like receptors (TLRs)
    • Pattern recognition receptors (PRRs)

Pharmacokinetic basics

Drug movement

  1. Permeation
    1. Aqueous diffusion - governed by Fick's law
    2. Lipid diffusion - governed by Fick's law
    3. Carrier transport - NOT governed by Fick's law
    4. Endocytosis - allows for permeation of very large protein molecules into cells
  2. Fick's Law of Diffusion
  3. Water and lipid solubility
  4. Henderson-Hasselbach equation
\[ log \left( \frac{protonised}{unprotonised}\right) = pK_a - pH \]

Metabolism

Elimination

  • First order elimination
    • elimination is concentration dependent
    • exponential decrease in elimination rate
    • constant half-life
  • zero-order elimination
    • constant elimination rate due to saturation of elimination receptors
    • constant rate of elimination
    • e.g. ethanol, phenytoin, aspirin (above toxic/therapeutic concentrations)

A note on ionic charge and lipophilicity

  • The charged form of a molecule is more hydrophilic and lipophobic than the uncharged form
  • for a weak acid (HA)
    • the unionised form (HA) is more lipophilic
    • the ionised form (A-) is more hydrophilic → when put in a BASIC solution, this will be the primary state
  • for a weak base (B)
    • the unionised form (B) is more lipophilic
    • the ionised form (BH+) is more hydrophilic → when put in an ACIDIC solution, this will be the primary state

How does this clinically play a role?

  • Consider aspirin (a weak acid)
    • when filtered out in the kidneys, aspirin will be in the ultrafiltrate
    • in the case of an overdose, we want aspirin to REMAIN in the ultrafiltrate
    • the kidneys are able to actively resorb lipophilic substances by lipid diffusion → therefore we want aspirin to remain in the ionised (and therefore more hydrophilic) form
    • therefore we try to ALKALINISE (make basic) the urine
  • Consider an overdose of Tolbutamide, a weak acid of pKa of 5.3
    • alkalinising the urine will increase its excretion

Antibiotics

Antibiotics for common infections

  • Staphycoccus Aureus - bone, heart, skin, joint
    • MSSA:
      • oxacillin/cloxacillin/nafcillin/dicloxacillin or cefazolin (1st generation cephalosporin)
        • you can use 1st gen cefalosporin if there is a RASH to penicillin
          • cefazolin IV
          • cephalexin PO
        • if you have anaphylaxis to penicillin, CANNOT use cephalosporin
      • note that ALL cephalosporines will cover a sensitive staphylococcus
        • however no point in using a 3rd/4th generation cephalosporine because it gives extra gram negative coverage (too broad spectrum with no benefit)
      • failure rate of vancomycin for MSSA is 2x that of oxacillin/nafcillin
    • MRSA:
      • Severe infection:
        • Vancomycin IV
        • linezolid - thrombocytopenia;
          • can cause serotonin syndrome if using MAOi (e.g. psychiatric patient): abdominal pain, sweating, nausea
          • PD (selegiline, risegoline) - serotonin syndrome
        • tedizolid - tedizolid is like linezolid without the most common adverse effect (thrombocytopenia)
        • daptomycin - if cannot use vancomycin (due to toxicity) or vancomycin insensitive
          • associated with myopathy and rising CK (Rhabdomyolysis)
          • avoid mixing with statins
        • ceftaroline - only cephalosporine which covers MRSA; also can be used for vancomycin insensitive staph aureus (used by itself or as an adjunct)
        • dalbavancin, oritavancin, telavancin - long acting versions of vancomycin
          • can be used as single dose IV medications lasting 1-2 weeks - allows discharge home
        • tigecycline - derivative of tetracyclines; will not work for MRSA bacteraemia (only for skin and soft tissue)
      • Minor infections (e.g. impetigo, cellulitis)
        • trimethoprim/sulfamethoxazole - ~70% efficacious
        • doxycycline - ~70% efficacious
        • clindamycin - ~70% efficacious
        • linezolid - both IV and oral
          • best efficacy of oral drugs
        • tedizolid
          • best efficacy of oral drugs
      • NEVER use tigecycline for bactaraemia - simply not strong enough (only for skin and soft tissue infections)
  • Streptococcus / enterococcus
    • all the above drugs can be used for streptococcus too
    • Penicillin can be used - penicillin G or VK
    • Ampicillin/Amoxicillin
    • Infective endocarditis prophylaxis for dental procedures: Bloody procedure, previous endocarditis, prosthetic valves, unrepaired cyanotic heart disease, transplant
      • prophylaxis can be done with amoxicillin
  • Gram negative bacilli - E.coli, Enterobacter, Citrobacter, Morganella, Pseudomonas, Serratia, Proteus, Klebsiella
    • lives in bowel/faeces
    • Covered by
      • Cephalosporines - cefepime, ceftazidime
      • Penicillins - piperacillin, ticarcillin
        • also covers streptococci and anaerobes; but only use if you want to cover gram negative rods
      • Monobactam - aztreonam
        • no cross-reaction with aztrenam and penicillins
      • Quinolones - ciprofloxacin, levofloxacin, moxifloxacin, gemifloxacin
        • also excellent pneumococcal coverage
        • gemifloxacin also covers pneumococcus
      • Aminoglycosides - gentamicin, tobramycin, amikacin
        • works synergistically with beta lactam to treat strep and staph
          • beta lactam pokes a hole in the wall of the bacteria and aminioglycoside kills the ribosome
      • Carbapenems - Imipenem, Meropenem, Ertapenem, Doripenem
        • should be only used in shocked patients with coverage for steph/staph/gram negative rod
        • cannot be used in penicillin anaphylaxis too
        • ertapenem is the only carbapenem not covering pseudomonas
        • same antianaeobic cover as metronidazole and clindamycin → no point adding
    • note aztreonam and aminoglycosides are used ONLY for gram negatives
  • ESBL - resistant to cephalosporines and penicillins and aztreonam
    • treat with carbapenems OR ceftolozane-tazobactam OR ceftazidime-avibactam
    • polymyxin/colistin - for carbapenem resistant gram negative rods
      • dont use polymyxin systemically (only topically) → can cause renal toxicity (acts like a detergent)
      • used as a last line therapy → due to high toxicity
      • would be used in failed therapy for ventilator associated pneumonia - strongly active against MDR gram negative rods
  • MRSA quinolone
    • only quinolone which covers MRSA - delafloxacin AND gram negative bacilli
  • Anaerobes
    • gastrointestinal anaerobes (Bacteroides)
      • metronidazole - abdominal anaerobes
        • not good for mouth
      • carbapenem, piperacillin, ticarcillin - equal efficacy for abdominal anaerobes as metronidazole
      • cefoxitin and cefotetan - only cephalosporines which cover anaerobes
        • note cefoxin and cefotetan (2nd gen cephalopsorines), has some gram negative cover
        • useful in coverage of female genital infections (including doxycycline) - used in PID (pelvic anaerobes)
    • respiratory anaeobes (anaerobic strep)
      • clindamycin - best drug for anaerobic strep
    • medications with no anaerobic coverage - aminoglycosides, cephalosporines (Except 2nd gen), aztreonam, oxacillin/naficillin/cloxacillin/dicloxacillin

Combinations with beta lactamase inhibitors

  • look like a beta-lactam
  • beta-lactamase sees the beta-lactamase inhibitors
    • clavulanate, sulbactam, tazobactam, avibactam, vaborbactam
    • cleaves the beta lactam ring within the beta lactamase inhibitor (i.e. acts as a decoy) → they are destroyed instead
  • also known as 'suicide inhibitors' → purposefully destroyed for the antibiotic to act on the bacteria
  • additional coverage against sensitive staphylococci and some gram negative bacilli
  • does not cover MRSA
  • useful in sinusitis, abscesses, oral infections, otitis media, human or animal bites
    • covers anaerobes
  • meropenem-vaborbactam - for carbapenem resistant bugs

Penicillin allergies

  • anaphylaxis - hypotension, shortness, confusion
    • cannot use cephalosporines or carbapenems
  • allergy - no hemodynamic disruption
  • cephalosporines are OK in rashes
  • severe reactions (anaphylaxis) → must use
    • vancomycin
    • linezolid/tedizolid
    • daptomycin
    • telavancin, dalbavancin, oritavancin
  • minor reactions
    • clindamycin
    • TMP/SMX

Beta lactam antibiotics

  • inhibits cell wall synthesis by binding PBP
  • 4 classes are
    • penicillin
    • cephalosporins
    • carbapenem
    • monobactam
  • these 4 classes will NOT be synergistic → they work the same way!
    • have cross-reactivity but no synergy
    • aztreonam (monobactam) and penicillin will not be cross reactive at all

Vancomycin and its derivatives

  • Vancomycin, telavancin, dalbavancin, oritavancin
  • bactericidal lipopolysaccharides
  • inhibits cell wall synthesis - binds to D-Ala-D-Ala terminus and replaces it with a D-Ala-D-?Leu
  • increased risk of AKI when combined with pip/taz

Cephalosporines

  • inhibits cell wall growth by binding to the penicillin-binding protein (transpeptidase)

Linezolid

  • only inhibits protein synthesis → makes it only bacteriostatic
  • inhibits the ribosome
  • not the second choice for MRSA bactaraemia as it is bacteriostatic (daptomycin is 2nd line)

Daptomycin

  • doesn't cover the lungs → inactivated by surfactant
    • if MRSA pneumonia, daptomycin cannot be used as second line after vancomycin
    • in this case, should be using ceftaroline

TMP-SMX

  • folate antagonist

Tetracycline antibiotics

Fluroquinolones

  • inhibition of DNA gyrase (topoisomerases)
    • DNA cannot unwind (for reproducing base pairs)

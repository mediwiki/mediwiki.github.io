Antiarrhythmics

Background

  • Classification system (Vaughan Williams system)
    • Class 1: Sodium channel blockade
    • Class 2: Beta receptor blockade
    • Class 3: K+ channel blockade
    • Class 4: Ca2+ channel blockade
  • Disadvantages of this system
    • Some drugs have more than one class of action
    • Indication cannot always be easily deduced from class
    • Drugs don't always fall into a class at all
  • Alternative system - Sicilian Gambit system

Use dependence

  1. Use-dependence (Rate dependence)
    • Increased action at increased heart rates
      • Seen in Ic Class I Antiarrhythmics (Na+ channel blockade)
      • Seen in Ca2+ channel blockers
  2. Reverse Use-dependence (Reverse Rate dependence)
    • Increased action at slower heart rate
      • Seen in Class III Antiarrhythmics (K+ channel blockers), excluding amiodarone, but also including K+ blocking effects in Class Ia drugs
    • Thus more likely to be effective at slower HRs, but also likely to cause torsades

Class I Antiarrhythmics (Na+ channel blockade)

  • More effect in Fast AP (Atria, ventricles, His/purkinjie system)
    • Slows the upward (Phase 0) slope of AP
  • As it preferentially affects the fast AP , it widens the QRS complex

Class II Antiarrhythmics (Beta blockers)

  • Antagonises Adr and NAd
    • slows Ca2+ current into cell
    • effectively the same effect as class IV antiarrhythmics
  • Greater effect in slow AP - will slow SA and AV nodes
    • Slows sinus rhythm and prolongs PR interval

[[Class III Antiarrhythmics (K+ channel blockers)]]

  • Slows down repolarisation
    • Thus prolongs action potential
  • delays repolarisation
    • As T waves indicate ventricular repolarisation, this will therefore prolong QT interval

Class IV Antiarrhythmics (Ca2+ channel blockers)

  • Slows depolarisation in slow AP
  • Greater effect in slow AP - will slow SA and AV nodes
    • Slows sinus rhythm and prolongs PR interval

Unclassified Antiarrhythmics

  • Digoxin
  • Adenosine
  • Atropine
  • Isoproterenol (Isoprenaline)
  • Ivabradine
  • Magnesium

Selection of antiarrhythmics

Atrial fibrillation / Atrial Flutter RATE CONTROL

  • Acute management / inpatients
    • Heart failure or LV dysfunction?
      • No -> Diltiazem/Verapamil or cardioselective beta blockers (negative inotropes)
      • Yes -> Amiodarone, Digoxin or cardioversion (note digoxin has long onset of action and will take time, consider risk of amiodarone toxicity)
  • Long term management / outpatients
    • Heart failure or LV dysfunction?
      • No -> Diltiazem/Verapamil or cardioselective beta blockers
      • Yes -> Beta blockers (First line), digoxin (second line)

Atrial fibrillation / Atrial Flutter RHYTHM CONTROL (cardioversion)

  1. Is the patient hemodynamically stable?
    • No -> ELECTRICAL CARDIOVERSION
    • Yes -> Does the patient have structural heart disease (E.g. CAD, HF)?
      • No -> 1c agents (Flecainide, Propafenone) - will need pretreating with Class II or Class IV antiarrhythmic
      • Yes -> Amiodarone

Atrial fibrillation / Atrial Flutter MAINTENENCE (AFTER cardioversion)

  • No other significant CVD -> 1c agents (flecainide, propafenone), sotalol
  • Significant LVH -> Amiodarone
  • CAD -> Dofetilide, Sotalol
  • HF -> Amiodarone, Dofetilide

Monomorphic Ventricular Tachycardia RHYTHM CONTROL (cardioversion)

  • Hemodynamically stable?
    • No -> ELECTRICAL CARDIOVERSION
    • Yes -> Amiodarone, Lidocaine, Procainamide, can still consider electrical cardioversion

RISK OF RECURRENT Monomorphic Ventricular Tachycardia RHYTHM CONTROL (cardioversion)

  • ICD candidate?
    • Yes -> ICD +/- beta blocker
    • No (e.g. limited life expectancy) -> beta blocker
  • Patient still experiencing VT?
    • Add amiodarone (decreases rate, but be aware that ICD may not recognise slow VT when adding amiodarone)
    • OR switch beta blocker to sotalol
    • OR add sotalol to beta blocker

Miscellaneous drug-indication pairings

  • Termination of AVNRT:
    • Vagal maneouvre -> Adenosine
  • Termination of orthodromic AVRT
    • Vagal manouvre -> Adenosine
  • Termination of antidromic AVRT
    • Vagal manouvre -> Procainamide
  • Cardioversion of AF in setting of WPW (i.e. pre-excited AF)
    • Procainamide
  • Prevention of VT/VF in Brugada syndrome
    • Amiodarone OR quinidine
  • Prevention of AVRT (either orthodromic or antidromic)
    • Metoprolol
  • Prevention of pre-excited AF (without structural heart disease)
    • Flecainide or propafenone

Antiarrhythmic pitfalls

  1. Treating unknown wide complex tachycardia as anything OTHER than VT
    • i.e. should always treat wide complex tachycardia as VT
  2. Treating AF in patient with WPW with a beta blocker or Ca2+ channel blocker
    • These will slow down AV node conduction
    • this will paradoxically increase conduction down accessory pathway
    • Can further accelerate HR
    • Thus need to be treated with drugs which can both slow down accessory pathway AND AV nodal conduction (i.e. procainamide)
  3. Treating Atrial flutter with Na+ channel blocking drug without concurrent beta blocker or CCB
    • can slow the rate to a low enough that a 2:1 block becomes a 1:1 block which conducts
    • Need to start beta blocker or CCB before or at same time

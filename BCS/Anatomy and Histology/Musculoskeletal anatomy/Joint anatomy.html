Joint anatomy

Background

  • defined as where two bones connect

Histological classification

  1. Fibrous
    • units bones together by fibrous tissue
    • doesn't necessarily enable movement
    • Examples
      • skull sutures
      • tooth roots
      • inferior tibiofibular joint
  2. Cartilaginous (primary)
    1. Connecting bones via hyaline cartilage
    2. Also doesn't necessarily enable movement
    3. Examples
      1. Between epiphysis and metaphysis (i.e. the physis/growth plate)
      2. Between rib and costal cartilage
  3. Cartilaginous (Secondary) - symphyseal joints
    1. Bone surfaces covered with hyaline, but are joined with fibrocartilage
    2. Found in the midline of the body
    3. Enables mild movement
    4. Examples
      1. between vertebral bodies
      2. between pubic bones
  4. Synovial
    1. Bone surfaces covered by hyaline, but separated by a joint cavity and a capsule with synovial fluid
    2. Enables greatest range of motion
    3. Examples
      1. limb joints
      2. Temporomandibular joint
    4. Mechanical classification of synovial joints
      1. Ball and socket joint
        • 3 axes of movement
        • e.g. shoulder joint
      2. Ellipsoid joint
        • 2 axes of movement
        • e.g. wrist joint
      3. Saddle joint
        • 2 axes of movement
        • e.g. first CMC joint at base of thumb
      4. Hinge joint
        • 1 axis of movement
        • e.g. elbow joint
      5. Pivot joint
        • 1 axis of movement (rotation)
        • e.g. proximal R-U joint and atlantoaxial joint
      6. Plane joint
        • no real axis of movement
        • e.g. facet joint, intercarpal joints

Ligaments

  • strong, short bands of connective tissue
  • connects boen to bone
  • helps stabilise joints

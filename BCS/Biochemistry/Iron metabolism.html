Iron metabolism

Iron intake

  • Dietary iron (Fe3+) is reduced to Fe2+ in the gut
  • Fe2+ is transported by DMT1 (divalent metal transporter 1) found in the enterocyte (cells of the GIT) INTO the enterocyte
    • Iron is absorbed in the duodenum and proximal jejunal segments of the gut
  • Fe2+ is exported OUT of the enterocyte via the basolateral membrane via ferroportin, or it is stored as ferritin
  • ???binding to transferrin
  • Fe2+ can be transported out of the enterocyte, oxidised to Fe3+ and bind transferrin for mobilisation in bloodstream

Iron circulation

  • Transferrin-iron complexes circulate in the blood and bind to Transferrin receptor 1 (TFR1) on other organs
    • Transferrin-iron complexes are endocytosed (Via receptor mediated endocytosis)
    • TFR1 is expressed on many cell types and is regulated by local iron demands
      • high iron demand = higher TFR1 expression
      • mediated by iron regulatory proteins intracellularly

Iron storage

  • Iron is stored in the enterocytes as ferritin
  • Iron is stored in the liver as ferritin (after being transported over)
  • Iron is stored in bone marrow and reticular/endothelial system due to high turnover of iron used for blood
    • iron is stripped from old/damaged erythrocytes with help from splenic macrophages
      • haem oxidase 1 in splenic macrophages strip iron from haem
      • stripped iron is then stored as ferritin, or released into circulation

Iron regulation

  • iron is regulated at systemic level by hepcidin (produced by hepatocytes)
    • hepcidin increases with hepatic ferritin stores are high (??? is this true)
    • hepcidin has an inhibitory function on
      • iron absorption at the enterocyte level
      • formation of transferrin-iron complexes from hepatic ferritin stores

Function of iron

  • key component of haemoglobin in red blood cells, myoglobin, metalloproteins and enzymes
  • used for oxygen uptake and tissue delivery

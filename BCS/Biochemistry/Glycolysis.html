Glycolysis

Background

Glycolysis is the breakdown of glucose to produce ATP

\[ \text{Glucose} + 2NAD^+ + 2ADP + 2P_i \rightarrow 2\text{Pyruvate} + 2NADH + 2ATP + 2H_2O + 2H^+\]
\[\Delta G_1^{'o} = -146kJ/mol\]

In this process:

  • Glucose (6 carbons) into 2 pyruvates (2x 3-carbon molecules)
  • Utilise 2 ATP (investment phase) to produce 4 ATP -> NET production of 2 ATP
  • Reduction of \(NAD^+\) to \(NADH\)
    • gains a \(H^+\) and an \(e^-\)
    • NADH can be oxidised in the Electron Transport Chain to produce ATPs later
  • The final carbon compound is pyruvate
    • this contains most of the chemical potential of glucose which is then extracted in the citric acid cycle and oxidate phosphorylation
    • pyruvate can also be used in [[fermentation]] to produce lactic acid in the body
    • pyruvate can also be used to synthesis alanine (Amino acid) or in [[fatty acid synthesis]]

Phases of glycolysis

Phase 1: Preparatory phase - Gluc

  1. Glucose is phosphorylated at the hydroxyl group on C6 by hexokinase to form Glucose 6-phosphate (g6p)
    • This phosphorylation step utilises ATP as a phosphate donor
    • this first phosphorylation event can only happen on C6 as C1 is a carbonyl group which cannot be phosphorylated
  2. g6p is converted to fructose 6-phosphate (f6p) by phosphohexose isomerase
    • This step is necessary to free up the C1 carbonyl groupinto a hydroxyl group (for further phosphorylation)
  3. f6p is phosphorylated at c1 to form fructose 1,6-biphosphate (f1,6p) by phosphofructokinase-1
    • This phosphorylation step utilises ATP as a phosphate donor
    • This step allows phosphorylation of the newly formed hydroxyl group
  4. f1,6p is split to form two three-carbon molecules: dihydroxyacetone phosphoate and glyceraldehyde 3-phosphate by adolase
  5. Dihydroxyacetone phosphate can isomerise to form glyceraldehyde 3-phosphate by triose phosphate isomerase
    • These two molecules simply differ from the location of the carbonyl group - you want the carbonyl group to be at the end (i.e. an aldehyde) rather than in the middle (i.e. an acetone)

Phase 2: Payoff phase

  1. Glyceraldehyde 3-phosphate is oxidised and phosphorylated by inorganic phosphate (NOT ATP) by glyceraldehyde 3-phosphate dehydrogenase to form 1,3-bisphosphoglycerate
  2. Each 1,3-bisphosphoglycerate is converted by phosphoglycerate kinase into 3-phosphoglycerate
  3. conversion by phosphoglycerate mutase into 2-phosphoglycerate
  4. conversion by enolase into phosphoenolpyruvate
  5. conversion by pyruvate kinase into pyruvate

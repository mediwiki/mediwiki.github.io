Renal replacement therapy

Background

  • slow to resolve AKIs may result in toxin/electrolyte/metabolite buildup which can result in life threatening complications
    • fluid overload → pulmonary odema and cardiorenal syndrome
    • electrolyte buildup → arrhythmias
    • toxin buildup → encephalopathy and other end organ injury

Physical principles

  1. Removing fluid/water (ultrafiltration)
    1. transmembrane pressure generation (i.e. pushing blood against a membrane until transmembrane pressure overcomes oncotic pressure)
    2. osmosis (i.e. increasing dialysate pressure) to drive water across semipermeable membrane
  2. removing solutes
    1. solvent drag /convection - transmembrane pressure generation pushes water and drags solutes with it
    2. diffusion - electrochemical gradient drives solvent diffusion across membrane

Indications for RRT

  1. Anuria/oliguria (UO < 200mL/12h)
  2. Electrolyte derangement
    • K+ > 6.5mmol/L or rapidly rising
    • Na < 110 or >160mmol/L
  3. Intoxication with dialyzable toxin (e.g. lithium)
  4. Overload - Pulmonary oedema unresponsive to diuretics
  5. Uremia
    1. Urea > 35mmol/L
    2. Uremic complications (encephalopathy, myopathy, neuropathy, pericarditis)
  6. Other new modern criteria
    1. uncompensated metabolic acidosis (pH < 7.1)
    2. temperature > 40C

Continuous renal replacement therapy (CRRT)

Definitions

  • Continuous veno-venous haemofiltration (CVVH) - convection only
    • no dialysate is used and effluent is replaced with substitution solutions pre-filter
    • ultrafiltration rates of 2L/h can clear urea at 25mL/kg/hr
  • Continuous veno-venous haemodilaysis (CVVHD) - diffusion only
    • countercurrent dialysate delivered to blood flow to allow pure diffusive clearance
  • Continuous veno-venous haemodiafiltration (CVVHDF) - convection and diffusion
    • countercurrent dialysis to achieve mixed diffusive and convective clearance

RRT considerations

  1. Line clotting
    • high flow blood through filter has increased clotting risk
    • the line therefore can be anticoagulated to prevent damaging the filter (normally can last ~ 24h) - options include:
      • No anticoagulation - done in 10-20% of patients due to endogenous severe coagulopathy or recent surgery or inability to metabolise citrate; aim for blood flow of ~ 200mL/min
      • Anticoagulation with low dose pre-filter heparin (<500U) - typically won't affect a patient's coagulation studies
      • medium dose pre-filter heparin (500-1000U)
      • full heparinsation - necessary when concomitant indications exist e.g. PE, MI
      • LMWH - needs to be adjusted for renal function
      • Regional citrate anticoagulation - requires special dialysate / replacement fluid; considered the safest and most effective way of achieving anticoagulation
        • can possible induce coagulopathy in impaired liver function - look for increasing base deficit, increasing calcium requirements to maintain target calcaemia and widening of total Ca : ionised Ca ratio
        • will need Mg supplementation
      • Regional pre-filter heparin/ post-filter protamine anticoagulation - if further patient anticoagulation is dangerous
      • heparinoids and prostacycline - if patient has developed heparin-induced thrombocytopoenia and thrombosis in past
  2. Membrane choice
    • no evidence to suggest one confers a clinical advance over another
    • AN69 most common CRRT membrane in Australia
    • no evidence RE membrane size
    • high volume hemofiltration is planned - aim membrane surface of 1.6-2m^2 range
  3. CRRT dose (defined as effective effluent/kg/hr)
    • no evidence to suggest increasing intesity improves outcomes
    • aim 25-30mL/kg/hr

Intermittent haemodialysis

IH considerations

  1. dialysate flow rate
    • aim for high dialysate flows (>400mL/min) and generated by mixing concentrate WITH purified water
      • applied for 3-4 hours every 2-4 days → results in rapid volume loss and repeat hypotension
      • episodic solute removal results in inferior uremic and acid-base control
      • rapid solute shifts can increase brain water content and raise intracranial pressure ('dialysis disequilibrium syndrome')

Slow Low Efficiency Daily Dialysis (SLEDD)

Definition

  • hybrid of IH and CRRT - daily dialysis

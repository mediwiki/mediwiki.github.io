Hematuria

Background

  • Defined as > 3 erythyocytes/hpf in urine sediment
  • Can be microscopy (detectable only on urine testing, e.g. dipstix) or macroscopic (Grossly visible)
  • Source
    • Most commonly non-glomerular
    • Glomerular origin supported by
      • proteinuria, dysmorphic RBCs, increased SCr or systemic signs/symptoms

Differentials for GROSS haematuria

  1. Kidney
    • Renal cancer
      • Most common - Renal cell carcinoma
    • Acute pyelonephritis
    • Polycystic kidney disease
  2. Ureter
    • Nephrolithiasis
  3. Bladder
    • Acute cystitis
    • Urothelial cancer - note, do not use urinalysis for SCREENING in asymptomatic adults for bladder cancer
  4. Prostate
    • Prostate cancer
    • Prostatitis
    • Benign prostatic hyperplasia
  5. Urethra
    • Trauma
    • Urethral strictures
  6. External urinary tract
    • Menstruation
  7. Other
    • Pseudohematuria
    • Exercise induced
    • Benign essential haematuria - diagnosis of exclusion

Targeted history

  • Occupational exposures (e.g. paint, fumes)
  • Travel exposures (e.g. schistosomiasis)
  • Dietary history (E.g. beets, rhubarb)
  • Medications history
  • Urine specific questions
    • Dysuria - strongly suggests infection or urinary obstruction - consider Prostatitis, Acute pyelonephritis, Nephrolithiasis
    • urinary frequency/urgency/nocturia
    • Incomplete emptying, slow stream
    • Colour of urine - darker urine can be seen in Biliary obstruction
    • Hematuria at beginning (initial hematuria - suggestive of urethral disease) or end (terminal hematuria - suggestive of bladder/prostate pathology) or total (kidney, ureter disease)

Investigations

  1. Urine Dipstick (Urinalysis) to confirm hematuria
  2. Urine MCS to quantitatively and qualitatively assess RBC/HPF and bacteria, cell casts, etc.
    • absence of proteinura generally rules out glomerular processes (except mild glomerular disease/mild IgA nephropathy or thin GBM disease due to a type IV collagen defect)
  3. Bloods
    1. FBC - for WCC (infection), Hb (anemia), Plt (thrombocytopenia)
    2. EUC - renal function
    3. Coags - R/O coagulpathy
    4. PSA - PCa, can be elevated in instrumentation or UTI
  4. Imaging
    • Concern of Nephrolithiasis - CT KUB (Kidney Ureter Bladder)
    • Concern for cancer - CT urogram, cystourethroscopy

Management

  • Clot retention or ongoing significant hematuria - 3-way catheter with continuous irrigation
  • Treat primary cause
  • Cystoscopy
  • macroscopic hematuria should prompt urology referral even if self-limited
    • Low risk for malignancy - repeat urinalysis within 6 months and no additional evaluation if normal; consider immediate kidney ultrasound +/- cystoscopy
      • Women < 50 and men < 40 who have never smoked
      • 301- erythocytes/hpf noted on single urinalaysis
      • no additional risk factors for urothelial cancer
    • intermediate risk (positive at 6mo for low risk) - prompr kidney US and cystoscopy
      • < 25 erythrocytes/hpf
      • all others
    • high risk - cystoscopy and CT urography / MR urography

      • 25 erythocytes/hpf

      • 60 years old

      • 30 pyhx smoking

      • gross hematuria

Things to be aware of

  • Do not place IDC in trauma patients where blood is noted at the urethral meatus - possible urethral injury
    • can result in a complete transection - need to rule out injury with retrograde urethrogram
      • Retrograde urethrograms must be performed in
      • Blood at meatus
      • High riding prostate
      • Open book pelvic fracture

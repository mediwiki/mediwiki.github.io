Skin grafting

Background

  • graft vs. flap
    • flap = tissue moved from one site of body to another, taken with its own blood supply
    • graft is similar but without its own blood supply
  • basically all tissues can be grafted across the body except for muscle (it is a functional unit, it will NOT regenerate, it will only form scar tissue) - muscle CAN however be moved as a flap
  • Types of skin grafts
    • split thickness - epidermis and some dermis harvested
      • donor site heals within 2wk by immigration of epidermal cells in the hair follicles, and other adnexal structures in the dermis
      • different from healing by secondary intention - will NOT cause ulcer because the dermis is left behind (and the dermis will grow to refill the graft donor)
      • not as cosmetically good as full thickness graft, but it is easier to take (easier to survive), and will contract and become more pigmented
    • full thickness - entire dermis harvested
      • does not heal by itself due to loss of dermis - needs a primary closure or graft
      • looks cosmetically better (preferred over split thickness graft)
      • cannot be done if it is a big size graft
    • composite graft - has skin and some other tissue
      • e.g. cartilage or fat or hair
      • not typically done
  • Skin grafts cannot be placed over bare:
    • bone
    • tendon
    • cartilage
    • needs periostium, perichondrium or partenon (bare bone and cartilage do not have blood supply)
  • Care also must be taken to prevent:
    • graft shearing (damage to newly formed blood vessels)
    • haematoma/seroma under graft - this can lift the graft and damage blood vessels
    • infection
  • tissue expansion
    • slow expansion of a tissue stimulates mitosis and generates more tissue
    • balloon is placed under skin and slowly expanded
    • a permanent implant can be placed afterwards
    • e.g. done after mastectomy for breast reconstruction

Skin flaps

Background

  • local - flap from adjacent area to the wound
    • flap from globella area over
    • wound on medial canthal edge of nose
    • globellar flap stited across
  • regional - from the same region as the wound
    • e.g. forehead flap for nose reconstruction
    • e.g. pectoral flap raised to reconstruct neck/jaw area
    • pedicle can eventually be discarded
  • distant (microsurgical)
    • e.g. abdominal tissue used to reconstruct breast tissue

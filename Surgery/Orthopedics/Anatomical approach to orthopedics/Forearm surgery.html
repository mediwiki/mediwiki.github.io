Forearm surgery

Distal radius fractures

  • Functional anatomy
    • composed of 3 concave articular surfaces
      • Scaphoid fossae
      • Lunae fossae
      • Sigmoid notch
    • Stability maintained by intrinsic and extrinsic ligaments
      • primary intrinsic ligament injuired is the Scapholunate
      • most common intra-articular sift tissue injury is the TFCC (NOT scaphoid ligament)
        • treated non-operatively unless DRUJ unstable
  • Distinct groups injured
    • Young - high energy
    • Elderly - low energy
  • Normal paramters
    • 11mm radial height
    • 22 degrees radial inclination
    • 11 degrees volar tilt
  • Treatment
    • Non-operative
      • Non-displaced/reduced fractures with OK alignment
      • risk of extensor pollicus longus rupture 3-4 months down the track
        • treatment - transfer from Extensor indicus to extensor pollicus longus tendons
      • Age is primary determinant to maintain fracture alignment
        • In Patients > 65
          • operative - improve gripstrength and improved XR, but no difference in functional outcomes
    • Operative
      • intra-articular distacement > 2mm
      • Post-reduction radial shortening > 3mm (risk of ulnar impaction)
      • Dorsal tilt > 10 degrees from neutral
      • Issues
        • Dorsal plating - risk of extensor tendon irritation/rupture (e.g. EPL)
        • Volar plating - risk of flexor tendon irritation/rupture (e.g. FPL, FDP index, EPL if screws long)
        • External fixation - carpal complex regional pain from over distraction
      • acute and delayed carpal tunnel syndrome → need to release carpal tunnel
      • CRPS - 50 days vitamin C 500mg PO QD can reduce incidence potentially

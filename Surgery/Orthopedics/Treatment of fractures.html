Fracture management

Reduce

Considerations

  1. Degree of displacement of fracture fragments
  2. If the bone were to heal in this position, would it be compatible with optimum function in short and long term?
  3. was this reduction adequate?

Open reduction

  • Surgically putting bones together by direct manipulation
  • If acceptable closed reduction is NOT achieved or UNLIKELY to succeed → open reduction - risk of stripping intact periosteum and devitalising fracture fragments - need to consider degree of residual displacement acceptable

Closed reduction

  • Indirect external manipulation of limb manually or with fracture table
  • articular joint surface involvement → need perfect reduction to original anatomical position
    • purpose: restore normal joint movement in short term
    • purpose: avoid degenerative joint disease in long term
    • intra-articular fracture → anatomical reduction important
  • extra-articular → restoration of mechanical alignment of joints above and below - fracture fragments need not be perfectly reduced (acceptable alignment, length, rotation acceptable)
    • extra-articualr fracture → mechanical alignment
    • extra-articular fractures in children have greater remodelling ability so greater displacement is acceptable

Hold

Consideration and goals

  • optimise biological and mechanical environments for favourable healing conditions
  • minimise period of disability by speedning healing process
  • methods of holding fragments in place
    • Plaster casts and splints
    • traction
    • Kirschner (K-) wires
    • External fixation
    • Plates and screws
    • Intramedullary nails
  • can consider arthroplasty where fragments cannot be held together

Internal fixation

  • Placement of instrumentation entirely within the body and under the skin with NO outside communication
  • typically left in place permanently

External fixation

  • placement of pins or wires into the bone
  • hardware extens outside the skin/body and commected to metal rods/rings outside the body to maintain alignment
  • ALWAYS removed at some point due to infection risk

Stability

  • Absolute stability - no displacement or movement - achieved by accurate anatomical reduction with compression across fracture fragments
    • desirably for intra-articular fractures
  • Relative stability - little movement at fracture site, optimising environment for callous formation and indirect bone healing

Rehabilitate

Tools

Plaster casts and splints

  • hold stable fracture or supplement fixation of unstable fractures (e.g. below elbow cast for distal radius fracture after K-wire fixation)
  • Two forms of plaster casts
    1. Plaster of Paris - preferred in acute fractures
      • backslab used given risk of sqelling in acute injuries and compartment syndrome
      • moulding of cast
        • 3-point moulding - used to control position
          • often uses intact dorsal peristeal hinge to mould against
          • often will look crooked - 'bent casts make straight bones'
    2. synthetic casting materials
  • Splints - commercially available, provide comfort/support and easy to remove/shower/bath with
  • Advantages of casting/splinting
    • no wound
    • no interference with fracture site
    • cheap
    • adjustable
    • no implants to remove
  • Disadvantages
    • limited access to soft tissue
    • interference with function
    • poor mechanical stability
    • 'plaster disease' - joint stiffness, muscle atrophy

General advice on fracture treatment

  1. Diaphyseal fractures
    • Extra-articular fracture → mechanicla reduction by correcting length, alignment, rotation
    • angular malunion → abnormal joint forces → pain and secondary degenerative joint disease
    • generally well suited to intramedullary fixation techniques
  2. Metaphyseal fractures
    • AO A: extra-articular
      • joint congruity not an issue
      • mechanical alignment, length, rotation need to be considered
      • less predictable with intramedullary nailing - therefore use plate and screws, or external fixation (or K-wires in smaller joints)
      • consideration given to stable fixation to allow early joint movement and rehabilitation
  3. Intra-articular fractures
    • AO B: Partial articular and AO C: Complete articular
      • principle of treatment - anatomical reduction of articular surface, rigid stabilisation
      • Aim: allow early joint movement, avoid degenerative joint disease
      • Considerations: increased wound complications of open surgery, devitalised bone fragments, excessive bone exposure
    • Osteoporotic intra-articular fractures - though anatomical reduction MAY be achieved, rigid fixation may cut out of soft bone - would consider using bone substitutes to fill bone voids and augment fixation
      • stable fixation not possible - consider non-operative treatment and delayed joint replacement
        • can also consider primary joint replacement
    • AO C: Complete articular, where articular sruface has separated from metaphysis
      • articular surface initially anatomically reduced with temporary K-wires or lag screws
      • then articular block reattached to the shaft

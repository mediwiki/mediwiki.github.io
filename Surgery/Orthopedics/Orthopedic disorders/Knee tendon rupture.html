ACL rupture

Background

  • 4-5x more common in women
    • likely due to smaller ACL and intercondylar notch, landing mechanisms (more valgus/extension on landing) and muscular imbalance (lower hamstring:quadriceps strength ratio)

Pathology

  • Contact pivoting injuries
    • direct blow to knee (e.g. lateral or hyperextension blow to knee) occurs (E.g. with helmet in football)
  • Non-contact pivoting injuries (MORE COMMON)
    • football, basketball, soccer
    • associated with awkward landings

Clinical features

  • Commonly associated with a 'popping' noise due to high ligamental tension prior to tear
  • positive Lachman's test (Anterior drawer test))

Diagnostic criteria

Investigations

  • XR of knee
    • Segond fracture is pathognomonic
  • MRI is best technique to evaluate ACL integrity

Management

  • Non-operative (conservative management) IF
    • Partial tear + nil instability
    • Complete tear + nil instability on low-demand sports + giving up high demant sports
    • do light manual work or live sedentary lifestyles
    • advanced osteoarthritis
    • significant comorbidities precluding surgery
  • Non-operative management includes activity modification, bracing, PT (muscle strengthening)
    • can lead to abnormal knee motion, increased future meniscal injury risk
  • Surgical management includes arthroscopic reconstruction of ACL (tendon graft in bone tunnel)
    • Repair of the native ligament DOES NOT HEAL due to (1) intra-articular location and (2) synovial fluid inhibiting healing
    • Graft selection
      • Autograft - from patient (bone-patellar, tendon-bone or hamstring tendon)
        • decreased risk of disease transmission or immunologic reaction, faster incorporation
        • donor-site morbidity (e.g. anterior knee pain, weak knee flexion, pain with hamstring graft), longer operating times
      • Allograft - from cadaver
        • prevents donor-site morbidity
        • risk of disease transmission - though processed by freezing/irradiating
    • Consideration needs to be made for adolescents as ACL surgery will risk growth plate damage and physeal arrest

Prognosis

  • Surgical risks include
    • postoperative infection, Septic arthritis (can damage cartilage and result in early Osteoarthritis)
    • knee stiffness
    • local saphenous neuroma formation (due to surgical incisions and graft failure)

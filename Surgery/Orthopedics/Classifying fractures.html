Classifying and describing fractures

Open fracture

  • A fracture which communicates with the outer environment due to disruption of the skin and soft tissue
  • Increased risk for
    • Osteomyelitis
    • Hardware infection
    • Slower bone healing (non-union risk increased)
  • ALL should receive antibiotics and are surgical emergencies

Gustilo-Anderson Grading system (grading for open fractures)

  • Increased grade = increased risk of

    • infection
    • nonunion
    • amputation

  • Type 1:

    • low energy fracture
    • wound size <1cm with minimal soft tissue damage
    • wound is clean and free of contamination
    • simple fracture pattern - minimal to nil comminution of bone and nil periosteal stripping
    • w/ intramedullary (IM) nailing, average time to union is 21-28 wks
  • Type 2:
    • moderate energy fracture
    • wound size > 1 cm with moderate soft tissue damage
    • moderate contamination of the wound bed
    • fracture pattern with moderate comminution of bone and nil periosteal stripping
    • w/ IM nailing, average time to union is 26-28 weeks
  • Type 3a:
    • high energy fracture
    • wound size > 10cm with extensive soft tissue damage
    • extensive contamination of the wound bed
    • fracture pattern demonstrating severe comminution of bone and periosteal stripping
    • Local skin coverage of the fracture site is usually possible
    • w/ IM nailing, average time to union is 30-35 weeks
  • Type 3b:
    • high energy fracture
    • wound size > 10cm with extensive soft tissue damage
    • extensive contamination of the wound bed
    • fracture pattern demonstrating severe comminution of bone and periosteal stripping
    • Local skin coverage of the fracture site is usually NOT possible, requires free tissue flap/rotational tissue flap for coverage
    • w/ IM nailing, average time to union is 30-35 weeks
  • Type 3c:
    • high energy fracture
    • wound size > 10cm with extensive soft tissue damage
    • extensive contamination of the wound bed
    • fracture pattern demonstrating severe comminution of bone and periosteal stripping
    • 3C fractures typically require free soft tissue flap coverage
    • Fractures of this sub-category involve major vascular injury which requires repair

Describing bone injury

Components of describing bony injury include:

  1. Name of bone
  2. Region of bone
  3. Pattern of fracture line: transverse, oblique, spiral, segmental, multifragmentary
  4. Presence of compression: Compression fractures where cancellous bone collapses, vertebral wedge compression fracture
  5. Presence of displacement of fracture fragments, displaced/undisplaced
  6. Type and degree of displacement
    • Angulation
    • translation
    • rotation
    • shortening
  7. Presence of pre-existing pathology
  8. Associated joint pathology, dislocation or subluxation

AO (Arbeitsgemeinschaft fur Osteosynthesefragen) classification system

  1. First number defines bone injured
  2. Second number: Segment of bone (proximal metaphysis, diaphysis, distal metaphysis)
  3. Letter/number: Nature of injury

Salter-Harris classification

  • Used to classify growth plate (physis) injuries
  • can lead to abnormal growth, growth arrest (complete or partial)
    • complete growth arrest - length abnormalities
    • partial growth arrest - angular deformities
  • Classification types
    1. Simple fracture line only involving physis - seldom affects growth
    2. fracture line THROUGH physis, exiting THROUGH METAPHYSIS - seldom affects growth
    3. fracture line THROUGH physis exiting THROUGH EPIPHYSIS (intra-articular) - can affect joint surface, seldom affects growth
    4. fracture line ACROSS EPIPHYSIS and ACROSS METAPHYSIS - can cause focal fusion of physis, leading to abnormal growth
    5. Crush injury through physis - growth disturbance common
    6. injury to perichondial surface by direct trauma - rare, high chance of abnormal growth

Trauma and fractures

See - Classifying fractures - Mangled Extremity - Fat embolism syndrome - Describing management of fractures

Approaching a Long bone fracture

  1. Radiographical evaluation (XR)
    • Must also evaluate joint ABOVE and BELOW a long bone fracture
    • In cases of traumas, ensure secondary surveys are completed
  2. History
    • high energy fractures (energy) is the most important consideration → increased likelihood of soft tissue injury
    • Mechanism of injury
  3. Examination
    • Assess the functional components
  4. Remain vigilant post-op
    • Compartment syndrome
    • Crush injury risk of [[Rhabdomyolysis]] and Acute Kidney Injury

Management

  1. Antibiotics ASAP (see Orthopedic antibiotics)
  2. Surgical management
    • Open fractures are surgical emergency - OT ASAP for irrigation and debridement with excision of devitalised skin and bone
    • Important to be done ASAP to restore length, alignment and rotation
      • protects surrounding soft tissue, prevents further tissue damage
      • reduces dead space → reduces rates of infection
      • increases recovery speed
    • In cases where there is vascular disruption with hard signs (E.g. no distal pulses)
      1. Temporary vascular shunting should be done first
      2. Orthopedic stabilisation
      3. Definitive vascular repair last

Complications

  • Infection
  • Iatrogenic nerve or vessel injury
  • non-union (failure to heal)
  • malunion (healing with improper position or alignment)
  • instrumentation failure
